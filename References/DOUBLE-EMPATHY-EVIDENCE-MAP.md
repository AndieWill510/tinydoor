## Purpose

This file maps claims about the double empathy problem and autistic/non-autistic social misunderstanding. These claims matter because the book is challenging a deficit-only account of autistic social life: it can say that communication breakdown is often mutual and contextual, but should not overstate one Scientific American explainer as if it were primary empirical proof.

## Claim A: The double empathy problem reframes autistic social difficulty as mutual mismatch, not one-way autistic deficit

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Autistic/non-autistic social misunderstanding is not simply caused by autistic people lacking empathy; it often reflects a bidirectional mismatch in experience, communication style, expectation, and interpretation.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 63: Scientific American — “Autism, Human Connection and the ‘Double Empathy’ Problem”

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE as public-facing explanation / MISSING primary theory source

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 63 supports that the double empathy problem is a recognized public-facing autism concept and that reputable science journalism has explained it as a challenge to one-way empathy-deficit models. It can support reader-facing exposition of the concept, especially if clearly labeled as a Scientific American article rather than primary research.

**What these sources do not prove:**
Ref 63 does not itself prove the theory empirically. It is not a study, has no sample size, and should not be presented as direct experimental evidence. It does not prove that all autistic/non-autistic misunderstandings are symmetrical, that autistic people never have social-cognitive impairments, or that support needs are only socially constructed. It also does not replace the primary Damian Milton source that coined the theory.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “The double empathy problem reframes many autistic/non-autistic social breakdowns as a mutual mismatch rather than a one-way autistic failure.” Attribute the concept and keep the tone calibrated: “reframes,” “argues,” “helps explain,” not “proves all social difficulty is society’s fault.”

**Action required:**
Add Damian Milton’s 2012 primary article, “On the ontological status of autism: the ‘double empathy problem,’” *Disability & Society*, before using this as a core theory claim. Add empirical studies testing autistic-autistic, non-autistic/non-autistic, and mixed-neurotype interaction quality if the book makes research-backed claims beyond the concept.

## Claim B: Autistic people can communicate, empathize, and build rapport more effectively in some autistic-autistic contexts than mixed autistic/non-autistic contexts

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Autistic communication may work better among autistic people than non-autistic observers expect, suggesting mismatch rather than universal social incapacity.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 63: Scientific American explainer

**Status:** MISSING adequate primary evidence in current list

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 63 may point toward this claim and may summarize the relevant research, but it cannot carry the empirical claim by itself. It can be used as an accessible doorway for readers, not as the proof store.

**What these sources do not prove:**
It does not prove interaction-quality findings, sample sizes, study design, effect sizes, or replication status. It does not prove that autistic-autistic interaction is always easy or uniformly successful. It does not erase communication support needs for nonspeaking autistic people, autistic people with intellectual disability, or autistic people under stress, sensory overload, trauma, or burnout.

**Book treatment:**
Use a careful version only if primary studies are added: “Some studies suggest autistic people may experience smoother rapport and communication with other autistic people than in mixed autistic/non-autistic settings.” Until then, treat as an idea introduced by the double empathy framework, not as a proven empirical claim.

**Action required:**
Add primary studies such as Crompton et al. on autistic peer-to-peer information transfer / interaction quality, with sample size and design captured after reading. Add a review of double empathy evidence if available.

## Claim C: Double empathy challenges mind-blindness and empathy-deficit stereotypes without denying disability or support needs

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
The double empathy problem undermines the stereotype that autistic people lack empathy while preserving the reality that autistic people can have real communication, sensory, and support needs.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 63: Scientific American explainer

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE for framing / MISSING for full scholarly grounding

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 63 supports a public-facing explanation that the double empathy problem is commonly used to challenge simplistic “autistic people lack empathy” narratives. It is suitable for a gentle conceptual correction in prose.

**What these sources do not prove:**
It does not prove that empathy-deficit research is wholly false, that theory-of-mind measures have no validity in any context, or that all autistic people experience empathy in the same way. It also does not prove that social barriers are the only source of autistic disability.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “This does not mean autistic people have no communication disabilities or support needs. It means the old story — autistic people fail socially because they lack empathy — is too small and too often cruel.”

**Action required:**
Add scholarly sources on Milton, mind-blindness critiques, and empirical double-empathy studies. Verify whether any sources already in `Chapters/REFERENCES.md` cover alexithymia or empathy heterogeneity and should cross-map here.
