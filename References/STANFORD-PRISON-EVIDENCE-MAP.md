## Purpose

This file maps claims about the Stanford Prison Experiment, obedience, authority, institutional harm, and the danger of using famous psychology studies as too-clean moral proof. These claims matter because the book may use SPE/Milgram-style material to illuminate coercive systems, but must not treat contested demonstrations as settled science or confuse SPE with obedience experiments.

## Claim A: The Stanford Prison Experiment is historically important but methodologically and ethically contested

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
The Stanford Prison Experiment shows that ordinary people placed into coercive institutional roles may become cruel or compliant under situational pressure.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 132: Stanford Prison Experiment official exhibit / archive
- Ref 134: The Atlantic URL, “Rethinking One of Psychology’s Most Infamous Experiments” — appears to discuss Milgram/obedience rather than SPE; verify before using

**Status:** CONTESTED for SPE as scientific proof / SUPPORTABLE for cultural-historical illustration

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 132 proves that Stanford hosts an official archive/exhibit presenting the experiment, its timeline, materials, and institutional narrative. It can support historical description of the experiment as an event: a 1971 mock-prison study associated with Philip Zimbardo, guard/prisoner roles, early termination, and later cultural influence. It is also useful as a primary institutional source for how Stanford/Zimbardo’s materials frame the study.

**What these sources do not prove:**
Ref 132 does not independently validate the experiment’s conclusions. It is not a neutral replication, not a modern ethics review, and not enough to prove that assigned roles alone caused cruelty. The SPE had a small, homogeneous sample; no robust control group; researcher involvement in the simulated institution; demand-characteristic concerns; ethical problems; and later archival criticism. It should not be presented as clean evidence that “power automatically corrupts ordinary people.”

**Book treatment:**
Use SPE as a cautionary cultural artifact, not as settled proof: “The Stanford Prison Experiment became famous because it seemed to show how quickly systems can train people into domination and submission. But the study itself is deeply contested; the safer lesson is not that cruelty is automatic, but that coercive systems, authority cues, role scripts, and researcher/leader expectations can shape behavior in dangerous ways.”

**Action required:**
Add independent scholarly critique before using SPE as evidence: Thibault Le Texier (2019), “Debunking the Stanford Prison Experiment,” *American Psychologist*, and/or Haslam, Reicher, and Van Bavel (2019), “Rethinking the Nature of Cruelty.”

## Claim B: Institutional settings and authority structures can produce harm, but SPE alone cannot carry that claim

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
When people are placed inside systems of authority, control, surveillance, and role hierarchy, harm can emerge even when individuals see themselves as ordinary or good.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 132: Stanford official exhibit / archive
- Ref 134: Atlantic article — likely Milgram/obedience-related, content verification required

**Status:** SUPPORTABLE as analogy / MISSING for robust social-psychology proof

**What these sources prove:**
Ref 132 can illustrate that coercive role environments became a major public symbol for institutional harm. If ref 134 is confirmed as a Milgram/obedience article, it may support the adjacent point that authority pressure can shape harmful compliance in laboratory settings. Together, the sources are useful for framing “systems matter.”

**What these sources do not prove:**
They do not prove a generalized law that ordinary people inevitably become abusers. They do not prove that coercive systems remove responsibility from actors. They do not prove that all institutions are equivalent to prisons, experiments, cults, ABA clinics, schools, families, or workplaces. They do not prove specific claims about autism intervention harm without autism-specific sources.

**Book treatment:**
Write: “Famous social-psychology experiments are useful as warnings, not verdicts. They tell us to watch what authority, role scripts, and institutional incentives ask people to become.” Avoid using SPE as a bludgeon that overexplains all harm.

**Action required:**
Add current, less-contested sources on institutional authority, coercion, conformity, moral disengagement, and social identity leadership if these mechanisms matter to the chapter.

## Claim C: Ref 134 appears to be an obedience/Milgram source, not a Stanford Prison Experiment source

**Claim as currently implied in book:**
Ref 134 is paired with ref 132 as Stanford Prison Experiment evidence.

**Sources in current list:**

- Ref 134: `theatlantic.com/health/archive/2015/01/rethinking-one-of-psychologys-most-infamous-experiments/384913/`

**Status:** T2 / possible WRONG SOURCE for SPE; potentially SUPPORTABLE for obedience framing

**What these sources prove:**
The URL title and external indexing suggest ref 134 is Cari Romm’s Atlantic article on rethinking Milgram’s obedience experiment, not a direct Stanford Prison Experiment critique. If confirmed, it may support an “obedience to authority” claim, especially as journalism about reanalysis or reinterpretation of a famous experiment.

**What these sources do not prove:**
If it is a Milgram article, it does not prove claims about SPE methodology, SPE archival criticism, SPE sample, or SPE guard/prisoner behavior. It also does not carry primary Milgram evidence. It would remain journalism and should be labeled accordingly.

**Book treatment:**
Split the claims cleanly: SPE for prison-role/institutional mythology and contested role-power narrative; Milgram for obedience/authority pressure. Do not merge them as if they are the same experiment.

**Action required:**
Read ref 134 directly and classify it. If it is Milgram, rename the claim group or move ref 134 to an obedience evidence map. Add an actual SPE critique source for this file.
