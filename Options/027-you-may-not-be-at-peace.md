# Option 3.4 — You May Not Be at Peace

Author: Kevin "Andie" Williams  
Status: Regulation sprint draft  
Project: Tiny Door / You Have Another Option  
Source: Chapters/030.md  
Safety: regulation / anxiety / alexithymia / overwhelm frame; no method detail; evidence review required

---

## Doorway

You can look still and not be at peace.

You can be sitting down and still be running inside.

You can be quiet and still be carrying a room full of alarms.

That distinction matters.

A lot of our distress gets missed because people look for the wrong evidence.

They look for tears.

They look for shouting.

They look for the visible collapse.

But some bodies, especially us women and girls, go quiet under pressure.

## Pressure

If you have alexithymia, interoception differences, trauma, masking habits, or years of being punished for showing distress, you may not know how overloaded you are until you are already past your limit.

That does not make the distress fake.

It means the signal may be delayed, muted, or translated into something else.

Irritability.

Exhaustion.

A sudden need to leave.

A body can be doing everything it knows how to do and still be blamed for the moment the load becomes visible.

## Reframe

Rest is not the same as regulation.

Stillness is not the same as safety.

Quiet is not the same as calm.

If your nervous system has been carrying more than the room can see, you may need a way to check for pressure before pressure becomes crisis.

Not perfectly.

Not clinically.

Just gently enough to ask:

> Am I actually at peace, or am I only still?

That question lets the body become part of the conversation before the body has to scream.

## Move

Ask one body question:

> Am I still, or am I at peace?

One check.

Maybe shoulders.

Maybe jaw.

Just listen for one signal.

## Mercy

You are not failing because you did not notice the overload sooner.

Many of us were trained not to notice.

Now your body gets to be heard earlier.
