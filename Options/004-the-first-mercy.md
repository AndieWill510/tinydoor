# Option 1.4 — The First Mercy

## A Way In

Now we take the saxophone story out of the band room and hand it to you, the reader, as a concept.

The first mercy is not what happened to me.

It is what the story makes available to you.

The first mercy is this:

> Maybe the failure was not you.

That sentence is not the whole book.

It is the door.

## Pressure

We are often asked to begin from self-correction.

Fix your face.

Fix your eye contact.

Fix the way your body tells the truth.

There may be skills to learn.

There may be supports that help.

But if every path begins with the assumption that we are the defect, the path itself is already unsafe.

A person cannot heal while being asked to disappear.

## Reframe

Mercy does not mean pretending nothing is hard.

Mercy does not mean nobody ever gets hurt.

Mercy means we stop starting with blame.

We start with reality.

We live inside environments that create avoidable suffering: noise without escape, lights without mercy, schedules without recovery.

When those environments hurt us, the pain is real.

When we react to that pain, the reaction is information.

The first mercy is believing that information long enough to investigate it.

## Move

The next time you notice yourself thinking:

> I am the problem.

Pause, if you can.

Ask:

> What is the pressure here?

Name one pressure without judging it.

Maybe sound.

Maybe hunger.

Maybe being watched.

You do not have to solve the whole pressure.

Just name it.

## Mercy

You are allowed to ask what hurts before deciding you are wrong for hurting.
