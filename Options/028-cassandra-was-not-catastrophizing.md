# Option 3.5
# Cassandra Was Not Catastrophizing, Either

## A Way In

Sometimes people call it catastrophizing when you are actually remembering. You say the room will be too loud, the family dinner will turn into questions you cannot safely answer, or the doctor will not believe you, and someone says:

> You are assuming the worst.

Maybe. Or maybe your body is recognizing a pattern.

## Pressure

We are often treated as unreliable witnesses to our own danger: too sensitive, too anxious, too dramatic. But many of us have lived the same bad outcome enough times to know its weather. We know what happens when there is no exit, when the lights buzz and nobody cares, when asking for clarity becomes the problem.

Then, when our bodies prepare for the next version of the same thing, people call that irrational. Sometimes it is not irrational. Sometimes it is a nervous system with receipts.

## Reframe

Cassandra was cursed to know the future and not be believed. We are not prophets. We do not always know what will happen. Fear can overgeneralize. Trauma can turn one danger into every danger.

But the correction should not be automatic disbelief. The better question is:

> What has this body learned to expect?

That question does not make fear the boss. It makes fear evidence: something to check, hold gently, and update.

## Move

When dread arrives before a situation, ask what pattern your body might be recognizing. One pattern is enough: noise, no exit, disbelief, confusion, social demand, being trapped.

If you have capacity, ask one more question:

> What tiny door would make this safer?

If not, naming the pattern is the whole move.

## Mercy

You are not foolish because your body remembers. You are allowed to treat dread as information without letting it become destiny.
