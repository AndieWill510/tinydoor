# Option 3.15
# Stigma Teaches the Body to Hide

## A Way In

Stigma often arrives before language: a look, a laugh, a door closed too fast, a parent saying, "That's not appropriate," without explaining what would be appropriate, where, when, why, or with whom.

The lesson lands anyway:

> Some parts of you make people uncomfortable.

Then the body starts hiding before the person even knows what was hidden.

## Pressure

We are often punished for not already knowing the invisible rules: when to move, what can be asked, which needs are allowed in public. Stigma turns missing instruction into moral failure.

Instead of teaching privacy, consent, timing, context, boundaries, and safety, people teach shame. Shame is a terrible teacher. It does not teach a person how to be safe. It teaches a person how to disappear.

## Reframe

Privacy is not shame. Boundaries are not shame. Consent is not shame. Those are useful tools.

Shame is different. Shame says:

> The need itself makes you bad.

Tiny Door refuses that lesson. The body can learn safer channels without learning self-hatred. A person can learn context without being told their body is the problem.

## Move

When shame appears, ask one question:

> Is this shame, or is this a useful boundary?

A useful boundary gives information. Shame only makes you smaller. Start with one situation, not your whole life.

## Mercy

You were allowed to need guidance. You were not supposed to be made ashamed for needing it.
