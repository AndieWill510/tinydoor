## Abstract

This document defines the initial working frame for *You Have Another Option*.

The project is a book, not software.

It is a book about Autistic survival, suicide prevention, lifespan, joy, regulation, community, and the conditions that allow Autistic people to live longer and freer lives.

Its hidden engine is suicide prevention.

Its reader-facing promise is not despair management. Its promise is another option.

> This is a book about building an Autistic life your nervous system can survive long enough to love.

---

## 1. What This Project Is

*You Have Another Option* is a memoir-informed, research-informed, Autistic-affirming survival and longevity book.

It braids:

- personal narrative;
- autism history;
- harm reduction;
- social-model disability analysis;
- critique of coercive normalization;
- sensory and nervous-system regulation;
- Autistic joy;
- special interests;
- sexuality and embodiment;
- community-based prevention;
- practical case studies for Autistic flourishing.

The book argues that Autistic suicide prevention cannot only be crisis prevention.

It must also be life construction.

---

## 2. Core Thesis

Autistic people are not broken instruments.

Many Autistic people have been handed broken environments, broken explanations, broken standards, and broken tools for social connection — then blamed for the sound.

The book’s central image is the saxophone prelude: a child tries harder and harder to make music through a cracked mouthpiece and is socially judged for notes the instrument could never make.

The revelation — “You were never going to make those notes” — becomes a governing metaphor for Autism, masking, shame, diagnosis, grief, validation, repair, and hope.

---

## 3. Reader Promise

The reader should feel less alone early.

The reader should not be asked to absorb the full moral, historical, and statistical weight of Autistic mortality before being given a body-level experience of recognition.

The likely opening principle is:

> Story before theory.

The saxophone should open the door.

The mortality frame should enter after the reader has been seen.

---

## 4. Working Book Architecture

### Part I — You Were Never Going to Make Those Notes

Story, diagnosis, mortality, and the false blame placed on Autistic people.

This part names the crisis without making the reader carry it alone.

### Part II — The Darkest Valley

ABA, shame, masking, stigma, dysregulation, burnout, and preventable Autistic death.

This part identifies the parasite: not Autism, but coercive normalization, sensory invalidation, social punishment, and internalized ableism.

### Part III — Autistic Harm Reduction

Stimming, sensory care, sleep, food, movement, boundaries, self-defense, support teams, and survival margin.

This part turns the book from diagnosis of harm into practical survival architecture.

### Part IV — Autistic Joy as Longevity

Special interests, sexuality, community, affirming spaces, body safety, meaning, and regulated pleasure.

This part argues that joy is not optional decoration. Joy is part of survival.

### Part V — Another Option

Case studies, scripts, planning tools, and a practical model for Autistic flourishing.

This part demonstrates the path in ordinary life: gatherings, direct interests, adaptive interests, innovative interests, and concrete environmental design.

## 5. Safety Frame

This project discusses suicide prevention, premature death, trauma, ABA, dysregulation, abuse, and Autistic burnout.

The working rule is:

> Safety before rhetoric.

The book should avoid method detail, contagion risk, glamorization, fatalism, or language that accidentally implies that distressed readers are failing to apply “simple” solutions.

Where the manuscript currently uses phrases such as “simple fixes,” the safer editorial direction is:

> small, concrete changes that reduce load and increase survival margin.

The book should preserve urgency without creating shame.

---

## 6. Harm-Reduction Vocabulary

The manuscript currently explores the phrase “man-made death” / “MD” as a way to shift attention from individual blame to social and community preventability.

That instinct is load-bearing.

However, the phrase should be challenged before promotion into stable language.

Risks:

- it may confuse readers;
- it may read as euphemism;
- it may create friction with clinicians, suicide-prevention reviewers, and researchers;
- it may obscure rather than clarify the moral claim.

Possible alternatives for challenge review:

- socially mediated death;
- community-preventable death;
- preventable Autistic death;
- socially produced mortality;
- preventable mortality under hostile conditions.

No replacement is promoted by this RFC.

---

## 7. Joy and Regulation

The stimming material is load-bearing.

The book’s pressure-cooker metaphor should remain central:

> Stimming is not misbehavior. It is regulation, relief, expression, and often a pathway to Autistic joy.

The project should hold two truths together:

1. Autistic bodies need safe regulation.
2. Not every stim is safe in every environment.

The practical ethic is:

> Build a life where your body has safe ways to be true.

---

## 8. Positive Mental Attitude Reframe

The manuscript currently uses Positive Mental Attitude / PMA and Special Forces survival framing.

The useful material is not generic positivity.

The useful material is:

- team;
- training;
- withdrawal;
- rest;
- hydration;
- shelter;
- threat assessment;
- purpose;
- preserving options under stress.

Recommended reframe:

> Protect access to options under stress.

This better fits the title: *You Have Another Option*.

---

## 9. Relationship to CDP

This is not CDP software.

But it is related to CDP at the level of culture, memory, repair, and survival architecture.

CDP is governance architecture.

*You Have Another Option* is human survival architecture.

They share a concern with:

- false legitimacy;
- process used as harm;
- erased dissent;
- coerced compliance;
- dark priesthoods;
- premature closure;
- systems that call people broken when the environment is hostile.

The book does not need to become CDP.

The book may use a CDP-like collaboration discipline: memory, challenge, adjudication, repair, and no false certainty.

## 11. Initial C Challenge Packet

C should be asked to review for:

1. Historical accuracy — especially autism-history dates, claims, and attributions.
2. Safety framing — suicide prevention without contagion, glamorization, fatalism, or method detail.
3. Scope discipline — avoiding the attempt to solve all autism politics in one book.
4. Reader pathway — making sure a distressed Autistic reader feels less alone by page 5, not page 80.
5. Scientific humility — identifying claims that need citations, softer language, or clearer uncertainty.
6. Practicality — ensuring the book gives concrete survival options, not only critique.

## 12. Editorial Rules

Working rules:

- Story before theory.
- Safety before rhetoric.
- Precision before urgency.
- Joy is not decoration.
- Environment is not destiny.
- Autism is not the parasite.
- Shame is not evidence.
- Regulation is not misbehavior.
- No false certainty.
- No reader-blaming.
- No clinical overclaiming.
- No collapsing memoir, research, and universal claim into one undifferentiated voice.

## 13. Known Editorial Issues

Open issues identified in the initial project review:

1. The saxophone prelude may need to become the true opening.
2. The current table of contents appears to have section numbering drift.
3. The phrase “simple fixes” should likely be softened.
4. The “man-made death / MD” term needs challenge review.
5. PMA should be reframed away from toxic positivity.
6. ABA critique should preserve moral clarity while staying rigorous.
7. Sex-positive Autism material should be reviewed for scope, safety, and audience fit.
8. Mortality and lifespan claims require careful citation and methodological precision.
