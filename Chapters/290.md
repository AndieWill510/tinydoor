So, we talked earlier about the Medical/Pathological Model of Autism; with its kaleidoscope of “disease” causes from this toxin to that toxin, to causes in upbringing, to genetic or epigenetic spectrum of etiologies. We also know about the Social Model of Autism; that this is not a disorder/disease, but a disability caused by societal dysfunction and maladaptation in people with a different neurotype.

There is another theory about the neurodivergent mind called “monotropism;” which is an elegant model that reframes Autism into a model related to flow and focus – the mind/body reacts the way it does because it is being moved out of flow (its natural state) into a disruptive state of distraction from flow and is seeking regulation. The core of this theory is that once in flow, because of the significant cognitive resources (concentration) required, it is quite challenging to break out of flow and this leads to many social and regulation challenges for the Autistic person.

There is yet a different theory about the Autistic mind called the Intense World Theory and is equally elegant and reframes Autism into a condition of sensory hyperstimulation; the mind/body reacts the way it does because it is hyperstimulated (or hypostimulated? or mixed?) and it is seeking regulation and safety.

Related to regulation, or foundational to that concept, is a theory of biology called homeostasis, which states all biological systems seek internal balance in equilibrium with their environment (e.g., we sweat in hot climates to cool off and maintain a constant internal body temperature). The opposite of homeostasis, in humans and very common to Autistic and many other neurodivergent people, is dysregulation.

We have the foundational theory of biology which is evolution. Often referred to as survival of the fittest; this theory speaks to biological systems and their fitness (not strength, not intelligence) to the environment being the major determining factor in the propagation not only of the species but of your individual DNA. You, or moreover your DNA, have evolved to procreate and then help ensure your offspring’s DNA lives long enough to do the same.

Homeostasis is an assist to evolution as it helps living things better “fit” within their environment, especially as that environment changes. We sweat, for example, when it’s hot to maintain a constant internal body temperature. Whereas dysregulation does the opposite, we cannot “fit” into our environment, and that isolates us from the world inviting predation (“bullying”) which drives spiraling dysregulation. There is growing evidence that specific nerve systems in the body, starting with the vagus nerve, deeply impact emotional states. Leveraging these early learnings, Somatic Therapy (https://directory.traumahealing.org) can be a great relief to Autistic (and other) people. The Polyvagal Nerve Theory (PVT) speaks to this and highlights many compelling indicators of how this might be true. Although the PVT theory is nascent, and other nerve systems may be involved (and others indicated in PVT may prove not to be), as more data is collected, we will get a more complete picture driving better outcomes than are even possible today with Somatic Therapy. PVT has lit the way to relief for many Autistic people, and even if we don’t fully understand why, it takes nothing away from the transformative power of Somatic Therapy.

So, if we apply the Five Whys from Sakichi Toyota to all of this, what do we get?

1. Humans create a limited and highly stable version of the world in our minds, called models, abstractions or paradigms. Why?

2. Stable models drive homeostasis, which in turn enables focus on lifesaving actions. Why?

3. Without the ability to focus on lifesaving actions we would be unable to survive dangerous situations. Why?

4. The nature of danger is such that it often requires intense focus and quick reactions. Why?

5. Distractions that break focus have evolutionary consequences. Why?

6. Humans that cannot maintain homeostasis, and thereby focus, have a reduced likelihood of successful procreation and safety of offspring post-birth.

All humans, as far as we know, create models of reality in our minds. Neurotypical brains prune synapses in their brains that support their model of reality. There is a major advantage to this synaptic pruning in that NT brains no longer contend with (as much) sensory input that distracts from their model.

Autistic brains don’t prune synapses like neurotypical brains do. One of the consequences is the world remains highly dysregulating (Intense World) for Autistic people. One of the adaptations sailors have used for hundreds (thousands?) of years to avoid seasickness is to focus on a fixed point, the horizon or an island, for example. Seasickness is a sensory overload but also a dysregulation (or loss of homeostasis), specific to the vestibular, visual and proprioceptive senses. By shutting down all other visual inputs (just the fixed point), it allows the body to come back into homeostasis and out of dysregulation. In Autistic language, the hyperfocus on the horizon is a “stim” that helps regulate the system.

It is plausible that monotropism evolved out of that same sensory overload/fixed-horizon approach – to shut down some sensory input, end dysregulation, and bring the body back into homeostasis. When Autistic people are in monotropic flow, or hyperfocus, several senses such as interoception, touch and hearing can drop down to imperceptible levels. It gives the body time to reset into homeostasis, after being dysregulated through sensory imbalance. In time, through the positive reinforcement/habituation, Autistic people will behave monotropically with or without dysregulation.

In the time before modern medicine as a child, when I started experiencing migraine, my dad said the old family solution was to put me in a dark quiet room, have me lay down and be very still and close the door. I was hot, dizzy, couldn’t hear or see well, and with a horrible headache. The solution was to lower temperature, shut down vision (cold wet compress over eyes, dark room), shut down sound (quiet room) and lay very still – shutting down vestibular inputs. Time with sensory deprivation allowed the system to come back into homeostasis. They put me into sensory deprivation and in 20-40 minutes I was right as rain.

When I started experiencing low-blood sugar, after a failure of interoception to “notice” I was hungry, I would get short-tempered (“hangry”), headache, dizzy and difficult to see as well. Again, my family made sure everyone was fed on a regular schedule: at wake-up, noon and then around 5-6 at night. This time, it was an imbalance of (hypo)interoception causing low blood sugar. By keeping the blood sugar balanced, it overcame the consequences of a suppressed sense of interoception and homeostasis was (and is) maintained.

Finally, meditation and mindfulness are other altered mental states that many find useful in bringing regulation to an otherwise dysregulated mind/body. But what is meditation and mindfulness, but a reduction of sensory inputs (sitting still, closing your eyes, focusing on your breath)? Why does it work? Is there something to reducing the sensory inputs, and breathing regularly and deeply, that is key to balance/regulation (i.e., homeostasis)? Religious leaders for over a Millenia have thought so. Why is it that an increasing number of mental health workers are looking to mindfulness and meditative practice as a complement to traditional Psychotherapy and Psychiatry (“more skills, less pills”)? And what if Autistic people have what is in essence a built-in meditative state? What would meditation look like without the spiritual practice of meditation? Is monotropism itself a meditative practice that is an instinct, some genetic or epigenetic advantage, built into our neurotype? What if the whirling Dervishes of antiquity were stimming and thereby reaching homeostasis?

Our ability to enter hyper-focused flow, i.e., monotropism, brings Autistic people homeostasis and safety. However, too much of a good thing is not a good thing, just like with vitamin supplements. Autistic people can be in flow too long and with suppressed interoception (and other senses), this can represent its own dangers:

- Dehydration

- Low Blood Sugar

- Urination missed “signals”/mishaps

- Something burning (on the stove, etc)

- Alarm going off (fire, etc).

But clearly, monotropism confers an evolutionary adaptation of some sort, or it wouldn’t be there. From lessons in nature, circumnavigation, and my own family, the monotropic adaptation is there to help us regulate sensory-driven dysregulation. Again – with the apple analogy – nature packed the antidote (fiber) along with the poison (sugar). If our civilization is in fact, for Autistic people, poisonous (Intense World), then nature also gave us the antidote (monotropism). Evolution has built for Autistic people an adaptation that makes homeostasis, and therefore propagation of the species, more likely. It is critical that we treat it as such.
