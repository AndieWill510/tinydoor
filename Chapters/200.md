## Chapter 20: How Dysregulation Works

The isolation that bullies and shunners cause in Autistic people is not
the only option – Autistic people can, and increasingly do, connect with
other Autistic people. There’s another option to isolation that leads in
part to Autistic Burnout, which we’ll talk about later in the book.

Autistics are doing what they’ve been coached to do by nature and
nurture in the face of predation: Protective Mimicry. The intensity of
the impact of the protective mimicry is proportional to the intensity of
the impact of the trauma of being bullied. For Autistics it’s simply
more, so the symptoms and harm caused by Protective Mimicry is more.

Protective Mimicry can be thought of as a system, working within the
human body that has become dysregulated in the Autistic person. Let’s
talk about why, and some of the other highly correlated problems that
happen with Autistic people. When I was 11, we moved to Texas from
California. Texas, at least the part where I grew up, had a very high
pollen count from coastal, Southern California where I lived before.
After several months of severe allergies, my mother took me to a doctor
who ran a series of allergy tests. After the results came back, the
doctor in his inimitable bedside manner, turned to my mother and said,
“you don’t love him very much, moving him here, do you?” He explained
that North Texas wasn’t a great place to live for someone like me. The
words proved to be prophetic on a couple of levels, and transcended the
number of allergies I had relative to the pollen count. Not too long
thereafter I was diagnosed with IBS-D, which was excruciatingly painful
with diarrhea. I was given a colonoscopy without anesthetic at 11 years
old, and after the results came back, I was told that this is an old
evolutionary response to parasites that no longer serve us. The cause
was never determined, and I continue to suffer from IBS-D, but far, far
less with changes in diet (I eat a lot fewer simple carbohydrates). But
the important part is that this was an evolutionary response to
parasites, to which I had none. So, an evolutionary response to
parasites. Without parasites. Hmm. Allergies are not an autoimmune
disease common in Autistic people like rheumatoid arthritis, lupus,
celiac disease, and psoriasis, but it’s an immune dysfunction or an
immune *dysregulation*. IBS is a dysregulation of the gastrointestinal
(GI) tract stemming from, historically and evolutionary, a parasitic
infestation. So, just as a thought experiment, what happens when you
line up common symptoms of autoimmune diseases<sup>178,179</sup> and
parasitic infections?

Note: I am not saying that autoimmune diseases are caused by parasitic
infections or vice versa. That would be a Correlation-Causation Fallacy:
stating that because 1 happens when 2 happens, 2 must be caused by 1.
I’m saying that a lot of Autistic people have these symptoms, and we
don’t know why yet. It’s not a 100% match in two ways: first not all
autoimmune disease symptoms line up with all parasitic disease symptoms
and second not all Autistic people have 100% of all these symptoms. But
these issues are *common enough* in the Autistic community that I think
we need to think through why. We also can see that we have an
evolutionarily-driven Protective Mimicry Cycle that is fueled by Stigma
Cycle of autistiphobia, reinforced by a Grief Cycle of autistiphobia –
which trace back to SOURCE. These are major drivers to Autistic
meltdown, shutdown and burnout. But it also drives us to internalize
corresponding behaviors that accelerate and intensify meltdown, shutdown
and burnout: masking, compensation and assimilation.

This happens and we now understand this as the Autistic (Protective)
Mimicry Cycle. The deeper we dive into assimilation and burn(out), the
less protective the mimicry is and the more destructive and deadly it
becomes. Like a chain reaction, beyond a certain point the Protective
Mimicry becomes a positive feedback loop and the symptoms get worse and
worse until mental and physical illnesses occur. Autistic Protective
Mimicry becomes Autistic Destructive Mimicry. But this is not the whole
story.

What if there were another mechanism in play? What if, in addition to
this Protective Mimicry evolutionary response (driven by the Stigma and
Grief of others), there was a parallel track? Protective Mimicry is a
predatory response that animals evolved to keep us safe and more
importantly, allow us to propagate the species and our own DNA.
Predators, however, are not the only threats in nature. We know
parasites also can harm or kill us. Autoimmune diseases are a
dysregulation of the immune system: literally, the body doesn’t
recognize itself as itself and starts attacking cells, tissues and
organs. What if, and this is a big hypothetical leap, parasitic
infections triggered a similar type of immune response as autoimmunity?
What if the immune system recognized a threat, but didn’t know what it
was? We can call it autoimmune, but for our purposes I’ll call it
Parasitic Immune Dysregulation. In the face of a parasitic infection,
the immune system is doing its job, as I was taught by a
gastroenterologist years ago, IBS-D evolved as a countermeasure to
parasites like giardia<sup>182</sup>. But there are many symptoms that
autoimmune disease and parasitic infection share. What if the immune
system were merely confused? It sees something, recognizes something, as
foreign entity and goes to kill it. The immune system though, like a
hunter at midnight on the plains of Africa who senses a lion – but with
no ability to see it – starts firing shots wildly into the dark.
Autoimmune disease and parasitic infection symptoms have a *lot* in
common. What if the immune system believes something is wrong, there is
an invisible foreign invader, and acts out of self-protection?

I believe this is the other shoe to drop, when it comes to what happens
to Autistic people when faced with the Stigma and Grief Cycles. We issue
an all-out assault through Autistic Protective Mimicry, but the immune
system spins up a separate defense. It’s as if the immune system knows
that a predator-defense (Protective Mimicry) strategy is doomed to fail
*from the very beginning.*

But what if the predator (bully) isn’t a predator and isn’t a parasite
but by the body *are seen as both at the same time*. I’m not calling
bullies parasites, nor predators per se. But our Autistic immune system
has rendered a judgment, and whether we want to *think* of neurotypical
abusers as parasites or not, our immune system is mounting a defense as
if they are. We’ll call this the Autistic Predator-Parasite Defense
Model: both Protective Mimicry (Predator) and Immune Response (Parasite)
working in concert to protect us. I believe this is a big part of the
reason why Autism is so difficult to diagnose and treat, especially with
the various symptoms – all somewhat personalized to the Autistic person.
The protective mimicry system is confused/overloaded, and a system
designed to keep us healthy starts to make us sick (in one set of ways).
The immune system is confused/overloaded, and a system designed to keep
us healthy starts to make us sick (in another set of ways). And, as with
Protective Mimicry, the Immune Response will go and go until a “cytokine
storm” immune response happens as a last-ditch effort. These systems
(mimicry and immune) will do anything and everything to protect us, up
to and including *driving us to the point of death* to save us. But know
this: Autistic people find themselves in environments where this
combined Autistic Predator-Parasite Defense Model is often the *only
option presented.* I believe, though, there is another and simpler way
through to health.

Before we get to that, I think the parasitic infection symptoms are
worth taking a closer look<sup>180</sup>:

- Digestive issues, such as stomach cramps, pain, nausea, vomiting,
  diarrhea, constipation, gas, bloating, and weight loss.

- Weakness, fatigue, or anemia due to iron deficiency.

- Itching in the rectal or vaginal area, especially at night.

- Skin problems or rashes, such as eczema, hives, and blisters.

- Fever, muscle aches, or nervous diseases.

- Swollen lymph nodes or glands.

- Trouble concentrating or depression.

- Appetite gain or loss.

- Dehydration.

- Dry skin or sensitivity to cold.

I don’t focus on these symptoms to say you as an Autistic person have
these problems, but some of these symptoms are common with Autistic
people. It’s possible you (and by you, I mean your immune responses) are
simply defending your body from your environment in the best way you
know how. Your immune system wasn’t designed to physically move you out
of a pond full of leeches, and neither was your Protective Mimicry
System. It’s only with a full appreciation for what things in our
environment are doing to us can we begin to make the changes we need to
return to full health.
