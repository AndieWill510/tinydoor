The first thing to know is that for people with Autism, between 50% and 70% also have ADHD. From the meta-analysis study in 2022 from Hours, Recasens, and Baleyte:

“In the first case [ADHD], we are primarily describing distracted children who pay little attention in academic settings, lose their belongings, and have difficulty sustaining mental effort. In the second case, we are talking about children who seldom associate with others, have a hard time interacting and communicating, and may display unique motor or verbal behaviors, including stereotypies, echolalia, and idiosyncratic language. Whereas, children with ADHD tend to be relatively boisterous and talkative, and eager rather than apprehensive of interactions with peers or adults, autistic children may be distinguished by their repetitive and less coordinated motor function, difficulty communicating, emotions in sync with their sensory reality more than with their social setting, and uniform behaviors that keep the unpredictable at bay.”

From the same study:

“autistic symptoms were significantly more severe, especially in the area of social interaction (as evaluated by the social responsiveness scale and autism diagnostic interview), in patients with dual ASD-ADHD diagnoses than in those with ASD alone”

And also:

“autistic symptoms were more prevalent in children with ADHD…the intensity of hyperactive and impulsive symptoms directly impacts the severity of ASD symptoms”

Note that:

“ASD-ADHD comorbidity does not correspond to a mere combination or addition of both disorders: it is neurofunctionally distinct and merits further study for more accurate characterization”

And finally:

“ASD and ADHD are different manifestations of one overarching disorder. They made the hypothesis there is a single continuum in which emotion regulation is a crucial common factor.”

Of course, if you’re reading this book, you either have been diagnosed with Autism or self-diagnosed. So, you are both Autistic and ADHD. You cannot separate one from the other. Everyone who has both ADHD and Autism (“AuDHD”) are slightly different from one another. It’s important, though, to know what the key symptoms in ADHD are in adults, according to the Cleveland Clinic:

- Fidgeting

- Getting out of your seat often

- Chronic restlessness

- Difficulty engaging in quiet activities

- You’re always on-the-go

- Talking excessively

- Blurting out or finishing sentences

- Difficulty waiting in lines

- Interrupting or intruding on others

According to the latest research, ADHD behavioral regulation and executive function impairment significantly impact our ability to perform as Autists: said another way, it’s more difficult for us to give our areas of concentration our undivided attention as other Autistic people do.

What can drive these characteristics to be more prominent?

- Stress

- Lack of sleep

- Malnutrition and poor diet

- Overstimulation

- Changes in environmental factors like sound sensitivity, temperature and smells

- Lack of genuine interest

- Mood disorders such as Major Depressive Disorder and Bipolar Disorder

In other words, the guidance earlier in the book for how to live a healthy Autistic life are doubly true for those of us with AuDHD.

People with ADHD may benefit from having a daily routine that helps them manage their symptoms, boost their productivity, and improve their well-being. A routine can provide structure, consistency, and predictability for the ADHD brain, which may struggle with organization, time management, and focus. However, creating and sticking to a routine can be challenging for people with ADHD, who may find it boring, restrictive, or overwhelming. Therefore, it is important to find a routine that works for you, based on your personal preferences, needs, and goals.

There is no one-size-fits-all routine for people with ADHD, but there are some general tips and examples that may help you create your own. Here are some of them:

- Plan your schedule ahead of time. Before you start your day, write down what you have to do and prioritize the most important or difficult tasks. You can use a planner, a calendar, a to-do list, or an app to help you organize your tasks and track your progress. Try to estimate how much time each task will take and block off more time than you think you need. You can also set reminders or alarms to help you stay on track and avoid distractions.

- Break down large or complex projects into smaller and simpler tasks. This can help you overcome procrastination, reduce stress, and increase your motivation. For example, instead of writing a whole report in one sitting, you can divide it into steps such as researching, outlining, drafting, revising, and proofreading. You can also reward yourself after completing each step to keep yourself motivated.

- Keep your routine simple and flexible. Don't try to make your routine too complicated or detailed, as this may make it harder to follow and maintain. Focus on the essential tasks and activities that you need or want to do and leave some room for adjustments or changes. You can also experiment with different routines and see what works best for you. For example, you may find that you work better in the morning or in the evening, or that you need more or less breaks throughout the day.

- Use timers to help you manage your time and attention. Timers can help you stay focused, avoid distractions, and get things done. You can use a timer to set a specific amount of time for each task or activity, and then switch to something else when the time is up. This can help you avoid spending too much or too little time on something and create a sense of urgency and challenge. You can also use a timer to set regular breaks to rest, relax, or do something fun. This can help you recharge your energy, reduce stress, and prevent burnout.

- Be patient and consistent with yourself. Creating and following a routine can be hard, especially for people with ADHD. You may encounter some challenges, setbacks, or frustrations along the way. Don't be too hard on yourself or give up easily. Remember that it takes time and practice to form new habits and behaviors. Try to be positive and supportive of yourself, and celebrate your achievements, no matter how small. You can also ask for help or feedback from others, such as your family, friends, or therapist, if you need it.

- Separate work and relaxation time. It can be tempting to mix work and leisure, especially if you work from home or have a flexible schedule. However, this can make it harder to focus, finish your tasks, and enjoy your free time. Try to create clear boundaries between work and relaxation time and stick to them as much as possible. For example, you can designate a specific area in your home for work and avoid using it for anything else. You can also set a regular time to start and end your workday and avoid checking your emails or messages after hours. This can help you create a balance between your professional and personal life and improve your productivity and well-being.

- Set aside some time for self-care. Self-care is important for everyone, but especially for people with ADHD, who may experience more stress, anxiety, or mood swings than others. Self-care can help you cope with your emotions, improve your health, and enhance your quality of life. Self-care can include anything that makes you feel good, such as exercising, meditating, reading, listening to music, or spending time with your loved ones. Try to incorporate some self-care activities into your daily routine and make them a priority. You can also use self-care as a reward for completing your tasks or goals, or as a way to relax after a long day.

- Get enough sleep. Sleep is essential for your physical and mental health, as it helps your body and brain to rest, recover, and function properly. Lack of sleep can worsen your ADHD symptoms, such as inattention, impulsivity, hyperactivity, and irritability. It can also affect your memory, learning, mood, and performance. Therefore, it is important to get enough sleep every night, preferably around 7 to 9 hours. To improve your sleep quality, you can follow some tips, such as:

- Stick to a regular sleep schedule and go to bed and wake up at the same time every day.

- Avoid caffeine, alcohol, nicotine, or heavy meals before bedtime, as they can interfere with your sleep.

- Create a comfortable and quiet sleeping environment, and avoid any sources of light, noise, or temperature that may disturb your sleep.

- Avoid using your phone, computer, TV, or other devices before bedtime, as they can emit blue light that can keep you awake and disrupt your circadian rhythm.

- Do something relaxing before bed, such as reading, listening to soothing music, or meditating, to help you unwind and fall asleep faster.

These are some tips and examples of routines that may help people with ADHD. However, you don't have to follow them exactly or copy them from others. You can create your own routine that suits your needs, preferences, and goals. You can also modify or change your routine as you go along, depending on your situation and feedback. The most important thing is to practice with routines and find one that works best for you and stick with it. You’ll find as I did that routine above many other medicines and techniques helps put a foundation under what can often feel like a rollercoaster existence.

There is also a silent epidemic amongst Autistic people which is sexual abuse. It is very important that Autistic people, starting in pre-adolescence, get the facts regarding sexual health and relationship boundaries. A great resource for learning the boundaries of healthy relationships is the series of podcasts here: https://researchautism.org/self-advocates/sex-ed-for-self-advocates/sex-ed-for-self-advocates-podcast/ And, it is very important that Autistic people have a healthy relationship with sex, which starts with regular self-pleasure. Orgasms release oxytocin, which in addition to counteracting cortisol (a stress hormone), can also counteract some of the effects of toxic stress, also helps us connect with our bodies in a way that promotes somatic mindfulness208. People who have both Autism and ADHD may carry extra stress, so that natural release is perhaps even more important than for other Autistic people. This was discussed during the Stimming Chapter, and because the intersection of sex and Autism carries such a strong taboo it’s critical that Autistic people connect early with their bodies in a healthy, pleasurable way.

It is important to note that our character, our biology, drives us to be the best version of ourselves, in all times. AuDHD folks are “spiky” — some days we do a great deal, some days not much, lots in between. We show kindness to ourselves by honoring what we bring to the day, every day. It’s all good.
