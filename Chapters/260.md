Remember, as I write this, I’m a 52-year-old American. I know, for some of you that’s old. But when I saw a news report about a bunch of Autistic kids dying in the UK I got curious. Really curious. And I’m a Data Engineer by training and experience, which is a fancy title for telling stories using data, and my current area of focus is large scale public health. So, I started digging through the data. And it led me, inexorably, to longevity studies. Because when a bunch of one group of people die, regardless of cause, life expectancy takes a hit. It happened during the height of the COVID epidemic, so that idea was fresh in my mind.

Because I’m 52, and the average Autistic person dies in my next year, chronologically, it was like a white-hot poker right between my eyes. I didn’t write this book as a self-help book, nor as a way to get you to change so society will accept you.

Not to be dramatic, but I want to save your life. And the lives of your Autistic loved ones. And, not to put too fine a point on it, surviving is necessary but not sufficient. We have earned the right, and deserve, so much more than survival.

But, back to the story. So, I got this weird experience with this data I’m reading. We know Autism, and neurodivergence in general, runs in families – I’m not getting into genetics or epigenetics or any of that in this book. But we’ve known that scientifically, and intuitively, for decades. But I had this weird experience while reading that data. My grandfather, who was undoubtedly Autistic, died at 92. His dad, I’m going to go out on a limb and say also Autistic, died at 97. My dad, spectacularly Autistic, recently died at 79 of a very NT disease (he was a lifelong smoker, died tragically of lung cancer). I’ve been told my whole life that longevity runs in my genes. So, where is the disparity? Am I and were they rich? We know that for Americans there is a positive correlation between income and longevity47. No, I’m telling you, these men did not die rich and I’m still “working for a living.” Some other factor? And why did grandpa die in his 90s (and his dad in his late 90s), while mine just hit the NT average?

Was it some kind of Autistic "Blue Zone" effect? Maybe. But let’s talk about science. Let’s talk about what we can prove with data and science, not only anecdotes (which can work in a pinch). Richard Feynman, great teacher of quantum physics, wrote a book about Physics called Six Easy Pieces48. Sounds about right – but physics, this is not. So, this will be a few really, really easy pieces. Here’s a quick outline of the steps in the IEMS Harm Reduction Protocol:

- Autistic First Aid/Prevention

- Disrupt Those Who Treat You Badly, and the way they make you think/feel

- Prevention/Harm Reduction Best Practices

- Building Serious Support Networks/Community

- Medicine/Nutrition

- Activity/Volunteering

- Rest/Recreation

- Environmental Adaptations (*not* social skills)

- Pay It Forward

### Autistic First Aid

Many of us know or have heard of Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs60. It’s taught a lot in college level intro Psychology and it’s neat because it’s a pretty picture and easy to test students on (source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maslow%27s_hierarchy_of_needs ).

While famously not always 100% accurate, the basic idea is: you can’t be like Gandhi and start taking on the problems of the world if your house is on fire. Higher order thinking doesn’t happen unless and until basic survival needs are met first. And it’s a hierarchy, or pyramid. Each level builds upon the lower levels. Fail to satisfy the needs below? Prepare for reduced capabilities. Ever been in Autistic Burnout? My Actually Autistic Coach Matthew Lawrence says, you know you’re in burnout when you have a loss of skills. Just as Maslow predicted. As young people say nowadays, "mess around and find out" (they don’t say "mess," but this is a mostly family-friendly book). See my previous chapter on special forces survival training and the primacy of a Positive Mental Attitude. You can’t complete your mission (in other words, be "combat effective") if you’re in a panic attack.

Pre-Planning: Above All Else

The time to learn to administer first aid is before you need first aid. I’ll give you a brief example. Before you do cardiopulmonary resuscitation, or CPR, you train. We took it in high school. It was led by the Red Cross. We practiced again and again on life sized human models doing the steps, so when faced with a real person in cardiac arrest or choking we’d know what to do.

So, what’s first in our Autistic first aid plan?

- Plan to learn to swim and make sure your Autistic loved one learns to swim.

- For both Autistic people and loved ones: plan to take CPR and get certified.

Check with the Red Cross for both swimming lessons and CPR. Both are, in relative terms, cheap. Your local YMCA/YWCA may offer free or reduced cost lessons if you qualify. Ask. Don’t let pride or money be an obstacle. This is a life and death matter. Let me break this all down, in terms of the PDSA/Deming Cycle:

I: Injury

Injury is another leading cause of death amongst Autistic people, you need a plan to make sure it doesn’t hurt you.

Plan

Autistic children ages 0-14 are 160 times more likely to drown than non-Autistic children61. That means if a child 0-14 years old dies from drowning, it’s a sure bet they either didn’t know how to swim or were Autistic or likely both. I almost drowned at 7, my brother saved my life. My daughter walked tiptoe into a pool to where it became so deep for her, only her nose and mouth were showing. I saw her in time and saved her, thankfully. And immediately she and her brother went into swimming lessons. Based upon a bit of research, comparing the number of kids who drown to the number of kids who have Autism, roughly 1% of all Autistic kids die each year from drowning. That’s a lot. If that rate were applied to everyone, 33,000 kids in the US a year would be drowning to death. We might call that right there an epidemic and wrap up the book. But that’s only part of our epidemic story.

If you think your loved one might be Autistic, or if anyone in your family might be Autistic — i.e., “have any Engineers in the family?” The right thing to do is plan and get them in swimming lessons. Not test them for Autism. That can wait for now. Swimming lessons can’t wait. CPR is next in line.

Autistic people often have sensory-motor integration issues. So, they can choke on food or suffocate easier than NT people. All kids aged 0-5 account for 3 out of 4 choking fatalities62. We know that choking is one of the injury types most likely to impact Autistic people, especially children. Take preventive measures to reduce the risk of choking, such as supervising children around food and objects, cutting food into small pieces, avoiding hard or round foods that can block the airway, and learning how to perform first aid for choking. And again: plan to learn CPR.

Do

This is where your Plan goes into action.

Swimming lessons. Not a few weeks and not a few months, very likely multiple years. Get good at it. It might take a while. It’s worth it. Keep it up. Swim regularly, to keep your skills and muscles strong. Again YMCA/YWCA often will have low-income or even free memberships if your income level is low. I did it when I was moments away from homelessness. You can too.

CPR lessons. It might save your life if someone goes with you. You might have an Autistic loved one or friend and you could save their life. Worth the expense of time and money. Ask if your income is low ask for a discount. Pride is no excuse when it comes to life and death.

If you’re watching over an Autistic child, while they are swimming or eating, I know this sounds obvious, but don’t drink alcohol or do drugs. It will slow your reaction time. At that time my daughter almost drowned? I shudder to think what would’ve happened if I had been drinking that day with my daughter in the pool. I was just lucky I hadn’t been.

If you’re on a boat or other watercraft, make sure you’re wearing a well-fitting life jacket. If you’re with an Autistic loved one, make sure they wear theirs and that it fits well. It may cause sensory issues such as being scratchy, so please do what you can to make it as comfortable for them as you can. They must wear it either in the boat or in the water, especially in oceans, lakes or moving water (rivers, streams).

Study

You’ve Planned. You’ve Done. Now, take a step back and reflect.

Learn CPR and first aid, especially in water safety. This is not a one-and-done. You will need to stay current on your CPR skills. CPR skills can save someone's life in the event of a choking or suffocation incident. Many organizations offer CPR training courses for parents and caregivers that can teach you how to perform CPR on children and adults. You should also learn how to do the Heimlich maneuver or abdominal thrusts to dislodge an object from the airway. The Red Cross can help you learn what you need to learn.

Act

You’ve Planned, Done and Studied. Now, cement those skills further and see what you can do to help others and, by doing so, help yourself. If you’re comfortable doing it, once you’ve learned, become certified to teach others CPR. You can help assist in CPR classes if you’re not able to lead it. Or you can get the word out by letting others know of upcoming classes through social media. You’ll be glad you did. You might just save a life.

E: Epilepsy

Plan

Epilepsy is another leading cause of death amongst Autistic people, you need a plan to make sure it doesn’t hurt you.

Start your Plan for Epilepsy first aid here: https://www.epilepsy.com/recognition.

Also, if you or your loved one has had one seizure, they need a Neurologist to evaluate them for treatment immediately, which may include starting them on life-saving anti-seizure medication. Not every strategy works for everyone, and some people may need more than one strategy to control their seizures. It is important to consult with a qualified neurologist (one trained in the latest epilepsy treatments) before starting or changing any treatment for epilepsy. The right neurologist can help find the best treatment plan for each person with epilepsy based on their individual needs and preferences. To find a qualified one near you, or one that may be able to work remotely through telemedicine, visit https://www.epilepsy.com/find-doctor. And, again: plan to learn CPR.

Do

This is where your Plan goes into action.

If you have seizures or *think* you have epilepsy, get in to see a Neurologist. I recently did, and I was glad I did. If you are having seizures, you need this way more than you need a psychotherapist. It’s a must-have. They’re rarely cheap, sometimes not even covered by insurance. But epilepsy can kill you or your loved one. Nothing is more expensive than that.

Study

You’ve Planned. You’ve Done. Now, take a step back and reflect.

Learn more about epilepsy, and the specific type of epilepsy that you have. Avoid the triggers, know the warning signs. Wear a life alert bracelet. Epilepsy.com is your friend. Make use of its resources. The more you know about your condition, the healthier you will be, the safer you will be, and the more likely you will be to avoid as many seizures as you can.

Act

You’ve Planned, Done, Studied. Now, you put all those learnings to use to go further, get better, and get healthier.

Ask your Neurologist if there are support groups online or in person to help you (and the ones who love you) to learn, grow and thrive. You may even be the one in time helping others learn, grow and thrive through support groups online or in person. You’re not alone, according to the World Health Organization there are 50 million people worldwide with Epilepsy. Ask your Neurologist about getting a therapy/support dog specifically for people with Epilepsy – they're trained to help you to avoid injury and worse.

MD: (definition above and in the Glossary)

Plan

MD is another leading cause of death amongst Autistic people, you need a plan to make sure it doesn’t hurt you.

If MD is in your mind or if you have a plan to carry it out, call 988 in the United States immediately. Please know, it’s not enough to "reach out for help," any more than it would be to call 911 during a heart attack. Ambulance, rideshare, or on foot – you get yourself checked into a hospital if that is what is recommended. This is one of those times where you need to do exactly what they say, please don’t “get creative” or procrastinate here. 988 are the experts, and from firsthand experience, they’re kind. Please act like your life depends on it, because it does.

If you or someone you know needs help, call or text 988 or chat 988lifeline.org for mental health support. Additionally, you can find local mental health and crisis resources at dontcallthepolice.com. Outside of the U.S., please visit the International Association for Suicide Prevention.

Do

This is where your Plan goes into action.

For MD, this may mean being hospitalized or attending outpatient therapy. It’s OK to be angry about it, it’s perfectly natural. The desire to fight treatment is a sign of life inside you. But do the treatment plan anyway. By doing so, you’re not being weak or overly dramatic. Your life is in jeopardy, and it’s time to follow your treatment per your doctor’s instructions, exactly.

Study

For MD, go to therapy sessions. Might be individual, might be group. The people “in group” might be your age, they might not. It doesn’t matter, the goal is learning, sharing, and understanding that you are not stuck and that you have options. Stick with therapy until you’re safe. It might take a while, this is your “study.”

Remember that Autistic people often feel like they are trapped or defeated in some way63. It may mean that you feel that you have no alternatives. You do have options, you just don’t know them yet or don’t believe them yet. That’s OK. You may have to give up one or more things (or ideas) that you believe to be necessary in your life — to save your life. Stay flexible as you can and study ways to bring mindfulness and awareness to times where you are feeling trapped or defeated. Just because you have lost doesn’t mean you are lost. Just because you feel trapped doesn’t mean that there isn’t a way out – it just means you need help finding that way. Be open-minded and willing to get rid of the ideas, habits, jobs, towns, (or people!) in your life if it saves your life. No relationship, job, idea, drug, possession, situation or lifestyle — nothing — is worth losing your life in this way.

Act

You’ve Planned, Done, Studied. Now, you put all those learnings to use to go further, get better, and get healthier.

For MD, there are two things you need which likely are the last thing you want. One is therapy, medication or both. The second is exercise. Neither will sound appetizing. Remember that no cardiac patient likes the treatment, no person recovering from a severe bone break likes Physical Therapy. It sucks. But this is life and death. Take deep breaths and go – stick with your appointments and you will, over time, start to recover. Ask about getting a therapy/support dog specifically for people with Autism – they're trained to help you to avoid injury and worse. If you’ve faced MD as I have, I can tell you from first-hand experience a little more nature in your life (like a dog) can be healing.

S: Sensory Integration Crises

Plan

So, what else do we need to plan for? In a phrase, sensory overload. NTs talk a lot about information overload, but that’s something that for the most part they can walk into and out of. Sensory overload is a lot like information overload, but for Autistic people it can be all around you at home, at work or walking down the street. It can seem or actually be random, you rarely get a choice of when it hits you, and it can make you so sick you get a raft of debilitating physical symptoms like migraine or epilepsy and can go into a full-blown mental health crisis. So, we need to plan.

There’s an old saying in financial services/investing: have an exit plan. Goes for stocks, goes for real estate, and especially goes for Autistic Sensory Overload. If you’re going to a party, and at this point you’re already feeling nervous, if you can plan where you’ll go, once at the party, to get some sensory relief. You might need to sit outside. You might need to find a quiet room. You might just need to leave, and if able go for a walk or roll. If you’re going with people, make sure they know what you might need. Your parents may or may not be sympathetic (social embarrassment for them is sometimes more powerful than their concern for your mental well-being), but your brother or sister might. Or your friends. And if your friends aren’t, part of your plan needs to be finding new friends. Think of it as having a designated driver: someone you trust to help you get away from a situation safely when you have need. Do not, and mean do not, sit there and "tough it out," until you’re so sick your mind and body go into meltdown. You’re not trapped, don’t allow anyone to make you feel like you are. That’s abuse, whether they (or you) know it or not.

Do

This is where your Plan goes into action.

On sensory overload. Your senses are getting overloaded, and you’re starting to not feel well. Or you think you’re maybe, possibly, not feeling well. This is hard-won advice: if you think you’re not well and getting overloaded, my dear reader, you already are. Doctors will tell you by the time you feel dehydrated it’s already too late. This is just like that. You’re “dehydrated” and you need to take steps before you get even sicker.

Autistic people are often people-pleasers in this regard. It stems from years of being goaded, bullied, shamed, beaten, medicated or otherwise cajoled into sensory-overloading situations. We’ve been trained to "go along to get along." So, you bury it: the feeling, the thought of being overwhelmed. And, when it’s too late, you try to hide the symptoms. The rush to the toilet. Or the meltdown. Or subtler signs like lashing out verbally at someone who said the "wrong" thing. You’re already "dehydrated." You have a plan. Act. Now. You wouldn’t make a friend suffer like this. Don’t do it to yourself. Don’t do it to your Autistic loved one. Check in with them on the regular. They’re not overreacting. They’re probably toning it down, to be honest.

Study

You’ve Planned. You’ve Done. Now, take a step back and reflect.

For sensory-integration mental health crises, this starts with a positive mental attitude. You can, you will get better. But you will need to do what we in the software industry call a post-mortem, an after-action report. To yourself. If you like to write, write about it. Become your own scientist. What worked, what didn’t. Where did things go off the rails? Most importantly what SMALL thing are you going to plan and do next time so you don’t get as sick. It might be don’t go back to that environment. But it might be more subtle if that’s not an option. Like, say, at work. I find a lot of times at work I "get over my skis" and talk more than I’m comfortable. It’s ok. Talk less. You can listen less, too. Sometimes listening is overwhelming for Autistic people. I told a friend recently that NT speech is like a traveling performance art installation. It’s more impressionistic than literal. They talk a lot, but don’t often say a lot. I had a neurodivergent boss describe a meeting we once had as having the right kind of "signal-to-noise ratio." Funny, and about the most Autistic thing I ever heard. You can almost always circle back for clarification. Don’t stress the details (well, try. I know it’s hard).

Act

You’ve Planned, Done, Studied. Now, you put all those learnings to use to go further, get better, and get healthier.

Ok, you had a meltdown. Or now you have a migraine. Or a seizure. Or you started seriously choking. Or nearly drowned. Switch up the order. If it’s a seizure or a migraine, call your neurologist. You know for medical emergencies to call 911.

Otherwise, you administer sensory first aid. Like you do in a fire drill: calmly, quickly head for the proverbial exit. Get somewhere safe, darker and quiet. Look up sensory rooms, or sensory kits. Stim toys. Or stim your way, really stim. Weighted blankets can help. Even going to a quiet, dark bedroom for a nap. This is first aid and it’s essential for your wellbeing. Is it too much to say that this is your survival, long-term? No, no it is not. And do this not because the headache will go away. Or your nervous system will calm down. That’s all a secondary benefit. The primary reason to Act is to gently, repeatedly demonstrate to yourself, for yourself, that you are going to be made whole. That someone loves you: YOU. And, with time and practice, others around you will get the hint and let you do you. Or you need to have a loving but stern conversation with them about how important this is to your health.

Finally…

We teach people how to treat us, every day. And if a person around you

- won’t let you rest/recover

- won’t render Autistic first aid

- make you feel bad about it

- if they really won’t learn to give you this healing time and space

it’s important that you recognize this for what it is: abuse. And I don’t mean like a verbal bully. Like they’re beating you up with a stick physical abuse. If this is happening to you, make a plan, a new step in the loop, to prune that person or people from your life. It may take time. It may come at great cost. But the cost is low relative to saving years off your life. Because to you, they are toxic. And whether you know it now or not, having them around is making you sick. You are a human being, and human beings have rights. And stomping on those rights is never ok. Even if it’s you the one doing it to yourself.

### Disrupt and Discard Those Who Treat You Badly, and get rid of the way they make you think/feel

Before we start, this section comes from my experience and the experiences of other Autistic people I’ve spoken with. For Autistic people, it’s not uncommon to have more than one person in your life who is not treating you well. It might be your parent(s), a sibling, a friend, a doctor/therapist…or even you! So, keep in mind everyone’s situation is different and this is a general approach. But I hope it’s enough to get started down a healthier path, one where you internalize less of the unkind treatment. Make less excuses for it. Make less time and give less headspace for those voices telling you something other than: you matter, you have rights, you have value and are worthy of being loved and giving love in return. Don’t take my word for it. Take a moment and read the https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights. It’s short, it’s important, and a lot of countries on Earth agree that this is the foundation of what a civilized world looks like. You deserve all this and more. You are disabled. Life needs to give you a break!

Along those lines of life giving us a break, let’s switch gears and talk briefly about positive feedback loops. They happen in nature all the time. Sometimes, they’re a good thing, like blood clots when you get a cut on your arm. Another example is fruit ripening:

- You get unripe fruit, or just-ripe fruit.

- One starts to ripen faster than the others.

- Gives off ethylene gas.

- It accelerates ripening faster and faster as other fruit releases even more ethylene gas.

- Before you know it, they’re all rotten.

- One bad apple (or banana) ruins the whole bunch.

The same goes for global warming. Or a pandemic. Or stress driven by illness. You get so stressed you get sick. Now you’re stressed and you’re sick. On and on until you’re "sick and tired of being sick and tired". Maybe you’re there right now. Let’s see how we might disrupt that process and start feeling better.

Backgrounder

Everyone’s situation is different. There’s an old saying in the community that "if you’ve met one Autistic person, you’ve met one Autistic person." There’s a lot of hard-won truth in that. An Autistic-ADHD (AuDHD) person is different from an Autistic person with schizophrenia is different from an Autistic person with epilepsy and so on. But I liken Autism to a big, beautiful Ficus tree. Lots of branching, one leaf or branch different from the other, but at the base is a shared trunk. That trunk is the focus of this book, and it also happens to be where small changes to your environment over time can have lasting healing impact.

Let’s focus again on sensory overload, but this time instead of high-speed, high-intensity situations requiring first aid, let’s talk about something a bit harder: everyday situations that you deal with that just bother you. They include:

- Meetings at work (in person or online)

- Classes (in person or online)

- Talking with someone who dysregulates you ("bothers" you)

- Shopping: supermarkets, malls and other shops

- Live sporting events

- Live music concerts

- Carnivals, festivals and county fairs

- Church, synagogue, mosque or other place of worship

- Museums

- Parks/picnics

Now, I’m not trying to trigger anyone. Some of that list may be a "nothing burger" to you, and some of it a huge deal. For most probably something in-between. But something on that list (or something I missed) bothers you. Maybe not at first, maybe not all at once, but now you’re like a dog with a bone. You can’t let it go. So, this is where the positive feedback loop kicks into supercharged mode: you talk with someone about it, so you can feel better. Your mom, your dad, a sibling or a friend. And one of them inadvertently "gaslights" you.

Case Study: Gaslighting 101

What, really, is gaslighting? In a word, control. It’s the intentional use of confusing words meant to control you. It doesn’t matter why someone would want to control you this way, because of the enormous damage it always causes. First, though it’s better to give examples for something like gaslighting. It’s so commonly experienced by Autistic people, you may not even recognize it.

Some common gaslighting phrases are:64,65,66,67,68

- You’re speaking inappropriately.

- You're overreacting.

- That never happened.

- You are making that up.

- I did that because … was trying to help you.

- You're too sensitive.

- Your mind seems off, you need help.

- You're imagining things.

- You're being dramatic or emotional.

- You don't remember things correctly.

- You're not making any sense.

- You always twist things around.

- You're the problem, not …

- No one else thinks that way.

- Everyone agrees with me.

- I never said that you're misquoting me.

Gaslighting supercharges a positive feedback loop for an Autistic mind, like hot ocean water does for a hurricane69. One, because we often don’t know we’re being gaslit. We’ve been taught, almost universally, to distrust our interpretations of events from an early age. Two, gaslighting words at face value may appear helpful and are often phrased to be in our best interest. Three, because our Autistic minds often can’t resist a mystery, we go to work as little scientists. Proving our case. Disproving our case. Sometimes going back and forth between proving and disproving. And in a tie, we often defer our judgment to the person gaslighting us.

This can set in motion a destructive process that, if unchecked, can go on for months, years or longer. Meantime, you may not be aware, but two things are happening. The cortisol in your system is slowly rising, which is a stress hormone. Usually this is ok, as levels rise and fall as a natural part of life. In this case, especially if you live with the person or work closely with the person who is gaslighting you, the hormone levels stay elevated. Your stress response grows higher than it should, and stress hormones stay elevated70. But they’re not designed to do that. Your body isn’t built for that.

“...chronic stress can make a body system to anticipate, as if such a new (stressful) environment would persist, demanding a newly defined set point for future adaptation...Examples...may be found in the primary mediators (hypercortisolemia, increased inflammatory cytokines), secondary outcomes (elevated blood pressure, overweight, insulin resistance), or tertiary outcomes (hypertension, diabetes, obesity, coronary heart disease, neurodegenerative disorders).”

Your immune system starts to be impacted. Could be more asthma, worse lupus, allergies are worse. Could be more colds, more sick days. Could be more stomach problems like IBS. Could be more migraines, or tougher periods. You’re slowly, but surely, getting sicker. Maybe that’s you right now. Or maybe you’ve been there in the past but didn’t know why.

To add to this, maybe you’ve been trained not to stim. Maybe your one natural relief valve has been shut off. Maybe the person gaslighting you is also abusing you further by ridiculing you into stopping stimming. That happened to me as a child. I’ve talked with other Autistic people, and it’s happened, in one way or another, to all of them.

Just like before, we’ll use the Deming Cycle to make ourselves safer than we otherwise would have been. The Deming Cycle remember is Plan-Do-Study-Act, and the goal is to provide continuous improvement as you apply this cycle over and over for whatever issue is bothering you.

- Plan

Long-term, there is only one plan: you have to get away and stay away from people who gaslight or abuse you. Why? Because people who gaslight or abuse you don’t have a difference of opinion you can fix, and they’re not sick so you can’t fix that either. They’re abusing you, in a way that involves making you doubt your own perception of reality, memory, or feelings. Gaslighting is bad for everyone, but since we as Autistic people may not see it at first or at all, it’s downright dangerous. To your health, both mental and physical, because it represents “toxic stress.” What is toxic stress? Simply put, it’s your body’s recognition that your wellbeing is in jeopardy over a given period of time AND you are unable to do anything about it. Not once or twice. Ongoing. In our case, often because we can’t see it happening, and can’t feel its effects, we doubt ourselves once we do get a hint or an intuition that it’s happening.

- Do

Short-term, you must fight. Here are three relatively straightforward things that anyone can do right now to fight gaslighting:

I: Injury

Plan

A few statistics. For Autistic people, but especially kids aged 0-14, one of the number one causes of death is drowning. Plan? If you have a child this age who can’t swim, or haven’t learned to swim yourself, it’s time. One study showed that Autistic kids are 160 times more likely to drown than their non-Autistic counterparts61. Make sure they or you learn it well. It’ll reduce likelihood of early death. Period.

Other major causes are choking and suffocating.

Choking and suffocation are serious and preventable hazards that can affect Autistic People of all ages. We have sensory issues, oral motor difficulties, or behavioral challenges that can increase our risk of choking or suffocating on food or non-food items. Therefore, it is important to take measures to prevent these hazards and protect the safety of Autistic People.

Some of the planning/prevention strategies are:

- Choose and prepare food carefully. Avoid small, hard, sticky, or round foods that can easily get stuck in the throat, such as nuts, seeds, popcorn, candy, grapes, hot dogs, or chunks of cheese78,79,80. Cut food into small, non-round pieces and cook or mash food to make it softer and easier to chew and swallow 78,79,80. Consider you or your child’s developmental level and oral motor skills when introducing new foods and textures82. I’m 52, and I still easily choke on food (and liquids!) if I’m not concentrating.

- Keep non-food items out of reach. Small, round, or similar objects can be choking hazards and should be kept away from young children 78,79,80. You may want to purchase a small parts tester or use a toilet paper roll to help determine which items are small enough to cause choking hazards 79. Examples of objects that can obstruct the airway include coins, marbles, balloons, jewelry, buttons, pen caps, or batteries 78,79,80. If your child tends to mouth or swallow non-food items (pica), seek professional help to address this behavior82.

- By following these prevention strategies, you can help protect your child from choking and suffocation hazards. Remember, choking and suffocation are fast, silent, and preventable. Be safe and vigilant!

Do

- Wear a life jacket. Life jackets can reduce the risk of drowning for children with ASD, especially in natural water settings such as lakes, rivers, or oceans. Life jackets should fit properly and be always worn when boating or engaging in water activities. Do not rely on air-filled or foam toys as safety devices, as they can deflate or slip off.

- Build fences that fully enclose pools and other water sources. A four-sided fence that is at least four feet high and has self-closing and self-latching gates can prevent children from accessing the pool without adult supervision. Remove any toys or objects that might attract children to the pool when they are not in use.

- Supervise closely and constantly. Designate a responsible adult to always watch children in or near water, and avoid any distractions such as reading, using the phone, or drinking alcohol. If a child is missing, check the water first, as drowning can happen quickly and quietly. Be aware of any risks or hazards when visiting unfamiliar locations or homes with water access.

- Supervise your child while eating, or if it’s you, eat carefully. Have your child sit down while eating and avoid distractions or disruptions that may cause them to eat too fast or not chew properly83,84,85. Watch your child closely and monitor their signs of choking or distress. Do not leave your child alone with food or drinks. If you or your child has a history of choking or swallowing problems, consult with a speech-language pathologist or occupational therapist for guidance on feeding strategies87.

Study

- Learn basic swimming and water safety skills. However, swimming lessons do not replace the need for close and constant supervision when children are in or near water.

- Learn CPR. CPR skills can save someone's life in the event of a drowning incident, as every minute counts when it comes to brain damage and survival. Many organizations offer CPR training courses, both online and in-person, that can teach you how to perform CPR on children and adults.

- Know the risks of natural waters. Natural waters can have hidden dangers such as strong currents, waves, rocks, vegetation, and limited visibility. Check the weather forecast before going to natural water sites and avoid them during storms or floods. Follow the rules and signs posted at beaches or lakes and stay within designated swimming areas.

Act

- Avoid alcohol while around water. Alcohol can impair judgment, balance, and coordination, which can increase the risk of drowning for both adults and children. Do not drink alcohol before or during swimming, boating, or other water activities. Do not drink alcohol while supervising children in or near water.

- Prevent suffocation and strangulation. Place infants on their backs on a firm mattress with a fitted sheet and no pillows, blankets, bumpers, or toys in the crib to reduce the risk of sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS) and accidental suffocation91,92,94. Do not let your child play with plastic bags or other items that can cover their face or nose. Keep cords, strings, ribbons, or necklaces away from your child's neck. Do not use window blinds or curtains that have looped cords 91,92,94.

Other steps that may help prevent seizures are:

- Avoiding seizure triggers if you know what they are. Some common triggers are stress, lack of sleep, illness, fever, dehydration, flashing lights, or certain foods or drugs. You can read more information about seizure triggers on the Epilepsy Foundation website.

- Seeking support from your healthcare provider, family, friends, or other people with epilepsy. Living with epilepsy can be challenging and stressful, but you are not alone. There are many resources and programs that can help you cope with epilepsy and improve your quality of life.

Remember, epilepsy, with proper medical care, is a manageable condition that does not have to limit your potential.

Think of rest as the thing you give yourself – recharging your social batteries, recovering spoons, or spending time in your version of a sensory room. Recent research on the design of interior spaces speaks to the needs of Autistic people to have adequate solitary spaces, so rest is all about recovering time away from the masses116. But rest is only part of the equation. Recreation is a community way of playing – this could be a great D&D group, tabletop games of all sorts, or team sports. It could be with one person playing chess – that counts! I was never one for a lot of team sports, and maybe some of you are the same, but for those who love a pickup basketball game or weekend soccer game with friends are doing powerful work to connect with others and building time to re-create yourself. Last, but not least, if you can get out and get into nature no matter whether it’s the forest, beach, grasslands, wetlands, lakes, rivers or streams – recreation in nature is a great way to reconnect with yourself and your community. Whatever you can do to the best of your ability is good and it’s good for you and those around you117.

Before we talk about ways community can help, let’s talk about the opposite of community— a side effect of years of abuse and stress in neurotypical society — isolation, or loneliness. According to Dan Buettner, the author and host of the Netflix series “Live to 100: Secrets of the Blue Zones:” social isolation, or loneliness, can take 15 years off your life expectancy compared to someone who is socially connected in the US. Autistic people can suffer a lot of social rejection leading to isolation. Connecting with Autistic people is a cure for that.

Buettner says that having a strong sense of community and belonging is one of the key factors that contribute to the longevity of people who live in the Blue Zones, the regions where people have the highest rates of living to 100 years or more³⁴. He also suggests finding ways to stay socially engaged and connected with others, especially during the pandemic, such as joining online groups, calling friends and family, or volunteering for a cause.

ASAN has a great introductory video on Autism that will help orient you to other Autistic people and might just help you as you create your own community of Autistic people. Give it a try. It’s short and very informative. https://youtu.be/XnuGPJ7UdpU?si=I_gRIxAT6TCGSaZ3

In it, they expound upon my earlier discussion on keeping a positive mental attitude in the acronym HOPEFUL:

How does this lead to community and battle isolation? By itself, it doesn’t. It helps orient you to a world that needs each of us to be as self-reliant as possible. And that’s where community can really kick into high gear and make a difference. There’s an old joke in banking, if you have money, you can get money (a loan). Or in Human Resources, if you have a job, you can get a job. Is it fair? Hell, no! But is it often true? Yeah. If you have a friend, you can get a friend: but here’s the secret, Autistic people online often are also isolated and know it’s not healthy. So, your odds through Twitter and Bluesky and Mastodon are higher than you think, for finding online friends. And, if you work towards being self-reliant, you can start to not only rely on others in community, but they can rely on you. "Each one, teach one" goes the old African American proverb. Don’t come to a party empty-handed, bring a gift. And the gift is you and your life experiences and your Positive Mental Attitude.

Notice I didn’t say you needed a job, or money, or the wisdom of Gandhi to join a community. But look back on the HOPEFUL acronym. Look back on a positive mental attitude, and the survival lessons of military special forces. You don’t need money. You don’t need a job. But you do need to cultivate your own resourcefulness. You do need to know that you are a survivor and are willing to help yourself and others do the same. Their strength will become your strength and your strength will become theirs. Some days you’ll be in meltdown, or burnout, or shutdown. And you’ll need them. And then they’ll have bad days, and they’ll need you.

Moai

Small groups, or collectives of Neurodivergent people, taking care of each other not only emotionally but also pooling resources (financially, time and otherwise) draw from the "Blue Zone" of Okinawa in small groups called moai. It can start online. But it needs to be much more than short-form social media and memes. If possible, where possible, look for IRL (in real life) opportunities. With it, tightly banded squads find meaning, purpose and well-being in longer life. Start with one person. Know that it can be much more. In a world driven by goals that often make us sick, instead set that as a goal.

Ikigai

Drawn again from Okinawa, ikigai is a constant sense of purpose. Could be a garden. Water aerobics. Playing D&D, a tabletop game like chess or backgammon. It’s that which gets you up each day, and gets you through each week, without fail. It’s also knowing your values. It makes those day-to-day decisions very easy, because you know your core. It’s also a gentle but powerful reminder that you count. We need you. It drives vitality, it staves off depression and isolation.

### You’re Better Without (Some of) Them

I do not believe in social skills training for Autistic people. That’s not to say that we all can’t get better at communicating. It’s something all of us can and I believe should make attempts at improving—and that’s not only speaking, writing, signing, using AAC or other means. It’s the continued striving at making our thoughts, feelings and needs known, and making room in our hearts for those strivings from others that is valuable. But the time of Autistic people bearing the sole burden for communicating "correctly" — is over. It’s time for our environment, our society and our countries to begin adapting to our communication style. It is our environment, and the NT people who fill it, that need to do the right thing and meet us in the middle where communication and healthy relationships thrive.

You must slowly, inevitably change your environment and make it one that supports you. The first step is not adding anything. You’re fine, in fact you’re great. There is nothing, and I do mean nothing you need to add right away, except for perhaps adding a few Actually Autistic people online or in person. More than add, to become healthy and thrive you need to get the voices out of your life and out of your head that align to Ableism. Dilute them, disrupt them, and dismiss them. Prune them out of your life and your head. This fight is not in the streets but in your own mind over the voices that:

- Gaslight

- Tell you you’re inappropriate

- Fight for or seek the "cure"

- Prioritize parents over disabled Autistic children

- Ignore the needs of Autistic Adults

These messages penetrate so deeply, and are so unbelievably destructive, that they threaten your health and your very life. But they can be minimized through dilution: the dosage makes the poison. Loved ones, if this is you, change quickly and make amends to the Autistic people in your life. This is your chance to help them, and in turn you, to reach a healthier life.

You may have to move out to supportive housing to get away from Ableist voices. It may not be easy; in fact, it may be very hard. Do what you can, as soon as you safely can. Meantime, learn techniques and history of nonviolent non-cooperation. You cannot safely continue to internalize Ableism, specifically gaslighting. You are disabled and you have a right to live free from fear, judgment, and abuse for how nature made you. Avoid online groups and people who espouse "cures" of any kind or prioritize or equate the needs of parents over Autistic children. If this were colonial India in the time of Gandhi, ABA and Autism Speaks are the British Army and clergy, respectively. You do not have to accept or absorb their toxicity. If you will, I invite you to mute it or block it, but don’t engage/reply directly to it online, these harmful voices should not be amplified (and your time is more valuable than spending it with “trolls”). Make time and space for Actually Autistic and other neurodivergent voices. Dilute poisonous air with fresh air you were meant to breathe. It’s your right, but with great humility I offer that it may also be your responsibility (to yourself).

Networks like ASAN (https://autisticadvocacy.org/) are there to help you in many ways. You know, when a group’s motto is "nothing about us without us" — you’ve found a new home. They have resources that can and will help you as they did me. Find Actually Autistic Spaces through Autistic Coaches like Matthew @AutisticCoach_ on X/Twitter. Also, you don’t need a medical diagnosis ("dx"). They are quite expensive and add nearly nothing for many of us. In fact, depending on your circumstances, dx may act to deny your rights, certainly in the workplace. Other places you may be denied a visa or the ability to adopt children. Self dx is just as valid anywhere you want or need to be, at least as an adult. Be very cautious as you share your dx, self-dx or otherwise, especially at work. An old wise person once said, "don’t cast your pearls before swine." That wisdom serves me more and more every day. Not everyone has earned the trust to know that you’re Autistic. And you are not responsible for educating anyone on your disability.

### The Autistic Underground Railroad

"I think slavery is the next thing to hell. If a person would send another into bondage, he would, it appears to me, be bad enough to send him into hell if he could." — Harriet Tubman

"I grew up like a neglected weed - ignorant of liberty, having no experience of it." — Harriet Tubman

If I had an entire book, I wouldn’t be able to do justice to the contribution to humanity made by Harriet Tubman and the Underground Railroad. She, and a network of courageous volunteers from the early to late 19th century, put their lives on the line every day for decades, quite literally saving the lives of over 100,000 enslaved people through various paths to the Northern United States and Canada. For a more in-depth read, I suggest Jean Munez’s book Harriet Tubman: The Life and the Life Stories. She said many important things in her time and did many more.

First, she found her way to freedom originally with the help of two of her brothers, Ben and Henry. After a return briefly to slavery, she made a second attempt alone using the Underground Railroad. She recognized that while in slavery she was a shell of a living being, "a neglected weed," oppressed as she was by the horrors of an enslaved body and mind. Second, she recognized that one person binding into servitude of another was a type of hell on Earth. It was not enough to use the Underground Railroad to free her. She was going to "pay it forward" and dedicate part of her life to becoming a very, very effective "Conductor" of the Underground Railroad. She became a candle burning brightly in the darkness of slavery. After the Emancipation Proclamation she kept serving the cause of justice, first in the Civil War as a Union Spy, then later running a home for impoverished/homeless people. Pay it forward, indeed.

In our modern time, with ABA and a dozen ABA-like examples in society and in our families, like we see in other modern abusive examples like abusive Catholic “priests,” physical freedom does not equal liberty. ABA and their exemplars build the bondage into the deepest levels of the mind using self-control (a type of suppression), and with it:

- low self-esteem/self-hatred

- high likelihood of re-victimization through habituated subservience

- learned helplessness

- PTSD

- social anxiety

- pathways to premature illness and death

This subjugation was and is designed to do two things:

- Travel with the Autistic person once ABA sessions (or other abuse) were complete, often subconsciously

- Train us to do this to others, often Autistic people

All under cover of law and with heavy economic and emotional incentives to the ABA practitioners. "To save the savage you must kill the savage," went the thinking of colonialism. Echoes of this thinking permeate not only early ABA; it permeates modern ABA, no matter how they rebrand it to run away from an increasingly toxic reputation. And because ABA is the “gold standard” for how to treat Autistic people, most of society “treats” Autistic people in the same way.

So, we need a 21st Century Underground Railroad, and the great news is that it exists!

Organizations like ASAN and people like Matthew Lawrence are the modern-day Stations and Conductors of the Underground Railroad for Autistic people. The deeper you look, the deeper the parallels go. ABA is not therapy; any more than slavery was the "natural order" as proponents called it at the time. ABA, and the society that follows its lead, is functionally bondage. Conditioned into the body explicitly to shackle the mind in a way Harriet Tubman would very much recognize. This binding is hidden in plain sight, made permanent through repetition so that this type of bondage continues throughout life. The outcomes are sick people and early death. But there is a way out. These brave Autistic survivors of ABA, other Autistic and Autistic allies built a way out. Not using back trails and safe houses, but social media.

In the time of Jim Crow South, the Civil Rights Movement was growing, decade upon decade. And many have now said that Autistic people must fight, protest, and mount a Civil Rights level struggle. In time, I believe that will happen. But this will be after ABA and all its permutations are made illegal and the knowledge of its damage made widespread, as slavery once was. Autistic people have not yet had our Emancipation Proclamation, our Stonewall Riots. Autistic people are still in the era of micro-institutionalized, “slavery-to-go" world of legalized, Autistic ABA franchises and the Law of Intended Consequences of a society that mistreats and dis-ables Autistic people. I offer to you that Autistic people live in the equivalent time of Harriet Tubman, not Martin Luther King, Jr. So, Dear Reader, the Underground Railroad exists. Help yourself and get onboard. Then, if you are able, consider African American wisdom: "each one, teach one."

And loved ones of Autistic people? There is another bit of wisdom from the time before, during and after emancipation: "if you are not part of the solution, you are part of the problem."
