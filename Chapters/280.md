So, we hear a lot about resilience and it’s often in the context of a highly NT, highly capitalistic mindset of making more and more money or some other measure of “success.” There is nothing inherently wrong with success, of course, as no one wants the opposite. But successes have many measures, and one of them is strength and vitality. In this section we’ll build upon the previous sections: harm reduction protocol, avoiding harmful people/environments while adding more time in Autistic communities and being mindful of self-stigma. We’re going to take this a bit further and talk about anti-fragility, which is the principle of adding the right kinds of healthy Autistic stress while getting rid of distress as much as is humanly possible. We often think that Autistic people have enough stress and that we should remove or reduce all that we can. I believe that we should move to eliminate all toxic Autistic stress (bullies, gaslighting, other abusive people/environments, self-stigma) and add in positive Autistic stress (developing Autistic communities beyond simply participating in them, picking up new areas of concentration that help build employable skills)

### Distraction Management

Whether you are Autistic or, like me, Autistic with ADHD (AuDHD), distractions are everywhere. It seems every year a new app gets loaded on our phone designed to do one thing: take our attention away from things that make us healthy and onto things that make us sicker, weaker and exhausted. Back in the 1990s when I worked for Microsoft, we called this continual attentional shifting “context-switching.” Inside of a computer, high levels of context-switching is a measure of inefficiency or load on a computer from poorly written computer software. Another way to think about this is “churn,” and it’s busy work with no good outcome. We see this behavior a lot with fear of missing out (FOMO) in social media. For Autistic people, it might be better for our mental health if we did nothing at all. In Jenny Odell’s seminal work How To Do Nothing, “doing nothing” refers to actions that don’t have (NT) value, like being in nature, making art/music/sound/dance for its own sake, [I would add stimming] or learning about an abstract topic that interests you.

It doesn’t matter what you do more of as long as it doesn’t have NT/capitalistic value. Also, noteworthy is staying clear of social media. She calls this the “Attentional Economy” and sees this as worse, much worse, than “doing nothing.” For us, it would be more time spent reading/researching our Autistic areas of concentration, and less rabbit hole social media time and doom scrolling. I’m not saying social media is inherently toxic to Autistic people but it would be safe to treat it like reality TV. A few minutes a day might be ok. Hours a day is not good for you, physically or mentally.

### Pain Management

Pain is not that uncommon for Autistic people. Whether it’s fibromyalgia, Amplified Musculoskeletal Pain Syndrome (AMPS)/chronic pain, touch sensitivity, auditory or visual sensory related pain, or migraines, pain is a common issue for Autistic people. Even though it’s common, treat pain as a very serious health concern. It can cause sleep disturbances, fatigue, lower your immune system’s ability to fight infections, increase pain and most importantly create/intensify mental illness. When working with doctors I do not recommend approaching your discussions with your doctor as an Autistic issue: address the pain as an isolated issue. Most doctors are allopaths: one cause for one disease (contrast this with osteopaths). When talking to your doctor, present your pain as an isolated symptom (with one exception: if you are not sleeping properly) and leave the rest of the complexities of an Autistic diagnosis out. Most doctors do not and will not know how to properly treat Autism and this will only complicate the discussion and may lead to you being referred out – and not being treated for your pain, as you should, by that (or any other) medical doctor.

The website Pathfinders for Autism has a good guide for children (https://pathfindersforautism.org/articles/healthcare/pfa-tips-pain-mgmt-asd/). For adults, pain management can take many forms:

- Light sensitivity

- Sound sensitivity

- Touch sensitivity

- Stomach Pain/cramping (e.g., IBS)

- Neuromuscular pain (e.g., AMPS, chronic pain, fibromyalgia)

- Migraine and other headaches

- Hypermobility Spectrum Disorders including Ehlers-Danos Syndrome

Let’s take each of these in turn. As I indicated above, pain is a medical condition. I won’t go so far as calling it a disease or disorder, but it’s not something you try and treat yourself. Pain is the body’s way of signaling to you that you are not healthy, in one way or another. Yes, you can take over the counter medications to alleviate (mask) the symptoms, short term, but if this persists for more than a few weeks (I would even say a few days) you need to see a doctor. For all the different types of pain, follow these simple instructions:

- Although all of us feel some pain from time to time, living in constant or near-constant pain is not OK. You deserve to live free from regular pain. You also have the right to live without being in pain on a regular basis. Don’t let anyone tell you or convince you otherwise. You’re not being a “baby” and you don’t need to “suck it up.” Ignore people who tell you this and go get medical help. Autistic people are, in general, doubted first and believed second (or never). Don’t doubt your own pain.

- It’s important that you seek medical attention immediately because some pain can be symptoms of underlying serious conditions. Get seen. Find the facts. Then, get better – you do not have to suffer, and you have the right to live free from pain. As you already know, when you have severe pain, you can do little else, and this can severely impact your ability to live a healthy life.

- Pick a pain scale for how you’ll talk with your doctor. Many doctors will prefer you to use the 1-10 scale, but if you prefer another one you can learn about them here, and even print it out and bring it with you to the doctor’s appointment: https://www.healthline.com/health/pain-scale

- Keep a journal (or ask someone to help you keep a journal) of when the pain happens, how long the pain lasts, using the pain scale. It’s important to note if you ate specific food before the pain started, didn’t sleep well the night before the pain started, were under significant stress prior to the pain, or had an injury or accident that might make it worse. Some head injuries may be related, so let the doctor know. If you have migraines or frequent headaches (or any other seemingly unrelated pain) that make the pain worse, let the doctor know that, too.

- Don’t look the doctor in the eye when you’re talking with them unless you absolutely must, as it may help you hear better and formulate answers to questions.

- Let your doctor know that it takes you a little longer to process questions, so even if they are in a rush, you may need more time. I usually tell people (with a sense of humor :) that “I talk fast but think slow.” I never talk about Autism, and people typically make accommodations without negative consequences.

- Write down any specifics that you can learn about your pain, don’t rely on memory alone when you’re in the office as this may cause you to leave out important details.

- Don’t let anyone tell you this is “psychosomatic” as a way of saying “it’s all in your head.” There is a strong mind-body connection (for everyone) and honestly, what does it matter where the pain originates so long as you get the right treatment(s) that makes it stop?

For more information on chronic pain and Autism, see the following link: chronic pain.

### Pain Types

- Light sensitivity pain (sometimes called photophobia)

- If this is causing pain in your eyes, talk about that

- If this is causing migraines, cluster headaches, or other headaches or you *think* it is, talk about that

- If it’s worse in crowds, indoors or outdoors or while reading, talk about that

- If the doctor doesn’t bring it up, let them know you want to be referred to an Ophthalmologist or Optometrist who specializes in photophobia so you can get light-sensitive eyewear (FL-41 type) – even if you have perfect vision! I have a pair I wear all day, and they’re “readers” for “far-sighted” people with reading difficulty. It makes a huge difference in pain, fatigue, and it also lowers my stress as a side benefit. It’s cut my working stress by a third – just by reducing the blue and other light that was bothering me.

- Sound sensitivity pain (sometimes called hyperacusia)

- If this is causing pain in your ears, talk about that

- If this is causing migraines, cluster headaches, or other headaches or you *think* it is, talk about that

- If you have any other hearing problems, but only in specific circumstances (noisy room, etc), talk about that

- If the doctor doesn’t bring it up, let them know you want to be referred to an Audiologist who specializes in hyperacusia so you can get tested for it – and get custom-fitted silicone ear plugs (not Loops, or other over-the-counter non-fitted plugs) preferably with ceramic sound filters. I just got these and they’re a real help to me, especially in supermarkets or noisy environments. It makes a huge difference in hearing pain, fatigue, and it also lowers my stress as a side benefit. I went from an 8 to a 3 on a ten-point scale of stress/activation in these environments.

- Touch sensitivity (sometimes called hypersensitivity)

- Before seeking medical attention, do what you can to remove sources of tactile irritation (softer clothes/fabrics, remove tags from clothes, let people around you know how you do and don’t like to be touched).

- Consider weighted blankets and stim toys. Google these as Amazon and other retailers offer options that may help alleviate issues through compression or squeeze toys of one sort or another.

- If the pain continues, see a doctor. Let them know exactly how the pain happens and under what conditions.

- Ask for a referral to a pain specialist, if possible, and if not, then an Occupational Therapist who focuses on Sensory Integration Therapies. This can be a tough one to diagnose and treat properly so be patient as you find the right provider to help alleviate this pain.

- You may have unresolved/undiagnosed/untreated trauma, so don’t be surprised if your course of treatment goes towards EMDR or Somatic Therapy to resolve.

- Stomach Pain/cramping (e.g., IBS)

- Stomach pain can be a very complex issue to resolve, which is why it’s important to see a gastroenterologist. They are specialists who focus on digestive health.

- Don’t be surprised if you have to change your diet, when you eat, how you eat. Safefood/samefood is awesome, no question, hands down: avoiding texture issues and the pleasure of a “known thing” when we eat is a great thing, we all benefit from. But pain is worse and, so, if you’re willing to adjust your diet a bit – it might be worth the tradeoff.

- Also, don’t be surprised if part of the fix is psychotherapy. Worry/Anxiety is stored in the gut; traditional Chinese Medicine teaches. Working on my diet and my anxiety have meant less days with soul-crushing pain, cramps and numerous IBS problems in the toilet I’d rather not revisit here in print.

- Neuromuscular pain (e.g., AMPS, chronic pain, fibromyalgia)

- I’m fortunate that I live near a world-class pain center that offers integrative services beyond what you might find at the typical pill factory. For you, it might mean seeing a sports medicine doctor or an orthopedic surgeon (not for the surgery, avoid this at all costs – and get a second or third opinion especially if it’s back surgery), but because they may prescribe physical therapy. Opioids are to be avoided, if possible, because of the havoc they wreak down the line in addiction. The key is to find a specialist in AMPS, chronic pain or fibromyalgia.

- You may have to rearrange how you work or do homework, if you have a desk setup – or how you hold your smartphone or tablet. The main thing is describing any repetitive motions or positions in which you sit or stand. As before, keep a journal and note how long you’re in a single position or doing repetitive work like on a computer, phone or tablet.

- You may have to attend physical and/or occupational therapy. Pain is a complex issue to treat, and any doctor that prescribes a pill without any other therapies is in essence masking the problem. I’m not a doctor, and I’m not saying medication can’t help – it absolutely can. What I am saying is that pain is a signal of unwellness in the mind-body system. Fixing that pain sometimes (always?) requires a team approach.

- You may have to attend psychotherapy, as the mind-body connection for some kinds of pain is related. DBT and CBT can be helpful, but it’s important to find the right kind of therapist. I have a friend who told me “more than anything else, is how understood you felt by your therapist. It's not cost, not training, not even mode of therapy.” @ medusaswink.bsky.social

- Migraine and other headaches

- Every migraine specialist I trust was either previously a dentist or was trained to be a dentist. Why? Because dentists have to study in detail, as part of their schooling, the nerves in the head and neck. That’s not to say you can’t find an excellent migraine specialist who was not a dentist, it’s just my experience. The main thing is that there are an entirely new class of migraine medications out there that stop and, in some cases, completely prevent migraine. This is not a matter of prescribing an opioid, and I would be very, very careful about taking medical advice from a doctor prescribing them for migraine, or headaches in general. Do your research. Check out https://americanmigrainefoundation.org/find-a-doctor/ for more information.

- Not all headaches are migraine, and migraine is not just about headaches, they’re complex medical conditions. There are also more than one kind of migraine. There are cluster headaches, tension headaches, hypertension headaches and others. There are different kinds of pain that impact your head.

- It’s important that you seek medical attention immediately because some head pain can be symptoms of underlying serious conditions. Get seen. Find the facts. Then, get better – you do not have to suffer, and you have the right to live free from pain. As you already know, when you have migraine or severe head pain, you can do little else, and this can severely impact your ability to live a healthy life.

- Hypermobility Spectrum Disorders including Ehlers-Danos Syndrome

- If you are one of the 80% of Autistic people who have one of these conditions, it’s good to have a great rheumatologist who specializes in these conditions treating you.

- Follow the instructions they give you.

- There are additional options, detailed in Section III “For Physically Disabled”

### Sleep Management

My great-grandmother used to say: “everything comes out in the wash.” What she meant, for those of you who didn’t grow up in the South, was that the truth will come out one way or the other so you might as well deal with it now. For “the wash,” my dear reader, is your ability to fall asleep and stay asleep. If you:

- Fall asleep but most importantly wake up at a different time each day.

- Read on an iPad, tablet, laptop or other electronic screen after 6-7 pm at night

- Have someone in your life gaslighting, bullying, or otherwise abusing you

- Aren’t having any physical activity, could be:

- A walk

- A roll (if in wheelchair)

- Yoga

- Whatever you can do every day to move will help you sleep

- Have chronic, untreated pain (by a doctor)

- Have untreated epilepsy (by a doctor)

- Have untreated sleep apnea (by a doctor)

- Have untreated social or generalized anxiety or other mental illness (e.g., bipolar, schizophrenia)

- Mental illnesses are complicated, some can be deadly if not treated, and they are absolutely NOTHING of which to be ashamed. (Remember shame is “I am a bad thing” -- mental illness doesn’t qualify for that belief). In 50 years, we’ll be treating anxiety, bipolar and schizophrenia just like stroke or heart disease. Get ahead of the crowd and start looking at them that way now.

- Grave mental illnesses (bipolar, schizophrenia) require treatment from a Psychiatrist, e.g., a doctor

- Other mental disorders such as depression, prolonged anxiety (social or otherwise), PTSD, C-PTSD or any mental condition or trauma that impedes your ability to take care of yourself/go to work requires treatment from a licensed Psychologist (preferably one with a PhD). Remember my friend’s advice: “more than anything else, is how understood you felt by your therapist. It's not cost, not training, not even mode of therapy.” That said, I’m partial to DBT for most anxiety, then ACT+DBT for burnout. I’m partial to EMDR, then Somatic Therapy, for Trauma. If you can find a therapist who is Autistic or works with Autistic people and refuses to perform any form of ABA, then that’s preferred. Remember, there is nothing wrong with your behavior as an Autistic person. Your mindfulness practice and thoughts may need a bit of cognitive coaching, however. We learn a lot of self-harmful beliefs growing up and being bullied as Autistic people, don’t we? It takes time and a kind, healing hand sometimes to help us un-learn what the world has taught us.

Don’t be surprised if your doctor recommends that you start using either melatonin or diphenhydramine (Benadryl) to help you sleep. I’m not a doctor and I’m not prescribing anything here. Just don’t be surprised. You can do an internet search yourself to learn the benefits of both. The key is breaking the cycle of insomnia/sleeplessness. It’s like a bad habit, and one that you absolutely must break, if even for a few days.

Keep in mind that consistently quality sleep is the foundation of mental health, much like breathing, staying hydrated and eating nutritious food is for physical (and mental) health. Make the sacrifices you need to make to do what you can to prioritize yourself so you can regularly sleep...your mind and your body will thank you!

### Team of Care

If you are dealing with any of the medical conditions discussed above, and if you’ve gotten this far chances are good you have more than a couple of them, you likely need a team of specialists. I would recommend at minimum:

- An empathetic General Practitioner (medical doctor)

- If you have a psychiatric condition such as bipolar disorder (I or II), depression (major or otherwise), long-term anxiety (social or otherwise) or schizophrenia spectrum, you need a great Psychiatrist. Not a good one. Dig deep and ask around for the best one in your area. Don’t be surprised if they’re not in your insurance network and not cheap. In my experience, there are two types of Psychiatrists, the best in your area and bad ones. Caveat: I’m speaking for Autistic patients. I’m sure other patients do just fine with a “good” Psychiatrist.

- If you have anything in #2 above, or struggle with mental health that impedes your ability to do work, live life, or any other part of taking care of yourself or others (if you have kids for example), you need a great Psychologist (PhD, highly recommended). What I said about two kinds of Psychiatrists is doubly true about Psychologists: there are the best in your area and bad ones. You want the best in your area. Preferably one who specializes in either DBT, ACT or both. CBT therapists can be good for simple issues, but I’ve had hit-and-miss results, so be really cautious and fire your therapist quickly if you aren’t getting a good feeling and/or they don’t understand what you’re going through. If you can get one that understands Autism, I won’t go so far as saying that’s a must-have, but it’s pretty close to a must-have. There is almost nothing worse for you than having a therapist that gaslights you.

- If you have trauma, you need the best EMDR therapist you can find. Contact either https://www.emdria.org/ or https://www.camdencenter.com/service-options/mental-health-treatment and ask for a referral. They may also be able to refer you to a great DBT or ACT therapist in your area.

- A good physical therapist and/or massage therapist. “The body keeps the score,” and when you’re Autistic, does it ever keep the score. There are a lot of so-so physical therapists and massage therapists, and touch is a big sensory issue for many of us. This may or may not help you. If it does help you, it can be a game-changer and help release some of your built-up tensions and possibly even help you sleep.

- Some people swear by acupuncture, others chiropractic medicine. Personally, I think this is a "hire slowly, fire quickly” sort of provider. If you find them well-reviewed (in Yelp, with hundreds and hundreds of 5* reviews), then you could try it. Personally, I’ve been to both. That said, I haven’t seen one in years.

- An Authentically Autistic Coach. These people are licensed, and ideally you get one that is themselves Autistic or ADHD. They work with young and old(er) people alike. Start with https://aane.org/ or https://www.linkedin.com/showcase/autism-professional-coaching-association/ I’m partial to my coach, @AutisticCoach_ (on X/Twitter), aka Matthew Lawrence for many reasons, but you find the one that suits you best.

- Find your Actually Autistic community on social media (#ActuallyAutistic). Yes, that’s right. You heard me. Seek out the best and mute the rest. I mean seriously, mute with abandon – the second you hear something from someone hateful or just off somehow, mute them ad move on. You can find mean people in the grocery store, no need to go online to find that. We’re looking for kind, informative and helpful Autistic voices. X/Twitter, BlueSky and Mastodon can all be great sources for online #ActuallyAutistic communities. Some people I follow are better than others. I have heard some horrific things about what happens on Facebook, TikTok and Discord. You may have a different experience. Main thing is that they affirm your Authentic Autistic self and back up what they say with facts and research. Autistic people can be clueless, bullying, and misinformed just as much as anyone else. Be cautious and mindful – protect the voices you let into your head.

### Survival Skills: Adapting Environment (Not Social) Skills

So, there are a metric ton of books, papers, classes and therapists who are dedicated to teaching Autistic people social skills. I’ll say something here that might be a bit controversial: you don’t need to learn social skills. You need to learn how to alter your environment to better suit your neurology. Once you do that, the social “issues” you’re having will mostly fall by the wayside. I know, that’s heresy. But hear me out.

So, we in the Autistic community talk a lot about masking and unmasking. And I spent a fair amount of time above talking about this in terms of a Predatory Protective Mimicry response gone wild. Gone wrong – a race to Autistic Burnout. That in many cases, if these bullies aren’t predators, they’re either parasites or predator-parasite hybrids from an Autistic bodily response perspective. But your body is pouring more and more energy (spoons, straws, etc) trying to protectively mimic your way out of the social skill “issues” you’re facing. And as was said way above, “it’s not what we don’t know that gets us in trouble. It’s what we know that just ain’t so.” We know that we’re dealing with bullies, or bullying behavior, and we have to mask to protect ourselves: protective mimicry. But these aren’t predators: they’re more like parasites than a randomly encountered predator. Here’s another piece of heresy: anyone who defends them is also a parasite. You know what they say in Germany: What do you have when you have a table with one Nazi and 11 other people? You have a table of 12 Nazis. Now, does that mean that every social environment is full of parasites? No, just the ones where there’s a bully/parasite and people around him/her or you that make you feel like you should spend more time getting used to being around bullies. Think of it this way. If you had a pond full of leeches (blood sucker parasites), would you encourage a friend to learn leech social skills and spend more time in that pond? Of course not. You’d post a sign and stay the hell out.

So what is the solution then, withdraw from society entirely? No, certainly not. We need more society – more Autistic society – and less time in the pond. Now, you may not like my leech pond analogy. Here’s a different one: sunshine. We need sunshine to produce Vitamin D, just a bit. But, if we get too much then we develop sunburn, and if we continually get too much then we develop skin cancer. It’s radiation. Calling it “being outside in nature” or “daylight” doesn’t really warn us like it should, does it? You can’t, actually, avoid the world and develop the anti-fragile Autistic life you were meant to lead. But, if you’re 80% in the neurotypical world today and 20% or much, much less in the Authentically Autistic world, is that a good environment for you? Or are you swimming in the leech pond/spending all day sunbathing by the pool? What if we flipped the script? What if we were 20% (or much, much less) in the Neurotypical world, and 80% in the Autistic community? How would that feel? Is that even possible? I think it is. I think it’s not only possible, I think it’s necessary. For your health.

Researchers for years have been talking about the Double Empathy Problem – which in a nutshell says Autistic people don’t have a problem with empathy. They do great (or much better) with other Autistic people. When Neurotypical people are around Autistic people, they fail to feel empathy and therefore understand Autistic people: that’s Empathy Problem #1. Empathy Problem #2 (because there are so few of us, makes it a smaller problem) is that Autistic people fail to feel empathy and therefore understand Neurotypical people. A research team recently suggested we have a Triple Empathy Problem: add in the medical providers (doctors, therapists) fail to feel empathy for their Autistic patients. A third Empathy problem, that leads to worsening outcomes for the healthcare of Autistic people through no fault of their own. I tend to agree that the problem of a lack of Neurotypical people empathy towards Autistic people is a big factor in the overall wellbeing of Autistic people. But we can limit the impact that the Double (or Triple) Empathy Problem has on us as Autistic people. Just like we can limit the number of minutes we spend on social media or reality TV. If we set this as a goal, we will find a way to make it happen. But if we continue to believe that more social (parasite) skills are our problem, then we’ll continue to get sick and die young.

The Gallup Poll Organization has a great book that talks about this: Now Discover Your Strengths. It’s a great book for uncovering strengths you may or may not know you have. I highly recommend it. The gist of the book is this: you have been likely told in one way or another, your whole life, that you need to focus on your weaknesses and make them stronger so that you can become a more “well-rounded” individual. Not so, according to authors Buckingham and Clifton from Gallup. In their research, they find that successful individuals that lean into their strengths and manage (around) their weaknesses perform far better than those who try it the other way around. After all, isn’t that what society has been miseducating Autistic people to do all this time? Focus on your neurotypical social skills and develop them. Right? Well, that’s wrong. Neurotypical social skills for Autistic people is a weakness, just as Autistic social skills are a weakness for neurotypical people; we see this in the Double (Triple?) Empathy Problem. We don’t coach all neurotypical people in Autistic social skills, do we? Of course not, that wouldn’t make any sense (to them). Nor should it to us. We aren’t strong in their communication/social style (so-called “social skills,” as if there was only one set of such skills), just as they are not strong in ours. But they focus on their strengths and so should we, according to Gallup, if we wish to lead happy successful lives. Our strengths are numerous, but the top four as I see them are as follows:

- Autistic Joy/Stimming

- Autistic Areas of Concentration

- Specific Sensory Integration Profiles

- Strengths Profile

I introduced this book with the heretical thought: you aren’t stimming enough. What I was really trying to communicate is that Autistic people have a pathway to joy that is specific to us. We perceive the world differently, and I would even say better, than many. We see light differently, sound differently, touch, and all the other senses differently. This is a pathway to joy. When I’m standing on the cliff overlooking Malibu, California I’m sure I see the ocean below differently than my wife standing next to me. I know I do, because I surfed some of these same spots and I’m seeing the waves the way only a surfer can. Or in my case, an Autistic surfer. There is no joy like being in nature for me. The smells (some too pungent), the sounds (some too shrill), and sights (some too bright) -- but all of it a kaleidoscope of experiences that once blended and absorbed in my mind become something truly unique and joyful and relaxing. My daughter, as a toddler years ago, used to spin for minutes and minutes on a one-person “spinny” apparatus on the playground (sort of like a “sit ‘n spin” – but for standing)! It made my wife and I dizzy just watching her. But it was pure joy for her. A joy the other children could not and would not experience. Ever. Find your Autistic Joy and lean into it with gusto and often. If sensory overload is the rush of sugar in our bloodstream from eating an apple, this is the fiber in that apple that balances it all out. That’s strength number one.

Strength number two is a true superpower. We can focus on one topic for hours, and in some cases days, on end without breaks. Some call this “special interests” or “hyperfixation,” which are not my favorite descriptions. Others, researchers who developed the concept of “monotropism” (Murray, Lawson and Lesser, 2005), talk about “flow states.” That works, or “areas of concentration” (AOC). Flow makes far more sense to me. When you’re in flow, the world around falls away. You may not be able to hear, you may not be able to feel, but you are in a true “flow” of mental energy that seems to give more energy than it takes. We can dive deeper, absorb more content, and think more broadly about a topic than our neurotypical peers. It’s a miracle of evolutionary biology: and yes, most everything you’ve been told about this aspect of Autism is backwards. It’s not a “symptom of the disease.” It’s not a “social problem.” As we say in the world of computer software, it’s not a bug – it's a feature!

But it does require you to recognize that, like stimming, you don’t need less of it. It’s a strength. Lean into it. Do it more. But, set timers for yourself, ok? You need to eat, you need to sleep, you need rest – even from your superpower, right? Not even Superwoman/Superman flew around all the time. Gravity pulls on all of us, and we owe it some respect. Like the ancient Greeks would say: “in all things, no excess” -- and I would add including no excess. Cut loose every once and a while. But those around you need to respect that this is part of how you experience the world and it’s a very healthy thing. Don’t let anyone tell you differently. And don’t spend much time with people who do try to tell you differently... remember, not too much sunlight, it’s bad for you!

OK, while I’m on the topic of superheroes, here’s an old trope: the superhero’s greatest strength was also her/his greatest weakness. Superwoman/man came from Krypton, so the story goes, and it was this lineage and our yellow sun that gave them their super abilities. However, chunks of kryptonite (from, you know, Krypton) would weaken them. Well, I’m here to tell you that this is a story based in Algebra, and like the old rule goes, what happens on the right side must also happen on the left. If someone is talking about your greatest weakness (sensory integration), guess what? That’s might just be your greatest untapped strength. Why do I say this? Because not only is this a relatively well-established fact in the wisdom books that your weakness is a hidden reservoir of strength, it’s also well known in the Deaf community that reduced or no hearing begets better touch, better sight and better smell. Is it true for Autistic people? Let’s do a thought experiment, which for me was an actual experiment. What if we have light hypersensitivity. #### Dime Game (Pruning-light) or Setting Healthy Boundaries

One other important tool, this one from Dialectical Behavioral Therapy (DBT), is the Dime Game. It’s part of interpersonal effectiveness, and it’s super helpful if you have difficulty setting boundaries or asking for things you need. We as Autistic people often grow up trying to please others – or we’ve gotten so burned out (from people-pleasing) that we give up on socializing with people entirely. This Dime Game helps you transition out of people-pleasing, but conversely gives you a way to get back into socializing with others without fear of being used as maybe you were in the past.

The way it works is simple. You start with either something you are asking for or something you want to say no to. Both can be difficult if you have a people-pleasing mindset. Although you don’t need to use literal dimes ($0.10) in a jar, it’s the idea that matters as you ask each question – each yes is a dime.

From https://eymtherapy.com/blog/learn-to-say-no-dime-game/

When deciding on how strongly to ask for something:

You put a dime in the bank for each of the questions below that get a yes answer. The more money you have, the stronger you ask.

- Capability: Is this person able to give or do what I want?

- Priorities: Is getting my objective more important than my relationship with this person?

- Self-respect: Will asking help me feel competent and self-respecting?

- Rights: Is the person required by law or moral code to do or give me what I want?

- Authority: Am I responsible for telling the person what to do?

- Relationship: Is what I want appropriate for this relationship? (Is it okay to ask for what I want?)

- Goals: Is asking important to a long-term goal?

- Give and Take: Do I give as much as I get with this person?

- Homework: Do I know what I want and have the facts I need to support my request?

- Timing: Is this a good time to ask? (Is this person in the right mood?)

Then tap into the wise mind and go back over the list to see if some items are more important than others.

When deciding on how strongly to say no:

You put a dime in the bank for each of the questions below that get a no answer. The more money you have, the stronger you should say no. Remember: This does not count times of non-consensual touching or behaviors – you always have the right to say no and be respected in this capacity regardless of how many dimes you do or do not have.

- Capability: Can I give this person what is wanted?

- Priorities: Is my relationship more important than saying no?

- Self-respect: Will saying no make me feel bad about myself?

- Rights: Am I required by law or moral code to give or do what is wanted, or does saying no violate this person’s rights?

- Authority: Is this other person responsible for telling me what to do?

- Relationship: Is what the person is requesting of me appropriate to my relationship with this person?

- Goals: In the long term, will I regret saying no?

- Give and Take: Do I owe this person a favor? (Does this person do a lot for me when I ask and need something?)

- Homework: Do I know what I am saying no to? (Is the other person clear about what is being asked for?)

- Timing: Should I wait a while before saying no?

Then tap into the wise mind and go back over the list to see if some items are more important than others.

- Withhold participation

- Talk less

- Listen less

- Doodle more

It is well known that the way to get ahead in business is to participate, speak up, listen intently and focus on the task at hand. As an Autistic person, this advice is exactly backwards. You will get next to nothing from meetings and you owe it to your mental health to do all that you can to avoid (prune) them. If you can’t avoid them, the advice above will help. You will paradoxically get more from the meetings with less stress.

We’ve also been told, in the middle of all of these time-sucking (read: spoon draining) tasks of online meetings, that productivity is important, but not at the cost of collaboration. Again, wrong for Autistic people. Our superpower is deep focus and uninterrupted, undivided time. Collaboration is the problem. If you must do collaboration, one-on-one and rare meetings is the way to go. Build one-on-one relationships with a small number of people and shorthand and push, push, push for minimal meetings with everyone and use written correspondence instead. Also? Push for being offline instant messaging/chat apps (e.g., Slack, Teams, Zoom, etc) as much as you can. Instant Messaging applications may be all the rage, but rage (or anxiety) is exactly what they will produce inside of you in the way of meltdowns, shutdowns and burnout. Stay off of instant messaging apps of all kinds as much as you possibly can. Finally, we are told to multitask and learn to become good at ambiguity (what they really mean is vagueness). Again, wrong for Autistic people.

Multitasking, if this is an expectation of your management, is a sign you’re in the wrong job. We need to be able to handle tasks in sequence – NOT in parallel. By the way, I used to run performance and stress testing for the largest software company on earth. Single CPUs do not multitask. Our single CPU (the human brain) doesn’t either – it’s yet another Ableist, toxic notion that we’ve all just silently accepted. Managing ambiguity or vagueness, if a requirement, is a sign you’re in the wrong job. We need clear, detailed written instructions (preferably with pictures). I used to work for the largest automotive company on earth. Every month we would get clear, one-page desk guides with pictures on how to do new processes. It’s not rocket science and it helps everyone. If you can’t get clear, step-by-step written task instructions, you’re in the wrong job.

Remember that the accommodations flow to you, the disabled person. They don’t flow from you, who are least able to give them. That said, I do NOT recommend that you attempt to get accommodations from your company’s Human Resources or manager for you under an Autistic diagnosis. Get a hearing test and go for that disability (lots of us have working memory deficits and that can show up under Auditory Processing Disorder). Get an ADHD test (if hearing doesn’t work) and go for that disability. Autism is not a label you want to carry with you into the workplace – it is not safe in this part of the 21st Century and it will make you a target for dismissal. Your management can and will fire you and they will find a legal way to do it. Don’t be a victim.

### What is “Acting Inappropriately,” Anyway?

So most Autistic people have had the experience of being told that they’re acting inappropriately. In 2007, I stood up and asked the head of Microsoft Human Resources (when I was a manager there) in a room full of other managers. I asked if the employee survey would be a factor in manager annual review/performance appraisal. In a very snide, laughing tone I was told that would never happen. I had acted inappropriately. I suggested that what we told employees, that their voice, the survey really mattered to management. If it did, so I reasoned, then the survey should be reflected in how managers like me were compensated. How ridiculous I was (to them)! Of course, it wouldn’t work like that. How inappropriate! OK. So, we don’t mean what we say. Got it.

When people try (or succeed) in convincing you that you’ve spoken/acted inappropriately, what they are attempting to do is make you feel shame. Not guilt. Not that you did something bad. That you are something bad. Was I “bad” for suggesting Microsoft “walk the talk” and reward managers when their employees were satisfied and indeed punish managers that didn’t? Of course not. Was I made to feel that shame in a hall of 50 fellow managers? You bet.

“Appropriate” is defined by the Oxford English Dictionary as:

“Specially fitted or suitable, proper”

Which do you think most neurotypical people mean when they say appropriate? Specially fitted? Proper? No, I think it is the middle definition: suitable. And the judgment of the behavior, action, words in this case is transitive to you: it’s not that your behavior or words are not suitable – it's you. You are not suitable. It is intended to make you feel what you feel: shame/rejection. Something most of us feel more deeply and would avoid at any cost – and this is a universal feeling, culturally speaking. Avoid shame, and especially shame to your family, at all costs. Protect your reputation with your life. You know the advice; you’ve all heard it in one way or another.

“Inappropriate” is a little different, according to Oxford English Dictionary:

“Not appropriate; unsuitable to the particular case; unfitting, improper.”

OK, so this first definition takes our working definition of appropriate, suitable, and puts a “not” in front of it. So, we’re not suitable, according to so many neurotypicals. We can skip the second definition because we’re not clothing to be “unsuitable to the particular case.” The fourth definition of improper almost nails it – we're acting, speaking improperly per their judgment. But what of this third definition: “unfitting.” Yes, that’s it. Let’s turn it around: “you don’t fit in here.” “It might be better if you found a place you fit in better, because it’s not here with us.” That’s really the message, isn’t it? We aren’t one of them, and they'd like it if we went away, right?

I’m going to let you in on a little secret. Many Autistic people, even younger ones, are pretty good at masking, at “fitting in.” As long as what is happening makes sense. When it doesn’t make sense, we often find ourselves unable to sit still, not stim, shut up and “fit in.” We speak out. We act out. Unwillingly, or at least unconsciously it seems. And here’s the real secret: people telling you that your behavior or words is not their signal to you. It’s your signal to you. You’ve signaled to yourself that you’re opting out of their club, environment, etc. Neurotypical behavior is not actually a society – it's a country club. Groucho Marx once famously said: “I don’t want to belong to any [neurotypical?] club that would accept me as one of its members.” Being neurotypical is not diagnostic of who belongs in a society: being neurodiverse, is. We’re either all in society or we are not a society at all. To say something is socially unacceptable, or inappropriate, isn’t meant to keep society civil – it's meant to drive out those that do not or cannot fall in line. That’s not civilization, or society, again Oxford:

“The aggregate of persons living together in a community”

When we are being shamed for behavior or words – many of which we simply cannot control – that’s what happens in a country club, with strict rules of dress and behavior and wealth and power that is designed to marginalize and filter out “certain types.”

Saying your behavior is “inappropriate” -- as I was – dressed down at that Microsoft manager’s meeting, was meant to enforce country club rules. ### Environmental Engineering for Autistic People

There is more than one way to get what you need and want out of life. Said another way, “where there’s a will, there’s a way.” Let’s pursue this a bit further with the different options:

- Classic Neurotypical Model: you learn how to “fit in” or better yet force others to “fit in” and climb and climb so you can get table scraps from people with real power and money. It doesn’t matter what you “do” for a living, what really matters is that you are a moving performance art installation. You are a living mask and in no way represent the true you, whomever that is (as they likely don’t know). Self-denial (and largely, self-hatred) is common if not universal: this is George Orwell’s “1984” or better yet Aldous Huxley’s “Brave New World.” Tell me we aren’t taking “soma” in overprescribed Adderall for non-ADHDers or overprescribed anti-depressants or marijuana or street level cocaine/heroin/fentanyl, etc.

- Passion Model: you may adopt parts of 1 above, but you’re “following your passion.” You’ve heard this: “do what you love, and you’ll never work a day in your life.” This could also be called the Unlikely Dream or Funnel Model, in that of the many people who enter this approach, only an infinitesimally small number of people exit the funnel successfully. Nice idea but the reality is sadly elusive. This is a sucker punch model for Autistic people.

- Craftsman Model: this comes from comedian Steve Martin who famously said, “be so good they can’t ignore you.” Again, while much more attractive than the Passion Model in that getting good at something (“areas of concentration”) is something Autistic people were born to do. We do it naturally. Hyper-focused and ready to rock and roll. The problem is it’s still a funnel: if your AOC is My Little Pony or Star Wars, then it really doesn’t do much for you in terms of helping you attain power, money or status, does it? I know some of you might be saying these are all Capitalistic Values but believe me that anyone in a Socialistic Society works effectively the exact same way. They might do a better job of hiding it, but systems aren’t fundamentally different no matter the economic black magic they practice. Look under the covers at China, USSR/Russia, collectives across the world, cults, monastic religious communities, and the remaining subsistence communities – you'll see that money, power and status drive those places too they’re just called different things. People may try to convince you that money, power and status don’t matter, which is true – unless you’re Autistic and don’t have any of those things – and then they become unbelievably important. Autistic people are a walking gift to this planet and to the human species – and way too valuable to settle for less than being treated like the genetic royalty we are.

- Modified Craftsman Model: “be so good at something the world needs that they can’t ignore you.” I know Autistic computer hardware and software developers. Writers. Scientists. Researchers. Data Analysts. Engineers of every kind. Electricians. Plumbers. Drafts people. Construction workers. Autistic coaches. Do you need a special degree or certification? Hyper focus is good for doing that, too. The world needs people in certain roles who can focus really, really well. Don’t be surprised if you have to pick up a new AOC – that’s OK! I was never into programming or really that much into computers, but once I learned I could do a job where I was (mostly) respected for my knowledge and expertise and got to focus for long stretches of time, believe me it became maybe not a AOC, but an interest that I was willing to devote significant hours of my day getting better at.

In fact, neurotypical people who try these fields either quickly wash out as incompetent or quickly become managers of the job they used to have. I was a manager for 6 years. Most miserable 6 years of my professional life. I’m not a personal communications expert nor am I a face-to-face human problem solver – nothing dysregulates me faster. I wasn’t built with that equipment. If you were, good on you. If you weren’t, and you’re also Autistic, you need to know your Sensory Profile really, really well. And then look for opportunities in the world where that profile does well. You might need an Authentically Autistic Coach to help you build a bridge from your sensory profile to a career or starting a business. That’s more than OK! I wouldn’t have survived the first year of calculus without a tutor. I probably wouldn’t have survived the last couple of years without therapists and coaches, including my friend Matthew Lawrence (@AutisticCoach_).

It is the best time in the last 5,000 years of human history to be Autistic: the neurotypicals need us so badly, and if we (hyper) focus on the right things that all people really need, we will be so good that they will not be able to ignore us or succeed in their attempts to make us feel “inappropriate.” We import people who can hyper focus from India, China, and anywhere because we have such a shortage of people who can do these jobs or start these businesses. The best part is that if you find your way into a field that is (a) needed and (b) requires hyperfocus, you’re going to automatically find yourself around more neurodivergent people which overall is better than the alternative. Almost as if by magic.

The one caveat I will say here is know your worth. You aren’t broken. You aren’t sick. You aren’t an employment charity case. If you have aligned your hyperfocus with something highly valuable (a trade, a profession) -- you are in control and don’t let anyone make you believe otherwise. However, many like us don’t know that or believe the opposite of Groucho Marx – that we’re lucky to belong to any club that would have us. All Autistic people need to adopt a little more from Groucho Marx. If you’re being abused or mistreated in your workplace or business, for God’s sake and for your own health, find somewhere they’ll treat you well. There are a lot of abusive bosses out there looking for Autistic people because they are disabled – be aware! This is exploitation and abusive behavior. I’d argue, convincingly I might add, that being an abusive boss is bad for business, especially when it comes to working with Autistic people (our productivity grinds to nil)! But regardless of what you believe economically, make being treated well in your job or business a priority. Make that a hyperfocus, too. Actually Autistic Coaches like Matthew Lawrence and others can help with this, too.

### Characteristics of a Healthy Autistic Online Community are:

- A shared sense of being a community based on history and valuesa¹

- A general feeling of empowerment and control over matters that affect the community as a wholea¹

- Existing structures that allow subgroups within the community to participate in decision making in community mattersa¹

- The ability to cope with challenges and changesa¹

- A positive culture that supports social connection and sense of communitya⁴

- Responsiveness to the needs and concerns of the community membersa⁵

- Encourage critical thinking and open dialogue among members. Avoid suppressing dissent or questioning, and respect different opinions and perspectives.d¹d²

- Promote democratic and transparent decision making. Avoid giving one person or a small group of people absolute power or authority over the group. Allow members to have a say in the group's policies, goals, and activities.d¹d²

- Respect the individuality and autonomy of members. Avoid imposing strict rules or norms on how members should dress, behave, think, or feel. Allow members to have their own personal lives, interests, and relationships outside the group.d¹d²

- Foster a culture of inclusiveness and diversity. Avoid creating an us-versus-them mentality or demonizing outsiders. Recognize the value and contribution of people from different backgrounds, beliefs, and experiences. Seek to learn from and collaborate with other groups or organizations that share common goals or values.d¹d²

- Maintain ethical and accountable standards. Avoid engaging in illegal or harmful activities, such as abuse, violent language, fraud, or coercion. Admit mistakes and take responsibility for the consequences of the group's actions. Seek feedback and evaluation from members and external sources to improve the group's performance and quality.d¹d²
