Feelings of isolation and shame can have significant impacts on a person’s health, both physically and mentally. Here are some key points based on scholarly references:

- Mental Health: Isolation and shame can lead to increased risk for mental health disorders such as depression12. Young people are more susceptible to the onset of depression and have a higher risk of loneliness1.

- Physical Health: Both loneliness and social isolation have been associated with an increased risk for coronary artery disease-associated death, even in middle-aged adults without a prior history of myocardial infarction3.

- Behavioral Changes: Recursive experiences of shame can exacerbate a global negative self-attribution that is often associated with adverse effects on mental well-being such as poor psychological adjustment, interpersonal difficulties, and overall poor life functioning2.

- Emotional Impact: Feelings of guilt of infecting near and dear ones adds on to the pain of remaining socially isolated from the family in a “locked up” state that can have disruptive consequences on emotional reaction and impulsivity2.

- Self-Esteem: Untreated guilt that is related with a stressor event can be associated with severe mental health complications including posttraumatic stress disorder, depression, suicidal ideation, problematic substance use, and poorer functioning and quality of life2.

Remember that these are general impacts and the specific effects can vary from person to person.

Long-term stress can have significant effects on the immune system:

Reduced Immune Response: Chronic stress can reduce the number of natural killer cells or lymphocytes in the body, which are needed to fight viruses1. Over time, stress hormones can weaken your immune system and reduce your body’s response to foreign invaders2.

Increased Inflammation: Stress can cause the immune system to produce an inflammatory response, which can be temporarily beneficial for fighting germs. However, if inflammation is persistent and widespread, it can contribute to chronic diseases1.

Increased Risk of Illness: People under chronic stress are more susceptible to viral illnesses like the flu and the common cold, as well as other infections. Stress can also increase the time it takes you to recover from an illness or injury2.

Disruption of Body Processes: The long-term activation of the stress response system and too much exposure to cortisol and other stress hormones can disrupt almost all the body’s processes. This puts you at higher risk of many health problems, including anxiety, depression, digestive problems, headaches, heart disease, sleep problems, weight gain, and problems with memory and focus3.

Increased Risk of Chronic Diseases: Chronic stress leads to persistently high cortisol levels, which cause resistance to cortisol and impaired anti-inflammatory effects on the immune system. Such effects result in chronic infection, chronic inflammatory autoimmune diseases, or cancers as well as other physiological disorders4.

Increased Risk of Specific Diseases: Stress increases the risk of diabetes, worsens asthma, and increases the potential to develop ulcerative colitis5.

It’s important to manage stress effectively to maintain a healthy immune system. If you’re dealing with stress you can’t seem to shake, take time to identify the sources and find ways to avoid or cope with them1.

Children with autism are often more susceptible to toxic stress due to various factors. Studies suggest that children on the spectrum are up to three times as likely as their neurotypical peers to be targets of bullying and physical or sexual abuse1. Such maltreatment can cause severe stress and trauma, yet it often goes unrecognized or unreported1.

Toxic stress from Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) can negatively affect children’s brain development, immune systems, and stress-response systems2. These changes can affect children’s attention, decision-making, and learning2. Children growing up with toxic stress may have difficulty forming healthy and stable relationships2.

While specific prevalence rates of toxic stress in autistic children are not readily available, it’s important to note that about 1 in 36 children has been identified with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) according to estimates from CDC’s Autism and Developmental Disabilities Monitoring (ADDM) Network3. Given the increased susceptibility of these children to toxic stress, it underscores the importance of early intervention and supportive measures to mitigate the impact of stress on these children.

Yes, increased susceptibility to viral infection can indeed increase the risk for cancer. Here’s how:

Direct Effect on Cell Growth: Some viruses can directly affect the genes inside cells that control their growth. These viruses can insert their own genes into the cell, causing the cell to grow out of control1.

Weakening of the Immune System: Some infections weaken the immune system, making the body less able to fight off other cancer-causing infections2.

Chronic Inflammation: Some viruses, bacteria, and parasites can cause chronic inflammation, which may lead to cancer2.

Oncogenic Viruses: Some viruses are known as oncogenic viruses because they can cause or contribute to the development of cancer. Unlike other viruses, such as influenza viruses, that cause an acute infection, oncogenic viruses often cause long-term, persistent infections3.

Infections Leading to Specific Cancers: Certain infectious agents, including viruses, bacteria, and parasites, can cause specific types of cancer. For example, chronic infections with Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) or Hepatitis C Virus (HCV) can cause liver cancer2.

It’s important to note that not all infections lead to cancer, and many factors can influence a person’s risk. If you have concerns about this, it’s recommended to consult with a healthcare professional.
