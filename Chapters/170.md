## Chapter 17: Autistiphobia

In the Autistic community we talk a lot about “ableism,” and that’s a
helpful way to start to think about our experience. We are disabled, and
ableism has a definite bias and set of discriminatory behaviors, mores
and laws that go along with it. But in the same way that the Lesbian and
Gay community in the mid-20<sup>th</sup> Century felt that “bigotry” was
a necessary but insufficient word to describe what they were
experiencing, I find “ableism” the same. The word that became important
in the Gay and Lesbian community was “homophobia;” literally fear-driven
bigotry of people who loved the same gender. We have “transphobia” that
speaks to intersex and the trans community’s experience. Anyone inside
the trans community can experience transphobia from anyone outside the
trans community. Neither “homophobia” nor “bigotry” covers the
experience they face on the daily. What I’m about to describe is
specific to Autistic people, and anyone inside the Autistic community
can experience it from anyone outside the Autistic community. “Ableism”
doesn’t begin to cover it; so, I propose we begin to use the word
“autistiphobia,” a portmanteau of Autism and phobia.

Is autistiphobia different from the Medical/Pathologic Model of Autism?
In essence, no, because the latter drives the former. What is worse is
that the latter *legitimizes* the former, enabling it to be enshrined in
law and medical insurance practice. Is autistiphobia, at origin,
different from the homophobia different from transphobia different from
racism different from misogyny different from ableism?  Not in its
origin: it’s all ultimately about fear of those who in custom, culture,
language, appearance, sexuality, behavior, ability, or appearance
different than us. I make the distinction of autistiphobia not because
of a different origin than ableism, but because the manifestation of
“ableism” (towards Autistic people) is inadequate to cover the number
and manner of ways that ableism impacts us and disables us. Society has
made a conscious decision to build wheelchairs and ramps and closed
captioning and Braille available for physically disabled people, but
does society really have to accommodate the invisible disabilities
specific to Autism? For many this is a legitimate question; and this is
at the heart of the distinction I’m making – Autistic people are
discriminated against in a particularly damaging way that is not being
addressed by society. ADA and similar legislation does not cover this,
not by a long shot. It’s past time it did.

The cycles of bigotry/ableism will perpetuate until we talk about and
start to dismantle the underlying root cause: <u>S</u>ingle
<u>O</u>ption <u>U</u>nattainable <u>R</u>ole, <u>C</u>reating
<u>E</u>ntropy (SOURCE).

Heteronormative, neurotypical, patriarchal/colonial gendered roles, or
SOURCE, are driving Autistic people into the position where <u>we must
do the thing we cannot do</u>.  SOURCE has many names; the double-bind,
the no-win scenario, but the phrase that I think fits the best is “crazy
making.” Crazy making is a particular form of abuse so cruel that many
recognize it as a form of torture. SOURCE drives the Fear Cycle for
bullies, the Grief Cycle for loved ones of Autistic people, each in turn
driving Autistic people into their own (eventually Destructive) Mimicry
Cycle. Let me explain.

There are three cycles, all working in tandem. We’ll start with the
simplest, which is where the loved one of an Autistic person often
lives, due to shame inflicted upon them by society: autistiphobia and
SOURCE. The grief is real, even if the standard is fake: a societal
construct that no one can live up to. The parents, caretakers, and loved
ones often grieve over the challenges their Autistic loved one will face
as they struggle to meet this impossible standard. Trust me, this makes
no sense to an Autistic person, but as a parent I went through it myself
– as do many. I didn’t then understand the joy and possibilities in an
Autistic life. This is autistiphobia and SOURCE welling up inside an
Autist’s loved one.

Grief of all kinds follows the Grief Cycle, which was created by
Swiss-American psychiatrist Elisabeth Kübler-Ross, in her seminal work
<u>On Death and Dying</u>. There classically five stages in a curve
which looks roughly like this:

At the beginning many of us try to deny the source of grief, often
because of the Normalcy Bias, which then leads eventually to anger as
the first stage of acceptance. Then there is a bargaining stage where
the person makes deals with God, etc. to try to stave off the source of
grief. Bargaining is often where parents and caretakers go astray and
seek out “cures” and “solutions” to Autism through ABA, et al.
Regardless, after bargaining fails comes depression which can last a
while, after which comes for a healthy person acceptance.

Grief Cycle

In the Autistic family/friend circles, this grief is more of a cycle
than a wave. It’s very important that we do what we can to educate
parents, caretakers and other loved ones early about the needs of
Autistic people – to stop Denial in its tracks. Denial is a source of
Gaslighting for Autistic people and it’s very important that caretakers
and loved one’s support and be open-minded listeners to everyday
problems like bullying, sensory integration issues, and fatigue – these
are realities for Autistic people and denial, while neurotypical, is
deeply harmful to the self-esteem and overall wellbeing of the Autistic
person. Likewise, bargaining must be educated away because Autism is a
neurotype – it is not an illness to be cured. Instead of bargaining, the
energy needs to be put into first aid and other preventative health
risks to best support the Autistic person’s health and not focus on
“cures” that can and do make Autistic people sick.

What is different from Kübler-Ross's Grief “wave,” is that in Autistic
caretaker grief, if not dealt with early through education, instead of
resolving in Acceptance which is nearly impossible for many caretakers
because of SOURCE pressures, Depression gives way to Resignation. This
is very unhealthy, as it becomes over time a type of learned
helplessness that can also give way to seeking “treatment” (or giving
into it) with ABA. I believe this phase is very dangerous (which is why
it’s in **red**) in that it must be stopped through therapy with the
caretaker or other support for unhealthy treatments like ABA, chelation,
eugenics and other “cures” may be sought. Education is key. The other
reason resignation is in red is because if not stopped, this wave
becomes a cycle (habituation) and the process goes on and on. Denial
keeps happening. Bargaining, depression, and so forth. The reason these
last parts of the cycle are gray is because it becomes a very low level
of its own trauma and learned helplessness. Each time the severity of
the steps can become more intense, and it reinforces a destructive idea
that the Autistic person they love is either a disappointment or worse a
mistake. This is tragically untrue, unnecessary, and can cause illness
in the caretaker but most importantly it will cause illness (both mental
and physical) in the Autistic person through toxic stress, discussed
earlier in the book. SOURCE/autistiphobia is driving all of this, which
is not only a lie, but a dangerous deception at the heart of modern
society.

Stigma (Bullying) Cycle

The Stigma cycle is a bit more complex, which is where strangers,
acquaintances, and even friends of Autistic people often live, due to
SOURCE. Note that I add “Bullying” after Rage, because this is a key
part of the Autistic experience and I believe it is connected to the
Shame cycle Dr. Brené Brown describes. But there’s a bit more, beyond,
especially for Autistic people and bullying.

Never forget that there is something deep inside autistiphobic behavior
that triggers great relief and activates the pleasure centers of the
oppressor's brain. This is not a wave of pleasure that comes from
altruism: they know they are not “doing the Autistic person a favor.”
This is sadism. But it is institutionalized in that bullies are the
enforcers of SOURCE.  They are foot soldiers who serve often silently
but with purpose and tacit approval from (too) many around them. They
protect the tribe from the few who make the many uncomfortable.
Discomfort that arises from shame in the self-enforced fraud of SOURCE. 
So, we see not only habituation but reward.

Not everyone bullies. Many more will shun, so this cycle is a spectrum
of behavior from active abuse to shunning/isolating the Autistic person.
But the net effect is that whatever the case, the cycle continues and is
self-reinforcing. Unlike grief, however, this has a strong social reward
component. The loved one is not rewarded for abusing or placing their
Autistic loved one in ABA, etc. It often becomes a feeling of learned
helplessness for them, because they’ve been sold on the concept of a
“cure,” which is of course impossible since it’s not an illness. For the
bully/shunner, there is great social accolades to be received for
“standing up” to the Autistic person. Neurodivergent behavior is seen by
the bully through the lens of fear; fear of the unknown, fear of the
different, fear of the threat of perhaps having to examine the
falsehoods of SOURCE. This fear is at the heart of autistiphobia, not
that neurotypicals fear becoming Autistic, but that they fear that they
may have to challenge uncomfortable truths about the foundational fraud
of SOURCE in their own lives. Autistic people by their very nature, also
remind neurotypical people of their vulnerability and that too generates
fear.

So, rather than process the fear and deal with it head on, they attack
like a group of primates in the jungle. First it starts with blame, and
shame. Then, if those don’t change the behavior of the Autistic person,
which of course they don’t because they can’t, then it goes to rage and
bullying. If bullying is rewarded, then it becomes habituated. It’s very
important that the items in black (blame and shame) be educated out of
the population, because this is where the easiest, cheapest and most
effective social change can take place. Of course, reward must be
addressed, which is why it’s in **red**. It’s critical that no one in
society is rewarded, through verbal or non-verbal means, for abusing
another. It teaches horrifically destructive lessons to everyone and
critically it trains the bully to keep the cycle going. Often (if not
always), this cycle perpetuates upon isolated people who are often (if
not always) Autistic. Neurotypical people learn fast that they must
become part of a crowd to survive. Autistic people rarely have that
option in real life – with so few of us in the general population –
until they learn how to band together with other Autistic people often
later in life.
