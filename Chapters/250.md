That leads us to our next set of rules.

Rule #5: Keeping a Positive Mental Attitude is your superpower. Your situation is akin to Special Forces, try to learn from their coping strategies.

Rule #6: You must train for a PMA every day and with others who are as well. So that when you need it, you have it. Google the word "mantras" for examples43. As a young adult, I looked in the mirror every day upon waking-up for years and said “I’ll be better today than I was yesterday” – nothing religious, nothing economic, nothing behavioral – just simple affirmations that have a focus on well-being.

Rule #7: You must find a way to get away from or minimize your time with negative people. They’re poisoning your mind, or at least working against your PMA. There is evidence they are shortening your lifespan44.

Corollary #3: If you panic, you’ve moved from possible danger to real danger. See Corollary 4.

Corollary #4: If you feel yourself start to panic, it’s ok. You need to separate yourself from whatever triggering situation you’re in and ground yourself before it gets out of hand. You always have choices. Skip ahead to "On Grounding" later in the book.

Corollary #5: Seek out to find then spend time with your Autistic team. Train with people, who like you, are laser focused on Autistic survival skills and thriving, prioritizing PMA.

One final word. This is not about you becoming superhuman or something you were not made to be. This is about recognizing that being neurodivergent, and specifically Autistic, is one of the most difficult things human beings have or ever will do. Learn from those that do unsafe things in safer ways, like Special Forces operatives. Study how they do it. And train, together with other Autistic people. Loved ones, this means supporting Autistic people and helping them find a group of healthy Autistic adults, preferably an Autistic coach (who is Autistic/Neurodivergent) who have successfully navigated their way through this process. They can and will be your guides. In many cases, happily.

Ok, so stimming is one relief/path to more joy and focusing on a positive mental attitude is our superpower. So, now we shift gears. There’s an old saying that “if you can name your demons, you can defeat them.” Now, I’m not going down an angels-and-demons path with you. But ancient people, with a pre-scientific understanding of the world, were resourceful and some were very clever. They learned you can’t defeat a thing unless you know and name that thing. So, here goes.

We know what prevents Autistic people from living older than 53 (in the US)45,46. Epidemiologists and Gerontologists study this. For NT people, we all know the top three causes of death45. Remember the Gates Foundation early mission: find and eliminate the top killers of children worldwide — and thereby saving millions of people’s lives and reduce untold misery. So, for NTs, public health and medical research and medical treatments should be focused on those killers of NTs, right? And so they are: heart disease, cancer and respiratory disease (of course, this includes COVID-19+variants) kill NT people more often than not. Top three. Been that way for a long time. Generations, at least in the US. “Blue Zones,” the book and Netflix series I mentioned earlier, was all about dealing with that too. It’s called harm-reduction. Not death prevention. We all die. But how soon we die and how much we suffer before we do is a good focus for healthcare. And so it goes. Follow the money. It flows into researching and preventing those top three killers.

That’s NTs. But what about Autistic people? They live to 80. We live to 53. Same reasons? Same top three? Not exactly. In fact, not only are the top three not the same, in the studies we saw we don’t share a single cause in common amongst the top three. Not one (although some studies show that heart disease and cancer do also kill a lot of Autistic people). What’s going on? What are the top three killers of Autistic people? From this point forward I’ll call it IEMS, but here they are in ranked order (note some sources change the order):

- Injury (largely accidental drowning, choking, suffocating)

- Epilepsy

- MD (I spelled this out early in the book, it’s also in the Glossary at the back)

And, although this is not a direct killer of Autistic people, I’ll include a fourth:

- Sensory Overload/Crises

If you go through enough of #4, it may not kill you, but it may make you wish that you were dead – and may lead you back to #3. So, all four have to be addressed.

OK, so we know what kills us the most (and don’t forget heart disease and cancer, those make the list, too). We know those three things are driving our longevity to ⅔ what NTs have, and half of what people in Blue Zones enjoy. Which of course means a lot of Autistic people die much younger than 53. Way too young, across the board. Yet, and most importantly, we know how to minimize the effects of every one of the top killers of Autistic people. How much money is being spent on preventing the top three killers of Autistic people? Not much. From my research I found no direct evidence of research spending on these top three killers of Autistic people. It’s part of why I wrote this book. To give you the tools to get better, feel better and thrive. And, if you’re listening – researchers – we need more work done on prevention of these top killers, pronto.
