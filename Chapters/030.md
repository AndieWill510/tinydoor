## Chapter 3: Stimming Is a Beautiful Pathway to Autistic Joy

I’m going to introduce a term, which many (all?) Autistics already know
– *stimming*. Stimming was and is still a funny word for me, even though
I remember *the act* of stimming as one of my earliest childhood
memories. It brought joy to my very young life, which at that point was
in very short supply. Entire books could be written about stimming, and
one nearly was called <u>Unmasking Autism</u> by Dr. Devon Price. I’m
not doing the book justice really, it talks about a lot more than
stimming, but the process of becoming your true self as an Autistic
person. It’s a great book, written by an important Autistic voice, and
you owe it to yourself to read it. But for now, let me tell you
something important you may not hear anywhere else: you’re not stimming
*enough*<sup>17</sup>. First, that sounds like heresy since most of us
have been told, shown, hurt or threatened with violence for
stimming<sup>18,19</sup>. And yet again I say, we’re not stimming
enough. And, as with all things, it matters deeply what kind of stimming
you’re doing, and where. Because depending on who you are, and where you
live, stimming or the type of stimming you do can get you
killed<sup>19</sup>. I’m very nearly speaking from first-hand
experience.

Why then do I say that you’re not stimming nearly enough? Am I trying to
get you in trouble, hurt, or worse? Not at all. Let me explain. First,
the word *stimming* is perhaps a misnomer, or at minimum an incomplete
descriptor. To stim, or stimulate, is not all what we’re doing when we
stim: we are bringing our nervous system back into equilibrium or as a
biologist would say: *homeostasis*.

In acute stress response theory, we learn about fight-flight. In the
latter part of the 20<sup>th</sup> Century, freeze was added. So, ok,
fight-flight-freeze<sup>21</sup>. Then, someone added
*fawn*<sup>21</sup>, and I’ve also seen *fawn* referred to as “feign.”
To give in and go along with an abuser or abusive situation to minimize
the danger of the situation, is to fawn/feign (which might save your
life in the short run). But I digress, let’s go back to freeze. What
happens to a mind-body (person) if they aren’t aware or don’t feel the
adrenaline and cortisol coursing through their system<sup>22</sup>? This
condition happens with many Autistic people for all kinds of feelings,
and it’s called *alexithymia*, and there is evidence that it is
connected to a deficit in the inner sense of being called
interoception<sup>23</sup>. But whether you consciously freeze – or
freeze through alexithymia – that hormone-induced tension has to go
somewhere. And I mean **now**. It’s a biological imperative, akin to
exhaling carbon dioxide. *Sin qua non*. Without which, not. Stimming
gives this flood of bioelectrical-chemical energy a healthy way
out<sup>17</sup>. There is also growing evidence that it may also
trigger dopamine and endorphins, both of which feel pleasurable and may
be part of the phenomenon called "Autistic Joy.'' This needs to be
studied further to know for sure. But it’s not just Autistics.
Scientists see animals stimming all the time in and after stressful
situations<sup>25</sup>. Even non-Autistic people do it from time to
time, but it’s a matter of *degree*. But what happens when that distress
is not adequately processed out of your system? And what happens if the
flood of hormones like adrenaline and cortisol is somehow amplified?
What happens when both of those things happen *at the same time*?

We now know that Autistic brains are hyper-connected, which means lots
and lots of "extra" synapses<sup>50</sup>. There isn’t enough good
research out there to explain how this hyper-connected brain translates
neatly into a common set of inner experiences. But whether the
hyper-connection leads to hyper-active or hypo-active sensory
experiences, there is a need to better understand the relationship
between this and stimming. Meanwhile, people (meaning our loved ones and
others) wonder why Autistic people stim – but the problem is of course
not that people *wonder*, it's that *for decades professionals and
society have been trying to wipe out the behavior*. If we’re not
stimming, and/or we can’t stim fast enough to vent the sensory overload
we can become overwhelmed to the point of Autistic Meltdown, Shutdown
and Burnout<sup>51</sup>. I have a list of ideas for stimming through
the use of stim toys (for people of all ages!) in Section III.

A recent article<sup>29</sup> talked at length about Autistic Burnout,
Inertia, Meltdown, and Shutdown (BIMS), and why different approaches to
it are needed than the ones we have today, i.e., let people stim:

> “Their \[Autistic people’s\] whole-person descriptions stand in
> contrast to the more reductionist theorizations of dysregulation and
> behavioral responses *that are often seen in current literature and
> clinical approaches* \[italics mine\]. Current clinical approaches
> tend to focus on executive function, physiology or behavioral
> responses in isolation ([Hess et al.,
> 2008](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8595127/#B11)).
> Approaches such as auditory integration training, sensory integration,
> and cognitive behavioral modification, *aim to change autistic
> children’s intrinsic traits* \[italics mine\] in order for them to
> better fit into their environments ([Hess et al.,
> 2008](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8595127/#B11)).
> Instead, the descriptions from the children and youth in our study
> support [Leadbitter et al.
> (2021)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8595127/#B16)
> suggestion that a crucial element of advancing autistic research and
> clinical practice *is to understand autistic experiences as a way of
> being which is complex and dynamic* \[italics mine\].”

So, we stim when we’re overstimulated. What about when we’re
understimulated, at rest, or bored? The evidence is the Autistic brain
is still doing a lot of processing then too, even in the absence of
direct physical stimulation. So, we stim with any over or under
stimulation. Note that the overstimulation or understimulation may
*continue* in our minds and bodies long after the stimulus is removed.

So what *is* the Autistic brain doing when at rest/understimulated?
Processing memories? Trauma? Thoughts and feelings of being
overstimulated? There is evidence that for some brains, the imagined or
remembered experience activates parts of the brain as if you were
experiencing it in real life<sup>30</sup>. Some brains may not know the
difference between a thing that is remembered and a thing that is
happening<sup>30</sup>. We need more data to know for sure if this is
true for Autistic people. But it’s clear *something* extra is going on
in the Autistic brain, perhaps even at rest/while understimuated.
<span class="mark">We may be at rest, but we may not be *at
peace*</span>. Stimming helps any brain find peace and feelings of
well-being, but it may be pivotal for Autistic brains. To stim is to
approach equilibrium within one’s own nervous system. Many have referred
to this as an expression of *Autistic Joy*. It might be our primary
pathway to peace in this world. Or do you have another option here as
well? We’ll get to that.

So, if stimming is a path to Autistic Joy, then what is it when a person
can’t or has been taught not to stim? In a word, anxiety to the degree
of trauma. Anxiety has been identified, by the Mayo Clinic, as the root
of a number of physical symptoms that may be familiar to most of you:

1.  Feeling nervous, restless or tense

2.  Having a sense of impending danger, panic or doom

3.  Having an increased heart rate

4.  Breathing rapidly (hyperventilation)

5.  Sweating

6.  Trembling

7.  Feeling weak or tired

8.  Trouble concentrating or thinking about anything other than the
    present worry

9.  Having trouble sleeping

10. Experiencing gastrointestinal (GI) problems

11. Having difficulty controlling worry

12. Having the urge to avoid things that trigger anxiety

Like Cassandra, Autistic people know (approximately) what’s going to
happen as you go into a situation where we can’t stim, and yet we’re
locked into a future outcome we can’t stop. Actually, by legend,
Cassandra was gifted with the power of prophecy but then was cursed that
no one would believe her predictions<sup>31</sup>, which is often also
the case for Autistic people which compounds anxiety and finally trauma.
For Autistic people this is often labeled “social anxiety”<sup>32</sup>
which is anxiety/panic attacks about what *might* happen specifically in
current/upcoming social situations. Unlike the beliefs of health care
providers who may treat you for social anxiety, however, you may or may
not be "catastrophizing" in the sense that an NT person does. You may be
*remembering* the last several times it *did* happen, and you’re having
an adrenaline response to what’s *likely* about to happen – now you’re
much like Cassandra and people around you (including your therapist) may
not believe you or worse, gaslight you in a dozen ways.

But this is the point, when you’re finally in that overstimulated
situation and your adrenaline is pumping higher still, you might be
aware of it, and just as likely you might not through alexithymia. But
on some level, your body *is* aware<sup>32</sup>. Your body is
overstimulated, and if it’s high enough you can head into Autistic
Meltdown. <span class="mark">There is scientific evidence across the
animal kingdom to back it up, that "stimming" after adrenaline-inducing
events is not only common, it’s *necessary*</span>. I’d say if it’s safe
to do so, it’s necessary to do it *during* the event, too. And of
course, young Autistic people don’t need this advice: they just do it.
Often with abusive consequences. Afterwards, you may experience a type
of flashback or replay of the memories and intensely painful emotions
called Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)<sup>32</sup>. Don’t be
surprised if you want to stim, and if you want to or start stimming, if
it’s safe to do so, and you do it in a way that doesn’t physically hurt
you – go for it. Dance, flap your arms wide, whatever is safe to do and
feels good.

You, my fine Autistic reader, may have something in common with an
old-fashioned pressure cooker. But, just like researchers at UCSF
discovered with glycemic load, nature often packages the antidote with
the poison of under/overstimulation<sup>33</sup>. Case in point: an
apple. Poison being sugar in the apple, and natural fiber being the
antidote. The apple has both, so you eat it with virtually no glycemic
load, no poisonous effect through insulin spikes and therefore no fat
storage. This is well-described in a YouTube Video<sup>33</sup>, and
although a bit long, I promise you it’ll be worth your time. But the
analogy is this: yes, our brains are more hyper-connected than NT
brains, and the result may be like that of a pressure cooker. But, with
every pressure cooker made in the last century, they have a pressure
relief valve built in. Because the ones that didn’t often exploded
through overpressure, leaving a mess to clean up in the kitchen. The
ones that still don’t or have them disabled in real life are used today
in war as improvised explosive devices ("IEDs"). But the opposite is so
much better than an unexploded bomb. The opposite – *which is what we’re
all shooting for* – is the experience of joy, the true release to the
stressors experienced by an Autistic brain not allowed freedom in this
world as we know it now.

So, your stimming may or may not be *stimulating* us. It’s clearly
helping to *regulate* us, the opposite word being dysregulated. It’s a
safety valve. But, much more than that. It’s a way to unlock joy. And
without that joy you’re an IED: an explosion in slow-motion progress.
But, if you’ve gotten this far, you already know that. So, what? So,
what if you need to stim and you CAN’T stim. That’s the kind of
double-bind that will drive a person to drink. Or worse.
