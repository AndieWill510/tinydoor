## Chapter 23: Self-Stigma

> “There is no standard way of being, or knowing, or perceiving.
> Variation is of the essence. There are an infinite number of ways of
> being and doing and knowing and remembering. And there are an infinite
> number of ways of being healthy.”
>
> --Oliver Sacks

We’ve talked a lot about bullying, whether these are predatory or
parasitic (or both). We’ve talked about Autistiphobia, and why this more
accurately describes what we’re experiencing than “ableism.” Now,
whether you see these models and definitions as helpful or prefer the
Ableist/Internal Ableist Model, it really doesn’t matter in the end.
What causes Autistic people to internalize these harmful messages is
*less important than the fact that they often do*. If you are surrounded
by bullies/shunners/gaslighters/trolls, predators, parasites or just the
Ableist mindset — you will absorb it whether you want to or not! A big
part of staying out of Autistic Burnout and getting out of it if you are
in it, according to my friend Matthew Lawrence (@AutisticCoach\_) is
removing yourself from these sources of toxicity. Additionally, he would
add, you need to regularly be with Autistic people. But first things
first; you must remove the sources of toxicity driving your Autistic
Burn(out). It’s not enough to be around Autistic voices. The toxic
messages of Autistiphobia/Ableism, the environment you’re in and/or the
work you do that is harming you, must be reduced and/or eliminated.

Why? We are sponges, each of us. We absorb the material around us. If
the voices and messages we’re receiving from others is that we’re:

- Less-than as we are

- That we must change, learn “social skills,” etc.

- That we’re broken

- That being Autistic alone means we’re sick

These messages penetrate deeply and reinforce destructive beliefs and
behaviors. Make no mistake, *you are different* – and vive la
difference! However, depending on the environment you have *right now*,
you are more likely than not dis-abled by it. But you are not broken,
sick or wrong.

### The Power of Self-Stigma: Carrying Stigma with You

Your brain may not know that you are not broken, sick, or wrong —
especially if you’ve been living in a primarily Autistiphobic
environment for a long time, and/or the abuse you’ve suffered because of
being neurodivergent has been intense. This type of internalized
Autistiphobia has a name: *self*-*stigma*. Self-stigma refers to
negative beliefs that an individual holds about themselves due to their
condition or identity, often influenced by societal stereotypes and
prejudices³⁴. Self-stigma is very treatable, but it’s important that
once you’ve identified self-stigma that you begin treatment as soon as
possible – this is the abuser that is living “rent free” in your head.
There are a couple of therapy types that work well for self-stigma: DBT
and ACT. You could say that ACT may work better than DBT, but the most
important thing with any therapist is that they understand you and what
you’re going through. I’ve had very successful therapists who knew
nothing about Autism help me using ACT and DBT, but it’s critical that
they not gaslight or minimize what you’re going through. Focus on
specifically what is bothering you and how intensely, and less on
identifying this as Autistic or not. Labels, I’ve found, help less than
you might think in therapy. There’s also a big movement to
de-pathologize so stick with the symptoms and not the diagnoses or
disability labels. Compassion and understanding though is
non-negotiable. Hire your therapist slowly and fire them fast. You’re
not stuck with them if they’re not helping you.

While self-stigma is not specific to Autism, it is perhaps specifically
and intensely harmful to Autistic people. Self-stigma has several key
types, see if any of these resonate with you – if they do – there is a
strong likelihood you have self-stigma around your
Autism<sup>188,189,190,191,192</sup>:

- Alienation: Feeling isolated from others, inferior, or ashamed. Strong
  fear of losing friends without knowing why.

- Stereotype Endorsement: Applying negative stereotypes to oneself
  (e.g., believing that Autistic people are odd or weird or can't lead
  fulfilling lives).

- Discrimination Experience: Feeling discriminated against or
  patronized, leading to feelings of low self-worth or that our ideas or
  words don’t matter, or are overly intense or verbose.

- Social Withdrawal: Avoiding social interactions, including school,
  friends, family and work due to feeling like a burden or
  embarrassment.

And, just as there are common types of self-stigma, there are also
common self-stigma consequences, so see if any of this
resonates<sup>188,189,190,191,192</sup>:

- Barrier to Recovery: Self-stigma can hinder seeking treatment and
  self-care.

- Reduced Self-Esteem: It impacts self-worth and self-identity

- Increased Depression: The emotional toll can be greater than the
  disability itself.

- Self-Sabotage: Expecting failure and engaging in harmful behaviors.

And lest you think that your experience of self-stigma/how you feel as
an Autistic person is personal only to you, here are some of the
negative beliefs in self-stigma that Autistic people often experience
about ourselves<sup>193,194</sup>:

1.  Feelings of Isolation or Loneliness, combined with unworthiness:
    Difficulties in maintaining friendships and relationships can lead
    to feelings of isolation, loneliness and unworthiness

2.  Feeling 'Different' or 'Odd': Autistic individuals often perceive
    themselves as different in a defective way from their neurotypical
    peers, which impact our self-image.

3.  Low Self-Worth: Negative experiences, such as bullying, gaslighting
    and discrimination, may contribute to low self-worth and feelings of
    being a burden to society.

4.  Feeling Misunderstood: Autistic individuals may struggle to
    understand others and often feel misunderstood themselves.

5.  Challenges in Communication: Difficulties in expressing needs and
    perspectives so that neurotypical people understand leads to
    frustration and self-doubt.

6.  Anxiety and Sensory Overload: Anxiety related to sensory overload in
    public places can affect self-perception.

7.  Lack of Self-Identity: Some autistic individuals struggle with a
    solid sense of self-identity.

8.  Educational or Employment Failures: Past failures in education or
    work roles impact self-esteem and often lead to despair.

9.  Not Having Differences Recognized: Autistic differences may go
    unrecognized by others, leading to feelings of invisibility or being
    unimportant to society.

###  Recovering from Self-Stigma

OK, so that’s self-stigma – what’s important are some *proven therapies
for healing* from self-stigma<sup>195, 196</sup>:

1.  Group-Based Interventions: Most self-stigma interventions are
    group-based. They effectively reduce internalized stigma through
    psychoeducation, cognitive behavioral theory, and disclosure-focused
    approaches.

2.  Peer-Led Empowerment Work: Engaging with peers who share similar
    experiences can be empowering. Peer-led sessions provide support and
    strategies for dealing with stigma.

3.  Myth/Fact Challenges: Challenging stereotypes by distinguishing
    between myths and facts helps combat self-stigma. Recognize harmful
    stereotypes and replace them with accurate information.

4.  Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) has shown promise in addressing
    self-stigma, particularly when working with marginalized
    populations. Here are the key points<sup>197,198,199,200</sup>:

- DBT and Self-Stigma: While DBT was originally developed for conditions
  like borderline personality disorder (BPD) and self-harm behaviors, it
  has been found effective in treating other conditions, including
  self-stigma.

- Skills for Coping: DBT helps individuals develop concrete skills to
  cope with distress and challenging emotions. These skills include:

  - Mindfulness: Being present in the moment and understanding emotions.

  - Distress Tolerance: Reacting less intensely to distress.

  - Interpersonal Effectiveness: Improving personal interactions.

  - Emotional Regulation: Recognizing and controlling emotions.

  - LGBTQ+ Clients: DBT-based strategies can buffer clients from
    environmental invalidation related to sexual stigma. It enhances
    self-validation and helps affirm sexual orientation, gender identity
    and ultimately sexual health.
