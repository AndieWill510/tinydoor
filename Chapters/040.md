## Chapter 4: Sex-Positive Autism

It’s worth noting that as I write this, some believe that the sexual
expression of Autistic people is less than neurotypical people or
perhaps non-existent. The research does not support that conclusion
<span class="mark"><sup>cite</sup></span>. In fact, the opposite might
be true. A growing body of evidence supports the idea that the spectrum
of sexuality is at least as, if not more, pronounced amongst Autistic
people as it is amongst neurotypical people. If the spectrum of
sexuality is at least as great as neurotypical people, it stands to
reason that the rest of sexual expression follows suit.

We know that the spectrum of sexual expression in the lives of Autistic
people is more evenly distributed, or at least more recognized as evenly
distributed in the panoply of sexual expression. We cannot talk
effectively about sexuality without talking about concepts of gender
identity and sexual orientation and expression. We may one day come to
view the concept of gender identity the way many now understand race: an
artificial construct that bears little resemblance to the reality inside
people’s minds and bodies. Gender identity, rather than being binary,
also need not be cast in false-dualistic terms: “binary” vs.
“non-binary.” All of this might well be a contrivance. Now, are there
genitalia that map to male, female and intersex? Of course there are. In
the same way that there are darker-skinned and lighter-skinned people.
But is a darker-skinned person necessarily a member of the black “race”?
Is a lighter-skinned person a member of the white “race”? How dark must
one be before one is considered “black”? How light must one be before
considered “white”? I have had very olive-skinned Middle Eastern in-law
relatives with a straight face tell me they’re white – when clearly many
from a White Anglo Saxon Protestant background would not see them as
such. Do each act “white,” or “black” – and what does that even mean,
anyway? Clearly, this notion of race has been debunked but the false
words remain. Now, is there racism? Without a doubt. But am I of the
white race while my friend is of the black race? I don’t believe this
passes the laugh test. My friend is the target of racism while people
who look like me often cause them to suffer because of it. Every
scientific attempt to draw the distinctions between white and black
fails horrendously. It’s a pure fabrication, one of 19<sup>th</sup> and
20<sup>th</sup> Century pseudoscience
(<https://books.google.com/books?hl=en&lr=&id=cx6tDwAAQBAJ&oi=fnd&pg=PP1&dq=race+theory+pseudoscience&ots=DvKDblPY_N&sig=bdj6E60XxgB_Wngt17BRlc5MNGw>).

Autistic and other people are starting to raise similar issues with
gender. What does it mean when my genitals and other secondary sex
characteristics don’t match my lived experience relative to the
“typical” gender behavioral expectations? What does it mean when the
stereotypical behavior of gender enforced upon us is so offensive as to
be *a source* of severe mental illness? Note that I said it was a source
of mental illness, not the fact itself. It is *the experience of having
to live under a regime of proscribed sexual gender* places such stress
upon us as to drive us to a place of mental and physical unwellness.
<span class="mark">There is nothing mentally ill about a lived
experience of identity</span>.

We say intersex to describe primary and secondary sex characteristics,
either born or adopted, to be the middle-ground. Is it? Can we be sure?
What would it mean if we are all in some very real sense gender fluid?
Many Autistic people are raising these very important questions, if for
no one else themselves. They are important because as the evidence
mounts, that we have been driving a set of social behaviors upon far too
many that is driving them into states of physical and mental illness.
“Torture” would be another word that may fit here. Gender, like race, is
pseudoscience and the results may come to be seen as harmful in the
21<sup>st</sup> Century as Eugenics was in the 20<sup>th</sup> Century.
Laws are being passed right now, and the jury is still out. Watch this
space.

So that’s sexual gender (not gender identity), perhaps as scientifically
bankrupt as race. What about sexual orientation? Well, we’ve been told
that we have straight, gay, bisexual and trans-sexual. Is that it? What
about asexual? What about polyamorous? What about pansexuality? These
are serious questions for serious people. Many, who have grown
accustomed to having high walls around sexual orientation will throw up
defenses and claim that just *this* is ok, and *that* is not. Says who?
There is orientation and then there is expression, and here is where
things get really complicated.

Certainly, there are power imbalances between old and young, especially
children. We have laws to protect sexual predation in most civilized
countries. Ironically some of the same countries that fail to protect
children (“child marriage,” pedophilia) also are the worst offenders
against differences in sexual gender and sexual orientation. We act as
if there are natural laws, and in some cases there actually are human
laws, that protect society from anything that doesn’t fall into the
category of one man, one woman. What about serial monogamy? My mother
was married four times at last count. Why isn’t that illegal? It’s not
illegal because the entire concept of agency <span class="mark">for
some</span> is sacrosanct. We are simply hyper-judicious in our
application of protecting the definitional walls of that agency.
Autistic people are raising the flag. Many flags, of many colors,
actually. We are not all what we think we are. It’s time we stopped the
hypocrisy and recognize that we damage everyone when we prevent the
consenting expression of legal-age sexuality to legal-age sexuality by
anyone to anyone.

But sexual expression is squelched far too young and by far too many. We
are sexual creatures. Nature made us that way, even if in a spectrum of
gender, identity and expression. There is how I feel about myself
sexually and how I approach the world (gender), there is what I’m
attracted to in a sexual sense (orientation) and then there is sexual
expression. These things do not, and in the Autistic community many
times do not, neatly line up. And if they do, there are no hard-and-fast
rules that they need to stay lined up. Why do I need to choose? I’m a
cis male heterosexual in heterosexual relationship for my entire life
because you (some other person or entity) need me to be? Why is that,
exactly? To raise children? I did that. What if I don’t want to do that,
or if I want to with a gender not my own, or a sexual orientation or
expression not my own? Autistic people don’t always know, and don’t
always understand why this must be – regardless of the number of times
it's explained to them. Why do we not understand it? Because *it doesn’t
make any sense: it’s arbitrary*. Like race or gender. We made it up
because some people were uncomfortable with the reality: animals are not
easily divided, categorized or quantified. The first gay sex I ever saw
was on a ranch in Texas while doing the most heteronormative thing a 15
year old boy could do in Texas: hunting deer. As we were driving onto
the hundred-odd acre ranch, I saw two bulls mating in a field, like it
was just an everyday occurrence. Because it was. That’ll make you think
for a minute, and if you’re Autistic a lot more than a minute. And I
don’t just mean Autistic people, I mean all of us. I believe the ground
truth is that Autistic people just see what Masters and Johnson and the
Hite Report and Nancy Friday and countless others have reported over the
decades: that we are a spectrum. What Oliver Sacks said, as he did so
many other things, so well: “There is no standard way of being, knowing
or perceiving…*variation is of the essence* \[italics mine\]. There are
infinite number of ways of being and doing and knowing and remembering.”
(https://www.youtube.com/shorts/jr78zQ3GFi8)

Infinite.

The most important book on Autistic sexuality wasn’t written for
Autistic people; it was written for women. Emily Nagoski’s best-selling
book “Come As You Are: The Surprising New Science that Will Transform
Your Sex Life”<sup>cite</sup> may have a superfluous word in the title:
“Sex.” Her book will transform your life, especially if applied to the
lives of Autistic people. In it are many well-researched points but here
are several of the highlights:

1.  Masturbation is as healthy and necessary as breathing

2.  Understanding your body fully and without shame, including but not
    exclusive to the sex organs, is a pathway to whole-body wellness

3.  There is an accelerator and braking system in sexuality,
    <span class="mark">one that must be honored</span>, that may point
    towards a full-body realization of human health

4.  Failure to understand and know ourselves fully is a source of stress
    and self-esteem issues

5.  When we’re stressed, we close ourselves off from our partners (and
    ourselves?), negatively impacting intimacy and desire for sexual
    contact

6.  We must take deliberate steps to complete the stress cycle, ridding
    ourselves of adrenaline and cortisol; this points to exercise,
    stimming and masturbation but possibly other modalities such as EMDR
    and CBT therapies

7.  She speaks at length about attachment styles, which while outside
    the context of this book are absolutely relevant to Autistic people
    who attach in ways that are almost by definition different than
    neurotypical people

So, what is important to know about Autistic Sexuality?

1.  A healthy form of stimming is sexual release, i.e., orgasm, as a
    pathway to Autistic Joy

2.  It’s important to recognize that neurotypical society has
    significant stigma associated with sexual pleasure, perhaps
    especially around self-pleasure or masturbation

3.  Obviously, this type of stimming, more than any other stimming, must
    be done in a way that is private and does not cause
    stigmatization<sup>204,205</sup>

4.  Whether done with a partner or by yourself, it’s healthy for
    Autistic people to regularly achieve orgasm as it releases oxytocin
    into the bloodstream<sup>202</sup> which is known to alleviate
    stress (in part by reducing cortisol<sup>203</sup>) – something all
    of us but perhaps especially Autistic people have too much

5.  People who dissuade you from pursuing healthy sexual relationships,
    including regular self-pleasure, should be seen as any other type of
    environmental pressure that is unhealthy – and time with them should
    be pruned

6.  Sex education, starting in pre-adolescence and beyond especially
    around healthy relationship boundaries, is critical for Autistic
    people as they have a higher-than-normal likelihood of sexual
    assault or abuse<sup>205</sup>

7.  A great resource for learning the boundaries of healthy
    relationships for Autistic people is the series of podcasts here:
    <https://researchautism.org/self-advocates/sex-ed-for-self-advocates/sex-ed-for-self-advocates-podcast/>
