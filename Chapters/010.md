## Chapter 1: Introduction

"We are the ones we’ve been waiting for."

June Jordan

I’m glad you’re here!

I’m going to share with you what worked for me as I made the shift from
Autistic childhood into adulthood. By sharing with you my story it might
remind you of your own successes. I want to help you build upon those
successes as you build new ones.

And, as we build these successes together, we will also face a harsh but
perhaps familiar truth: we face a pandemic in the Autistic community
that isn’t Autism. We’re going to talk more about the pandemic causing
premature illness and even death, but more importantly we’re going to
talk about the solutions which are far easier than many people might
think.

If you’re Autistic, like me, you may have had a tough childhood. And, as
for many Autistic people, it was the transition to adulthood where we
trade one set of tough circumstances for another set: the “Autistic
cliff.” Childhood medical “treatments” and/or social pressures in school
give way to financial struggles and more complex relationships. Whether
you’re on your way through this cliff or well on the other side of it,
know this: although the challenges you face seem impossible, they have
simple fixes. We’ll walk through them together, and bit by bit life can
and will start to get easier. We’ve all seen too much of the
“healthcare” offered (or not) to Autistic people and I believe those
“solutions” to be part of the problem in this pandemic.

To caretakers/parents: this book, while primarily written for the
well-being of Autistic people, is also a path to your well-being. As
caretakers of Autistic people, you have different challenges, however
the biggest challenge you face is not what it appears to be. Autistic
behavior distracts from the pandemic facing Autistic people. You can
help, and you can make a difference! Simply by switching your mindset
from isolated parents/caregivers to being one of a team of people who
love Autistic people just as they are. This includes teaming up with
Autism-affirming coaches, health advocates and, yes, Autistic Adults
like me who have walked this path successfully. I promise it’s not
complicated or hard, it just requires thinking and acting in a slightly
different way. But this will require less effort, provide greater
benefits, and finally it will be cheaper financially. We’ll get to all
of that in this book.

Through techniques I’ll teach you in this book, as an Autistic person, I
grew healthier day by day. And, as a dad with two healthy adult
children, many of these techniques that kept me safe, keep them safe.
These techniques help them thrive as they make their own transitions
into their adult years. I did not do this without a lot of help, not
least of which a community that was Autistic affirming. Living as an
Autistic person is one of the most difficult challenges humans have ever
faced, bar none. Together, with Autistic guidance backed with science
and community resources we can trust, I believe that not only can
Autistic people and their loved ones rise to these challenges…but
succeed and thrive!

But let me discuss the elephant in the room: Autistic behavior is a
controversial but dangerously distracting area of focus. If you hear
nothing else, hear this: making Autistic people fit seamlessly into
society is the problem, not the solution. This problem results in health
risks, that originate within medical practices, that we all can prevent.
This book is about focusing on solutions that anyone can achieve. But it
will take a shift in thinking, and sometimes that can be hard. I find it
worth it, and I think you will, too. Because your health and well-being
matter. All our well-being matters.

So, the question arises: if this is so easy, why hasn’t it been done
yet? First, the problems are presented to us in the wrong order by
people believed to be authorities. We’ve been taught to focus on
Autistic behavior as if it were a problem, when lack of compassion for
daily stressors is the problem. Next, these stressors are simple but
slightly different depending on the Autistic person. Finally, it’s
critical that we all change our perspective from fixing a problem that
doesn’t exist (behavior of Autistic people) to being more compassionate
about the problems that *do* exist (ableism). Remember we once thought
ourselves the center of the solar system: the sun and all the planets
revolved around us. That was at the time scientific fact, and to argue
otherwise was to invite ridicule, exclusion, or worse! But, in time we
learned earth wasn't the center of the solar system, and the sun wasn’t
revolving around us. It was the other way around; it just required a
slight shift in perspective. Evidence matters, and as we saw with the
solar system, the right kind of evidence matters more than the amount of
evidence. Quality of evidence matters as does quantity.

I want to give you a real-world example of how we get it wrong with
understanding Autistic health issues. Imagine this. If social pressure
was causing early death, no matter how the life ended, it would never
strictly speaking be called self-harm — or parental neglect. It would be
another word. Many words come to mind, some of them inflammatory. Words
matter, and inflammatory words aren’t helpful. So, instead I’m going to
invite you to start using a phrase I’ll be using throughout the book.
From here forward self-harm resulting in death will be an acronym from a
new phrase "man-made death" — MD. Who commits MD (one of the top three
causes of Autistic death) isn’t helpful. Why? Fixing blame, especially
on the victim, distracts us from a deeper truth. Several African
proverbs state that the raising of a child takes more than the household
of the parent(s). Colloquially, “it takes a village.” I also believe it
takes more than a single household to keep children alive and healthy,
and this is especially true for Autistic children. When we say MD, we
side-step the cycle of blame and shame (and ultimately hate and
prejudice), which blinds us to where we need to focus, which is
community-based prevention and harm reduction. Harm reduction, when
taken in a community-based approach, is remarkably effective at
preventing this kind of death for not only Autistic people, but
everyone. There is cause for hope: *there is another option*.

And as I mentioned, MD is one of the three leading causes of death.
Research shows the other two are in some cases easier, cheaper and
faster to solve. You don’t need to wait years to make a difference in
your Autistic life, or the life of your Autistic loved one. It’s just a
small shift in where we focus our attention to realize these life-saving
benefits. This approach is one we will come back to again and again:
*you have another option.*

Building upon great anthropology work by Gianni Pes and Michel Poulain,
Dan Buettner of National Geographic began to expand the search for
long-lived and healthy people living in so-called "Blue Zones." These
people weren’t only living longer (some well into their 100s), but they
also lived relatively disease-free well into old age from heart disease,
cancer and Alzheimer’s. Seemingly through no fault of their own, but
rather from the habits built into them and their caretakers over a
lifetime by their community. This wasn’t simply luck of geographical
birth; no these were pockets of community and culture that stood apart
from their neighbors and country as outliers. What if the opposite were
true? What if there were a group of people, through no fault of their
own, lived to be half the age of the Blue Zone again from the habits
built into them and their caretakers over a lifetime by their community?
I wouldn’t call these "Red Zones," although research shows there are
regions where this problem is much, much worse than others. Regardless,
I see the same opportunity Buettner saw: what if we could apply the
lessons of the Blue Zones to a large community of people who live far
shorter and more troubled lives? Could we add 5 years to an Autistic
life? 10? How about 30? I think it’s worth it. And if you’re reading
this book, I think you might, too.
