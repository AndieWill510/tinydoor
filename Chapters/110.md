## Chapter 11: You’re Not Sick, But Your Environment Is

> “The sick individual finds himself at home with all other similarly
> sick individuals. The whole culture is geared to this kind of
> pathology. The result is that the average individual does not
> experience the separateness and isolation the fully schizophrenic
> person feels. He feels at ease among those who suffer from the same
> deformation; in fact, it is the fully sane person who feels isolated
> in the insane \[sic\] society - and he may suffer so much from the
> incapacity to communicate that it is he who may become psychotic
> \[sic\].”  
> ― **Erich Fromm, [The Anatomy of Human
> Destructiveness](https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/3235116)**

Most of us have been taught not to use words like “insane” and
“psychotic” in the 21<sup>st</sup> Century because they have been used
against people with mental illness and neurodivergent people. But when
you have a civilization like ours, it's tough to avoid these hurtful
words as Erich Fromm did in his tour-de-force work on why people
physically harm one another. In his view, the need for drama and
excitement – or the lack thereof – is the primary driver for violence.
He fact, talked about “mass media” being a junk-food substitute for that
human need for drama and excitement, where it feels like the need is
satisfied but in fact, it’s not. It builds and builds in a hydraulic
model until it’s released through violence. It is the junk-food nature
of the media filling this drive that spawns more violence than the
absence of drama and excitement alone, according to Fromm, since we
think we’re satisfying the need but in fact we’re frustrating it
building pressure in a hydraulic model. And if media in Fromm’s time did
this, social media in our time hypercharges it. Haven’t we seen an
uptick in violence, especially acts of terrorism/mass shootings? Do we a
global culture that generates such activity “mentally ill?” Or just
sick? And what happens to people seeking refuge, solace and sanity from
it all as many Autistic people do? Who, then, is well-adjusted? Who may
be called truly sick? And what happens when you take a process like ABA
or informal social reinforcement (bullying, really) to make essentially
healthy people “fit” into a society that is clearly unwell?

Researcher, and six-time \#1 New York Times bestselling author, Brené
Brown came to a similar society-wide conclusion with a slightly
different perspective: shame. Dr. Brown distinguishes between guilt and
shame<sup>156</sup>. Guilt is “I did bad,” while shame is “I am bad.” It
is shame that is at the heart of the anatomy of a different kind of
destructiveness that ails our Global Civilization: blame leads to shame
leads to rage<sup>157</sup>. It’s infectious and pervasive and is
absolutely the kind of citizen-on-citizen behavioral reinforcement that
Ivar Lovaas would have recognized as ideal and a pathway to utopia. I
think a growing number of us are seeing that this blame-shame-rage
paradigm is deeply pathological and root cause to several societal ills.
And this leads right up to the proverbial Autistic doorstep.

I’ll tell you something that all of you already know: NT people are, by
and large, “ashamed” of Autistic people<sup>158</sup>. The stimming, the
social awkwardness, the inability to “hold down a normal job,” etc. But
since language matters, let’s really focus on this a bit. If you adhere
to Dr. Brown’s model of blame-shame-rage, and understand that guilt is
about the act and shame is about the person, what does it mean to be
“ashamed of someone else?” Does that phrase really make sense? Or is
that a form of projection? When people feel something negative in
themselves, it’s not uncommon to “project” those feelings by ascribing
them to another person<sup>159</sup>. “I’m not being defensive, you
are.” And so, it goes. It’s at the heart of the phenomenon known as
scapegoating. We heap our ills, our guilt, our shame onto another and
then ride that “goat” (scape*goat*) out of town literally or
figuratively so we don’t have to deal with our own internal
demons<sup>160</sup>. Are NT parents, other loved ones, friends,
acquaintances “ashamed of” Autistic people and their behaviors? Or are
they reminded of the shame they carry themselves? Reminded of their own
vulnerability? And like the townspeople of olden times, heap the ills of
the village onto a goat and ride it out of town. “I’m not ashamed, I’m
ashamed *of you*.” Problem solved, or so it might seem, through the
power of projection and scapegoating. Of course, the problem isn’t
solved, it’s transferred.

But why would Autistic behavior bring up feelings of shame in, for
example, a parent? They certainly did a thing, had a child, so guilt
would be logically consistent if they felt like they *did* something bad
in having a child with a disability. But that’s not it – not exactly.
Through Neurotypical peer pressure – from a hundred “well-meaning”
family, friends and strangers – they are made to feel *not that they did
bad*. They are made to feel that they, the parents, *are bad*. And it
logically follows that one bad person makes another bad person. That’s
what this is all about: shame that Autistic people, through no fault of
their own, are causing NT people to confront within themselves. And
remember this is a blame-shame-rage cycle. Someone, something, must be
blamed. So, what gets blamed, how does this play out in practice:

- Genetics (of course, this means you as the parent are literally “to
  blame” even though you as the parent had no choice in your own
  genetics...)

- The “wrong” vaccine or set of vaccines, together

- Environmental pollution

- Neglectful, so-called “refrigerator” parenting

The desperate rooting around for a scapegoat, by the “well intended,” to
release the feelings of shame in themselves. Then the blame and shame
are heaped upon the Autistic child in a hundred different ways over
thousands of days. Then, as surely as night follows day, the rage wells
up and dysregulation gets worse.

We see the following actions in service of stopping the feelings of
shame in NT people against Autistic people:

- Bullying

- Gaslighting

- Physical Abuse

- ABA indoctrination (which includes all of the above)

- Eugenics

What if we just dealt with the shame head-on? What if, in the spirit of
Erich Fromm’s seminal work, we took our considerable powers of analysis
and focused on the root cause of this destructiveness? Autistic people
aren’t innately sick. And we’re certainly not people of which “to be
ashamed.” People can’t, through the way they naturally act and react,
make other people feel ashamed: as Brené Brown proved, shame is
something an individual has inside of them because of society’s
behavioral reinforcement<sup>156</sup>. Why? How? Because of
camouflaging, or what is known in the Autistic Community as
“masking<sup>161</sup>.” But *NT* camouflaging *is an attempt to meet
impossible societal standards*<sup>156</sup>. All of Asia has it. All of
Europe has it. The US has it. France, UK, all of Europe has it. Africa
has it too, all of it. Latin America has it. Every region is slightly
different, but the standards are universally impossible and therefore
generate blame-shame-rage. We can say that NT camouflage is an attempt
to meet “impossible societal standards,” or we can say that NT
camouflage is a desperate, lifelong effort to achieve *the only option
presented*.

When I worked for Toyota Corporation, I was instructed in *kaizen*
classes in a process called “The Five Whys.” The process is used when
you need to know the root cause of a problem or challenge, so you can
apply countermeasures<sup>162</sup>. You start with what is called the
“prioritized problem at the point of occurrence,” which is a fancy
phrase for we found out where this happens, and where it’s causing the
biggest issue. To better understand why NT people react like they do,
let’s use NT Ableist reactions to Autistic behavior as the “prioritized
problem at the point of occurrence.” And we’ll ask five why questions to
drive down to root cause and derive countermeasures.

Ready? Here we go:

- “The behavior of Autistic People (e.g., stimming) must be stopped.”
  Why?

- “It’s embarrassing to see people behave like that.” Why?

- “Because it makes people feel ashamed of it.” Why?

- Because it reminds people of their own vulnerability. Why?

- Because if I’m vulnerable, I won’t be accepted. Why?

- Because if I’m vulnerable, I’ll never feel loved, included, or belong
  and those feelings are key to my well-being/survival.

This exercise of the Five Whys technique speaks to a very powerful
dynamic where vulnerability and fear of rejection are driving NT
behavior towards Autistic people in the process known as Ableism. It
speaks powerfully to the underlying NT causes of why Autistic behavior
might be felt as necessary to change. Of course, it’s not necessary. But
it helps to explain why it might *feel* necessary. Again, we need more
data to confirm the findings of this technique, but this is a theory
that fits the facts.

Let’s go one more round of “Five Whys,” taking the last step and feeding
it into a new round, because on very complex problems sometimes that’s
required:

- “If I am \[as a NT person\] vulnerable, I’ll never feel loved,
  included, or belong.” Why?

- “Because, as I am \[a NT person\], I don’t fit into society.” Why?

- Because when I \[an NT person\] was younger, I got bullied (or *I saw
  someone else get bullied*). Why?

- Because bullies didn’t like me for who I am. Why?

- Because bullies were ashamed of me. Why?

- Because the bullies may have been bullied when they were
  younger<sup>163</sup> and blaming and shaming and projecting rage onto
  others (bullying) is a way to discharge some of the internalized shame
  making the original bully feel better<sup>159</sup>.

This multi-round analysis using the Five Whys is, if not the origin
story of bullied Autistic people, it is close enough to it. The real
question, the underlying question and possibly a countermeasure, is:

> Why are there impossibly high social standards?

Shifting gears, we might think, “oh this is just bullying, how harmful
can it be?” The answer is one of degrees. If bullying is minimal and it
doesn’t go on too long (depending on the person), it might have little
to no impact. For others, it might be more intense and have a larger
impact. But the evidence that sustained bullying introduces blame, shame
and often rage is well-understood to be true.

Now, this is for NTs. They are shamed into behaving in a certain way,
and that breaks neatly along gender lines. For NT women in the US, they
are likely to camouflage as<sup>164</sup>:

- nice 

- thin 

- modest

- resources for appearance

For NT men in the US, they are likely to camouflage with<sup>165</sup>:

- emotional control 

- primacy of work 

- pursuit of status 

- violence

People who don’t fit neatly into these stereotyped behaviors, especially
in childhood, are likely to experience shaming<sup>156</sup>. They are
not one of the “in crowd.” The more intense the bullying, the more
likely it is that the bully will bully others. Bullies are made, not
born. But this isn’t the biggest challenge.

Bullies in a sense enforce these impossible social standards, and it
doesn’t matter what the gender or neurotype. The biggest challenge in
changing these social stereotypes is that, to follow them means:

- acceptance

- feelings of belonging

- love

- friendships

- employment opportunities

- recreation and vacation opportunities

- social status

...and a whole host of other benefits to the individual that are very
positive and very powerful. There is both positive reinforcement and
negative feedback to keep everyone in line. But make no mistake. These
stereotyped behaviors are, at their core, camouflage. They are not
authentic to the individual. And everyone is highly motivated, if even
on a subconscious level, to either demonstrate or be the enforcers of
this behavior on those least likely to comply: the young, fragile and
disabled. Rare is the person who will flaunt social convention, and
these people often pay a heavy price, sometimes quite literally and
economically.

So, what is the problem when it comes to Autistic people? Well,
two-fold:

1.  NT camouflaged behavior *is inauthentic* to an NT person’s
    character/values

2.  Autistic people are in turn camouflaging (masking) to imitate
    inauthentic social behaviors

I believe that this is the core of some of the pressure that we’ll see
in Autistic meltdown, shutdown and burnout. Autistic people aren’t being
bullied into imitating authentic behavior. Autistic people, typically
children, <span class="mark">are being judged and bullied for failing to
imitate *imitation – and this is where things go from cruel to
diabolical*</span>. I believe that the pressure that builds within
Autistic people through masking, is found in the failure to imitate a
social *fraud*. The fraud, originating from bullying in their own NT
childhood, camouflaging was *the only option presented*.
