## Chapter 22: A Well-Regulated Autistic Mind-Body

I want to start to think about the opposite of dysregulation:
regulation, or as biologists call it: homeostasis. Recall that the
underlying driver of SOURCE is to allow people to reach homeostasis, but
because of what SOURCE is, it makes homeostasis (almost) unattainable.
SOURCE makes homeostasis impossible for Autistic people. So, what then
are we to do about it? I also want to reintroduce a graphic presented
earlier in the book, to think about the types of activities needed to
achieve better Autistic well-being. If you recall, this was the
triquetra or Celtic Knot, with Community at the center. I will add a few
more elements that I feel, considering the number of dysregulations and
antagonists we just discussed, are important to consider:

Let’s start with the outer ring, at the top, with the arrows moving away
from the center. There is a misconception almost universally accepted
that Autistic people should spend time around everyone else *just like
everyone else*. Moreover, that Autistic people should be able to spend
*the same amount of time* around everyone else, just like everyone else.
But what if that time spent simply isn’t worth it? What if that time
spent with everyone else is just plain unhealthy for us? A recent report
presented data that 94% of Autistic people are bullied<sup>201</sup>.
There are three kinds of people, and I won’t even call them neurotypical
because some could be Autistic: bullies, shunners and – the people who
deny the impact of the first two – gaslighters. For a happier life, I
believe we need to spend a decreasing number of hours every day with all
of these types of people. Remember, they’re not sick, and they won’t
change no matter how much you want them to or wish they would. There are
segments earlier in the book that speak to how to identify bullies, but
here’s the quick guide:

> “First, it involves a fundamental imbalance of power between
> perpetrator and victim. Second, acts of bullying are intended to cause
> harm. Third, bullying is repeated over time.”

In short, bullies have a position of power (e.g., are not disabled) and
intend to harm you over a sustained period of time.

Gaslighters:

1.  A form of manipulation in which a person covertly sows seeds of
    doubt in an Autistic person, making them question their own memory,
    perception, or judgment<sup>184</sup>.

2.  A gaslighter can be a negative manager, a scheming coworker, or a
    prejudiced workgroup. It can also occur in relationships, where one
    person convinces their target that they’re remembering things wrong
    or that they’re misinterpreting events<sup>184</sup>.

3.  The victim feels anxious and doubtful about their own feelings,
    memories, and thoughts – and often feel like they are always on the
    wrong side of things and need to apologize<sup>185</sup>.

4.  Victims are deliberately and systematically fed false information
    that leads them to question what they know to be true about
    themselves<sup>184</sup>. They may end up doubting their memory,
    their perception, and even their sanity.

5.  A gaslighter will initially lie about simple things, but the volume
    of misinformation soon grows, and the gaslighter may accuse the
    victim of lying if they question the narrative<sup>184</sup>.

For more gaslighting catchphrases, see the section on Gaslighting
earlier in the book.

Shunners

People shun other people for a variety of reasons. It’s an old word, but
per the Oxford English Dictionary:

> “shun, v.: To avoid (in modern prose use always to avoid persistently
> or habitually) from repugnance, fear, or...With object a person, his
> or her company, etc.”

For Autistic people, it is common for us to try harder and harder to win
favor with people who exclude (shun) us from their conversations or
plans. It sucks to be on the outside, and there is something within a
lot of Autistic people that think if they just try harder, mask more,
talk and stim less they will be accepted. Note that this happens a lot
online with “trolls” in social media who intentionally suck you into
emotional conversations only to make you feel like you’re the outsider
with the wrong point of view – *if only you try harder*, you’ll convince
them of your point of view. It’s painful, but let’s face facts: every
hour you stop trying to “fit in” with bullying/shunning/gaslighting
crowds means you open an hour for another group that *will* care about
you. Let’s focus instead on finding you a group that is Actually
Autistic and affirms who you are, as you are, every time (or nearly
every time). It’s very important that you conserve your energy (spoons),
*everywhere you possibly can*. Bullies/shunners/gaslighters/trolls are a
black hole of spoons. Also, no lasting good or justice will come of it.

Whether bullies, shunners, gaslighters or trolls, the important lesson
is to find ways and reasons to spend less and less time with them. You
cannot win, whether in an argument sense, or in a social justice sense.
The only justice is the adage: “the best revenge is living well \[and
far away from them\].” It may take creativity and time to get away from
these people, especially if you live in the same house. Do all that you
can to get away from these energy-draining people; your short and
long-term health depends upon you getting away from these people. It may
be natural to ask: won’t that mean I’m more alone or isolated? At first,
likely yes. I’d argue that you, always and forever, are safest with you
than anyone else: this is the core of the Positive Mental Attitude. And
start choosing to love yourself: not the emotion, the act of little acts
of self-care/loving kindness (Metta). The goal though is making sure you
are building, slowly by slowly, the inner **Community** “triquetra” at
the same time.

For isolation, the solution is simple: seek out Autistic spaces and
spend more time with people who “get you.” All of this gives you,
*almost for free,* a reduction in **<u>self-stigma</u>** which is not
your fault but very dangerous.

All arrows on the outside pushing in (or even pushing out) should be
encouraging you to spend more time learning and taking advantage of:

- Medicine (Epilepsy, ADHD, Bipolar, Schizophrenia)

- Nutrition

- Activity

- Volunteering

- Recreation

- Rest

Then the rings pushing in towards these activities are:

- Autistic Coaching (by Autistic/ADHD coaches!)

  - These people are licensed, and ideally you get one that is
    themselves Autistic or ADHD.

  - They work with young(er) and old(er) adults alike.

  - Start with

- Autistic-Affirming Therapy

  - It’s great to get therapists that understand what you as an Autistic
    person are going through. Here are a couple of great resources for
    therapists who are in your area/time zone and are available via
    telehealth. Autistic people carry a lot of stress, and some of us
    have other issues like bipolar and other severe cognitive/emotional
    issues to work through. Here are some good places to start.

    - https://www.ndtherapists.com

    - <https://neurodivergentpractioners.org>

  - I’m a fan of DBT and ACT for everyday issues, as well because each
    fold in elements of mindfulness and values, respectively. See if
    your Autistic-affirming therapist practices these techniques or can
    guide you to someone who does. It can go in parallel to other
    therapies.

  - I’m a proponent of, and there is a lot of good evidence for, EMDR
    for healing trauma – even C-PTSD<sup>186</sup>. A great EMDR
    therapist will be great with trauma, full stop. I’ve never had a
    great EMDR therapist dedicated to Autism, but you can check with
    your Autistic-affirming therapist or
    <https://www.emdria.org/find-an-emdr-therapist/>

  - Somatic Therapy works for many people, me included. You can check
    with your Autistic-affirming therapist to see if they know of a
    great practitioner in your area.

- Ritual/routine

  - I’ve been told by many professionals to keep the same sleep time and
    wake schedule. If you do nothing else, I invite you to make this
    part of your routine. It’s hard for many, but make it a goal, not a
    must-do every night. Look for things making it hard for you to sleep
    and work with your Autistic coach/therapist to prioritize this in
    your work together.

  - Areas of Concentration (AOC, so-called “special interests”) can be a
    form of ritual. Continue your AOC practice, and if you can set aside
    time each day at the same time every day to do this.

  - Stimming is for many random. Some stims, if they are safe to do, can
    become part of your routine. It’s nature’s built-in way for you to
    let off steam and regulate your system. Go for it!

- Meditation

  - AOC’s are a form of meditation, absolutely. Continue your practice
    and remember that you likely need this to regulate yourself. But,
    maybe set a timer? Too much of a good thing isn’t a good thing. I’m
    not here to say how much is too much. You might have to tune this to
    your life.

  - Consider joining a meditation group that is Autistic-friendly. There
    are Buddhist centers (often called “sanghas”) that welcome
    newcomers. Buddhist meditation practices that focus on mindfulness
    help you alleviate masking symptoms and uncover (unmask) your needs.

- Medication:

  - Are your meds working well?

  - If not, does your doctor know they aren’t?

  - Have they made changes if so?

  - If they haven’t made changes, especially because they aren’t
    listening to you or worse are gaslighting you, *is it time to fire
    your doctor*?

  - Make sure you have a great doctor (Psychiatrist), who affirms your
    Autism and understands the pressures/stressors. Work with your
    Autism-affirming therapist to find and the partner with a great one.
    A solid therapist won’t steer you away from meds especially if you
    need them.

- Better Sensory Diet

  - We’ve talked about doing regular sensory profiles:

    - https://www.sensooli.com (free and helpful)

    - <https://www.sensoryintelligence.com/> (inexpensive for most,
      worth every penny)

  - Once you have a sensory profile, it’s time to tune your environment

    - You may need light-sensitive glasses

    - You might need ceramic-silicone ear plugs

    - You might need stim toys

    - You might need to adjust the light where you work/play

    - You might need to adjust the sound where you work/play (TV too
      loud? Other annoyances?)

  - This will likely take work on your part. It may (likely will) cost

In conversations with my friend Matthew, we’ve talked about the fact
that Sensory Integration issues drive Emotional Dysregulation and
vice-versa. I’ve experienced this and the data supports it. We also
discussed the interconnected web of dysregulations that can happen,
beyond sensory and emotional dysregulation. But what about the Autistic
Protective Mimicry Cycle?

To a NT person, bullying looks the same both in appearance and impact to
NTs and Autistics alike. A bully to an NT person is essentially a same
species predator, if so in everything but name. So, NT people
(especially loved ones of an Autistic person) think it’s the same
experience *with the same remedy* for a bullied Autistic person. It
isn’t, not psychologically, not socially and certainly not
physiologically. Bullying through the Fear Cycle of an Autistic person
has the impact of a sustained, prolonged, and intense predator-parasite
hybrid attack. It’s an attack, yes. But not (just) like a physical
attack. It’s more like a parasitic infection, wearing you down, making
you sicker and sicker. The gaslighting we receive through the Grief
Cycle, and evolutionary instincts drive us towards a (predatory-only)
Protective Mimicry Cycle. That is the same. But the way Protective
Mimicry seems to behave in the Autistic person, once it becomes clear it
isn’t working, goes into overdrive. Protective Mimicry becomes
Destructive. Moreover, the body starts to react as if there is a
parasitic infection. Because, for all intents and purposes, there is
one.

Protective mimicry evolved to work against predators but not against
parasites. Protective mimicry in the Autistic person isn’t protective or
even useless/neutral. It appears to accelerate parasitic damage, almost
like an autoimmune disease. In this way we can call typical Autistic
responses to the Stigma and Grief Cycles: Autistic Predator-Parasite
Defense Model. Think this overreaction of self-damage can’t happen in
the human body? It did. It does. During the 1918 “Spanish” Influenza and
the more recent COVID-19 outbreaks, many people died. It’s believed that
the cause of a lot of people dying was a condition known as a “cytokine
storm,” or colloquially as “uncontrolled inflammation.” Basically, in an
all-out, last-ditch effort to kill the invading influenza virus, the
body’s immune system overreacts creating so much inflammation that the
lungs and other organ systems fail. The immune system, to kill the
virus, inadvertently kills the body. Is this, too, the only option? Not
if we as Autistic people intervene – and we most certainly can intervene
against bullies/shunners/gaslighters/trolls – borrowing a page from US
Cyber Command<sup>177</sup>:

1.  Deny: avoid, get away and don’t be a target

2.  Degrade: seek authority to change behavior; have evidence (write
    down notes) when you speak with authorities and be as calm/clear as
    you can

3.  Disrupt: do not respond, go silent;
    bullies/shunners/gaslighters/trolls thrive on back-and-forth
    discussions, and you’ll deprive them of that

4.  Deceive: tell them you’ll be right back, leave and never return

5.  Destroy: this is rarely necessary nor helpful, better to “starve”
    them of “fuel” by following the above four steps

The problem is a predator is recognized by an Autistic person; moves
instinctively into Protective Mimicry. After a time though, we see a
shift into *Destructive* Mimicry in the deepest levels of Autistic
Burn(out). But simultaneously, and bafflingly to physicians treating
Autistic people, we also see strong parasitic immune response symptoms
as well. You’re getting the sympathetic nervous system response in the
Mimicry Cycle, because your system is primed to know how to deal with
predatory behavior. But you may simultaneously be having a parasitic
immune response, which depending upon the person could be a true
cytokine storm, parasite-induced immunodepression, or other conditions.
Again, we need more data and diagnostics to know for sure. But one thing
we know for sure: in many cases, you as an Autistic person are dealing
with this all the time (or very often), and the human body was not
designed to absorb this much punishment. The Autistic person is in a
Predator-Parasite Defense Cycle which, depending on the duration, will
eventually lead to very destructive consequences for the mind and body.
Unless we as Autistic people *intervene on our own behalf*.

Part of what Autistic minds are dealing with is gaslighting that a bully
(predator-parasite):

1.  Is just a jerk and part of learning to live in the world, and
    maturing as an adult is learning *by yourself* to deal with them
    (wrong – “you cannot negotiate with terrorists”)

2.  It’s a “personality conflict” (it almost never is)

3.  OK, they’re not a jerk, but it’s “just” a bully and not something
    more serious like an abusive predator — “*you’re overreacting”* (if
    anything, you’re under-reacting)

4.  You, and you alone, will need to do one of the “known fixes” for
    bullying behavior and it will stop (it won’t)

5.  Failing all of that, it’s really a “you problem” and not a “we
    problem” (right and wrong – it’s an “us” problem)

6.  It’s also not a health crisis (it most certainly is)

7.  \[off to the side\] “well, they (the Autistic person) will just have
    to learn to deal with bullies themselves, I’ve done all I can” (they
    haven’t)

This maps neatly into Grief Cycle from <u>On Death and Dying</u>. The
problem here is that, as we’ve seen above in the Grief Cycle, which
instead of Acceptance becomes Resignation. Acting out of Resignation
creates gaslighting as seen above, which in turn converts Autism the
disability into Autistic comorbidities (diseases).

There is an old saying in the wisdom books that “if you can name your
demons, you can have power over them.” So, this is an attempt at naming
those demons. Here’s how gaslighting about bullying/shunning against
Autistic people works in practice:

1.  You’re not being bullied/shunned (this is what we hear first)

2.  Your actions are attracting the bully/shunner (shaming the victim,
    aka “it’s your fault”)

3.  You shouldn’t let these things get to you (after you change your
    actions, and it still doesn’t change – it’s really your fault since
    it’s “all in your head”)

4.  You’re just like the rest of us, and we all must get tougher by our
    experiences with bullies (except for Autistic people this *doesn’t*
    strengthen us it makes us increasingly sick)

> That’s the message we hear over and over and over, once again the
> *only option presented*. And it’s this message that drives what’s
> next. And it doesn’t have to be this way.
