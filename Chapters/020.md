## Chapter 2: Social Model of Autism

I’m going to say something here, that to many of you (even a lot of
Autistic people), may seem like madness. Ready? Here goes: Autism isn’t,
strictly speaking, a disease. Right, some of you might say, it’s ASD:
Autistic Spectrum Disorder. Probably read about that or had a doctor or
therapist describe it that way. But the latest research is bearing this
out, calling Autism “ASD” is simply calling a disease a disorder. In
logic, that’s what we call a distinction without a difference. It’s
*not* a disease. It’s *not* a disorder. It’s perfectly, 100% natural.
It’s a *neurotype*<sup>7</sup>. As natural (and rare) as being
left-handed or having "red" hair. But you might say, how can that be?
And what do you mean by "neurotype?"

If you know this one, feel free to skip this paragraph. *Neurotypes*,
meaning types of brains that function markedly differently from one
another, are something that occurs naturally, and *neurodiversity* (or
many different brain types) has been with us a while. Maybe a very, very
long time<sup>8</sup>. But the neurotype we see in Autistic people is
rare. Some studies put it at 1% of the global population<sup>9</sup>.
More recent data in the US nearly tripled that<sup>10</sup>. What
accounts for the disparity? We know that better screening of conditions
increases the perceived prevalence of conditions<sup>11</sup>, in other
words, we’re getting better at looking for it so we’re finding more of
it. We’ve all heard of ADD or ADHD or Down Syndrome, which are other
neurotypes, similar but distinctly different from Autism. And if you’re
none of these, and not another neurodivergent type, and you fall into
the great wide majority? You’re *neurotypical*, or as a lot of Autistic
people say *NT*.

And quick sidebar, but no, not everyone's a little
Autistic<sup>12</sup>, in the same way that all women aren’t a little
pregnant. You are or you are not. And if you are Autistic, you may be
different from literally every other Autistic person you meet. But if
you are Autistic, you are. And if you’re not, you’re really not. All
neurotypes are exactly how nature intended. And as with many natural
designs, there is a beauty and strength to all forms of neurodiversity
as well.

Ok, you may be saying, if it’s not a disease or disorder (the
Medical/Pathological Model of Autism<sup>13</sup>), is it a disability?
I mean, you might be saying, "Autism is no picnic!" People with Autism
suffer deeply, often every day. As we’re learning, far too many die far
too young. Many are on strong medication or become hospitalized, even
institutionalized. Their families and other loved ones, famously, suffer
too. There are entire groups of Autistic charities devoted to the
suffering that families, often parents, of Autistic people feel. So, no
doubt this is disabling. Thus, a disability<sup>14</sup>.

But disease? Not exactly. Please let me explain. To get into this, we’re
going to talk briefly about the Social Model of Autistic Disability. In
a nutshell, it goes like this: in a room full of Autistic people, the
disability fades and dissolves. <span class="mark">We, in those safe
Autistic spaces, are not disabled</span>. We are empowered. Relaxed, at
times, and joyful. Or, at minimum, relieved of the terrible burden of
isolation and *masking, i.e.,* presenting oneself as neurotypical. So,
wherein lies the disability? In the *neurotypical* world<sup>7</sup>.

But you might say (and a fair number of you Autistic), we must *live* in
that world: “The Real World.” Quite right, we all must live in that
world. <span class="mark">But that’s the point: *that’s the proof* that
the Social Model of Autistic Disability living in the real world is
disabling, versus being in an enclave of Autistic
individuals</span><sup>7</sup>. We learned this from the Deaf Community.
Deafness is not a disease, because in a room full of deaf people,
problems are solved. Signing, Braille and other deliciously beautiful
and varied ways of expressing ourselves cause the prior antiquated
notion of disease in Deaf people to fall apart. <span class="mark">It’s
society, as it is today, that is *causing* the disability</span>.
Deafness is not a disease. We know that, right? Typical society
dis-ables the Deaf<sup>15</sup> (and note that many Deaf people will say
that they are not disabled, either). <span class="mark">For Autistic and
many other *neurodivergent* people, there is no disease but only a
disability</span>. With accommodations, the ravages of the disability
fade. The wheelchair ramp makes access possible for those in need of
them. And so on.

It’s the way society is currently set up that is *causing* the
disability, so it is society and thus the *Social* Model of Autistic
Disability<sup>16</sup>. Also known as the Neurodiversity Paradigm.
Contrast this with the Pathology Paradigm of Autism. Big words, but even
bigger consequences. A *paradigm* is a way you enter the world. A way
that shapes everything you see. If you’re Autistic, and all your life
you’ve been told you’re sick. Or if you’re a caretaker, and you’ve been
told your loved one has an incurable lifelong *disease*. But is it
*true*? Can we absolutely know that is true – or is this merely a
disability, brought on by the way society is currently structured, that
requires accommodations? This is not to say that there are not serious
issues with disabilities: if a person in a wheelchair is not
accommodated with a ramp, for example. How do they get to work? To
school? How do they get out of their house?

Hold onto that image. That’s where we are *right* *now* in the Autistic
community. A world without the accommodations we need to safely enter
and traverse the world. But we’re not sick. Unless…the lack of
accommodation combines in a world measured almost solely through
material success and "productivity" you literally can’t get to. It would
drive any healthy person sick. Kills quite a few, as we’re learning.
<span class="mark">For Autistic people, the lack of adequate
accommodations drives our nervous / psychological systems to the
breaking point</span>. Just like it used to for people in wheelchairs
before federally mandated ramps. And Deaf People 60 years ago. On second
thought, <span class="mark">it’s more appropriate to characterize
Autistic people as being in the time *before wheelchairs*, much less
before ramps</span>. Paradigm matters. We’re not sick. We’re dis-abled,
and not just once. In a loop. A highly emotionally charged, high stakes
cycle of compounding disabilities. Part of it comes through society.
Part of it comes increasingly through a half-trillion-dollar industry —
bearing down on us — treating us as if we have an illness that *doesn’t*
*exist*. <span class="mark">We need more "wheelchairs'' and "ramps," and
less pathologizing for the fact we don’t have them</span>. It’s just a
small shift in thinking with a massive positive impact on lifespan and
health. But we don’t have to wait for the help we need, for the support
we deserve. <span class="mark">The rest of this book will be very
specific on what we can do *right now* to start making our lives better,
so we can start feeling better, *right now*</span>*.* And caretakers and
Autistic loved ones, this goes for you, too. As we make the world safer,
more accessible for your Autistic loved one, your stress level will very
likely drop as well.
