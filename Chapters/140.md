## Chapter 14: Evolution Has a Built-in Defense – Mimicry

In evolutionary theory, natural selection and adaptation are constantly
around us, vis-a-vis “survival of the fittest.” We know a lot about the
evolutionary pressures on NT people. What do we know about the
evolutionary pressures specific to Autistic people? Are they identical,
or are the pressures different? If they are different, why? What can we
learn from the social pressure upon Autistic people, where the
experience is much more intense and the consequences so much higher?
Although it may seem extreme, I believe bullying in an Autistic victim
context can be more accurately described in an evolutionary context as
predation. We often think of predation in terms of predator-quarry
models where immediate death is the cause-and-effect dynamic. Immediate
death is untrue in all but the most extreme cases: we refer to bullies
of the Autistic sort as behaving as a type of predator; like a “sexual
predator,” with or without the sexual component. But bullies, or
predators, against Autistic people are themselves in a spectrum of
behavior. This doesn’t need to be the schoolyard bully – it could be a
teacher in a packed classroom that says out loud, “you’re
acting/speaking inappropriately!” It could be a relative embarrassing
you in a way you can’t easily emotionally handle at a party. We often
talk now about microaggression, as opposed to aggression, when it comes
to “socially acceptable” discrimination – bigotry driven nearly
underground. Instead of bullying as predation, what if we think about
*micropredation*; a socially acceptable way of bullying a disabled
person. In an evolutionary context this means that the predator is
behaving, in an analogous and not literal way, as *parasitic* in
behavior. In the world of parasites, there are two cases; in the first
case of parasitic infestation life continues with reduced quality of
life or even a benefit (symbiosis). In the second case, death is rarely
immediate, so it is delayed. It is this second case that drew my
attention: a subclass of parasites called *parasitoids*<sup>166</sup>*.*
Parasitoids are a type of parasite that cause the eventual death of the
host. I believe this accurately describes the dynamic of
bullying/micropredation against Autistic people. Bullies don’t get their
needs met (aren’t “fed,” emotionally) because they live a life in shame
that, because it is not confronted and dealt with, can never be
extinguished/healed. So, they find another way to get their needs met:
through bullying or inflicting their hurt feelings onto another. If it
were bullying a NT person this would rarely be recognized as predation
(although I believe it is), but most importantly it would rarely
(never?) be recognized as (analogously) parasite/parasitoid behavior.
And that’s part of the problem: recognizing the behavior in an
evolutionary context to the Autistic person is half of the battle in
mounting an adequate defense.

Mimicry, or protective mimicry, is an evolutionary response and a
defense against predation, so the quarry (mimic) doesn’t become the
victim. I hope I’m not offending you, as many disabled people already
feel “preyed upon” in one way or another by society (and don’t like
being made to feel the victim) – please note that these evolutionary
strategies exist to *prevent living things from becoming* a victim.
According to research on the topic, there are at least two forms of
protective mimicry in nature, both relevant to Autistic people. “Signal
mimicry” is defined by Solan and Aubier (2019) as evolutionary traits
that have evolved for “predator avoidance.” Put in an Autistic context,
signal mimicry evolved to avoid future bullying. Signal mimicry is at
the origin, I believe, of two Autistic behaviors: Batesian (meltdown)
and rewarding (assimilation). With meltdown, the mimic signals a
“fitness cost” (a penalty to the bully) but delivers no fitness cost to
the receiver (predator). In other words, it’s a signal that the receiver
will be harmed, where no harm is (usually) delivered. With assimilation,
the mimic signals a fitness benefit and delivers a fitness benefit to
attract or cooperate with the receiver. Assimilation signals to the
predator that we are willing to “go along to get along.” Assimilation
may represent the single biggest threat to Autistic health, due to the
overwhelming number of adaptations required. Akin to “method acting,”
the authentic self is subverted in favor of an entirely different
persona, with the following costs<sup>167</sup>:

- Emotional Distress: feelings of sadness, anger, or fear

- Identity Confusion: identity confusion and psychological strain

- Resurfacing of Trauma: unresolved issues or traumas can re-emerge

- Fatigue, Fear, Anxiety, Shame, Sleep Deprivation, Personality Changes

Famously, both Jim Carrey and Heath Ledger, as method actors suffered
prolonged mental health in the first case and drug abuse/overdose
leading to death in the second.

“Cue mimicry” is the other form of mimicry in nature and is defined as
evolutionary traits that have evolved for other selective pressures. One
example is the vertebrate eye pattern on the backs of many animals,
including moths. It makes the quarry look like something else, thus the
“cue.” Cue mimicry is at the origin, I believe, of three Autistic
behaviors: masquerade (compensation), camouflage (masking) and crypsis
(shutdown and early-stage burnout). With compensation, the mimic
resembles an object that is irrelevant or beneficial to the receiver. In
other words, it’s a signal that the receiver hasn’t found quarry, but
something either irrelevant or beneficial – but not its next meal. With
camouflage/masking, the mimic resembles its background to reduce its
detectability by the receiver. Masking signals to the predator that we
are “just one of the crowd” -- vital since bullying hinges on the target
being isolated. Finally, with crypsis, the mimic reduces its
conspicuousness by hiding or being silent to avoid attracting attention
from the receiver. Shutdown (i.e., “mini-burnout”) and early burnout is
an anti-signal (“playing dead”) to the receiver and since
receivers/bullies require input to keep going (most predators prefer
live prey), they tend to move on.

It is this predator avoidance/environmental blending-in that is the
model that starts and continually reinforces the set of Autistic
Protective Mimicry:

| Mimicry Type | Subtype    | Autistic Behavior |
|--------------|------------|-------------------|
| Signal       | Camouflage | Masking           |
| Signal       | Masquerade | Compensation      |
| Cue          | Batesian   | Meltdown          |
| Cue          | Rewarding  | Assimilation      |
| Cue          | Crypsis    | Shutdown          |
| Cue          | Crypsis    | Early Burnout     |

Note at the end, where Autistic Early Burnout is Crypsis? I actually
don’t think Crypsis fully fits – meaning that I’m not convinced that
Autistic Burnout, certainly not the deeper levels (Level 4, Level 5)
continue to serve as *Protective* Mimicry. There is some evidence that
the deeper levels of Autistic Burnout are in fact Necrotic (aka
deadly)<sup>168</sup>. Protective Mimicry crosses a threshold in deep
Autistic Burnout from Protective Mimicry to non-protective or
Destructive Mimicry. Which is part of the seriousness with which
Autistic Burnout should be taken. Language matters. Instead of “burnout”
it might be more appropriate to treat burnout as a *neurological burn*,
and frame it in terms of first, second, third, and fourth degree
*Autistic Burns*. A fourth-degree burn is life-threatening. When we say
“burnout” to most people, especially NT people, there’s an image of
weakness/blame/shame. Conversely, we don’t shame and blame third/fourth
degree burn victims – we put them in the burn unit of a hospital without
judgment and with a great deal of compassion. A third or fourth degree
“Autistic Burn” conveys the correct intent, I believe. And there’s more.

Evolution has enabled forms of life such as Autistic people, through
millions of years of evolutionary pressures, to avoid predators and
blend into the background environment. Note, though, that this is not
like watching an episode from “Animal Planet.” This is not the
occasional lion or pack of hyenas in the African Savannah attacking a
herd of gazelles -- those are true predators. When Autistic people are
bullied, it's not predation like it is for NT people. The awareness for
those around the Autistic person (loved ones, medical professionals), do
not rise to the level of the threat – they are unlikely to understand
the severity of the threat. This leads to gaslighting. Also, the model
is wrong, so the reaction of everyone to bullying is wrong. The proper
analogy I believe is much more like a parasitic infection/infestation
that sits resident within the host. Autistic people spend our daily
lives with these parasitic-like people. And since the parasite is not
another species, this is *cannibalistic parasitism*, and this is where
Autistic Protective Mimicry becomes so important, powerful *and deadly*.
First, we are all reacting to something that for all the world looks
like a predator: a bully. Our environment and our bodies are geared
towards reacting to a bullying situation as it would a predator:
protective mimicry kicks in, and it’s all about the sympathetic nervous
system. We ask for help, or we go it alone, but the result is the same:
millions of years of evolution kick in and we’re in predator-survival
mode: it’s all about Protective Mimicry. But what if it’s not a
predator? What if it’s a parasite? Some part of us knows this, and we
struggle for the words. We struggle to describe how awful it is, how
this isn’t what it appears to be, and we are labeled as “overreacting”
or “too intense.” And this is, for many Autistic people, where the
gaslighting shifts from painful to potentially life-threatening. And
note, it is not the parasites that are killing us directly, rather they
act as a catalyst. It is the evolutionary adaptation of Protective
Mimicry that eventually, if it goes on long enough, stops being
*Protective* Mimicry and becomes *Destructive* Mimicry. Let me explain.
