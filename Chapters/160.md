## Chapter 16: Destructive Mimicry and Beginning to End It

Protective mimicry, when it is effective, works against (analogously)
predators but not against (analogously) parasites. There are few truly
effective adaptive immune responses to parasitic infestations.
Tapeworms, bedbugs and other parasitic infections linger, vs. a wolf or
mountain lion from which we can often evade or recover from. Why? A
predator you can outrun, outfox, outlive, confuse, hide and play dead.
You can see them/hear them coming (many times), and instinctually you
know what they are. And for bacterial and viral infections, predators of
a different sort, we have our immune system to do the same. It’s been
said that people don’t have instincts, and that’s why it takes so long
for us to mature: everything we know must be taught. On the other side
of that argument is that what we are as humans is all nature: we are a
combination of genetics, epigenetics and instinctual drives. We do have
innate immune responses, even if they must be trained through exposure
or immunization. Likewise, we have protective mimicry responses, even if
they must be trained through exposure or social training. What if
protective mimicry is 80/20, where it’s 80% environment (“nurture”) and
20% nature? In NT people, it might be that blend. For some Autistic
people it might be 20/80, with 20% nurture and 80% nature. For others it
might be a closer balance of 50/50, or something in a spectrum. Again,
more data is needed to know for certain. But one thing is for certain:
many Autistic people struggle to learn classically taught lessons
(schoolroom, etc.) especially early on…but regardless we are learning
*from our environment* all the time. It’s just different, because
Autistic sensory input is so radically different from both NT people and
indeed from other Autistic people.

Bullies, be they schoolteachers or classmates or doctors/ABA
technicians, are some of our first and most memorable social rule
“teachers” as Autistic people, almost universally either at school or at
home or in the doctor’s office. Bullying and embarrassment are some of
our earliest and remain some of our most powerful memories/traumas,
across the Autistic community. To deal with these ongoing painful
experiences, we seek guidance often from those closest to us. And the
lessons we receive are both warped by NT experience and then further
distorted and amplified by our own instincts that come deep from within
evolutionary adaptation.

These rules are directionally correct for NT people but never somehow
satisfying to us as Autistic people. So, we use instinct to reach for
better tools, reach for a more intense application of them, and then use
grit and stamina to see it through. But we can’t see what we can’t see.
For decades, the medical establishment believed that Autistic children
had a condition known as infantile schizophrenia. This belief, which is
an analogy of behavior seen in schizophrenia in adults, formed the
foundation of the Medical or Pathological Model of Autism. Increasingly,
led by the Autistic Community and an increasing number of researchers,
now understand Autism to be a disability, and not a disease, through the
Social Model of Autism. It is society that dis-ables us, not us when
alone and certainly not when we are with other Autistic people. It may
take decades to turn around this perception. But this wrong paradigm, or
model, set back support and services for Autistic people decades. And
suffering and early death is the result just as we saw in the Down
Syndrome Community.

But that’s a problem largely for researchers, medical professionals,
advocacy groups and parents of Autistic children to work through. For
Autistic people, the Pathological/Medical vs. Social Model is not
today’s problem (although I am not arguing it is not a problem).
Convincing others of the need for the Social Model is a minor issue in
comparison to the internal struggle I am describing. But we can learn
from the lessons in the Pathological Model misalignment of thinking,
resources and approach. Autistic People need our own, internal paradigm
shift: how to deal with bullies and bullying without relying (solely) on
the Protective Mimicry Model of Predation. We need a new paradigm to
understand some of the issues we’re facing: a Destructive Mimicry Model
with Parasitic Causes. Inscribed at the Temple of Apollo at Delphi are
the famous words: “know thyself.” In an echo of these words are from Sun
Tzu’s <u>Art of War</u>: “know your enemy.” We are called in this time
to do both. We must know how our environment acts in opposition to our
biology as Autistic people and we must know how to best react and
protect ourselves.

Predators are opportunistic. They search for the quarry and if that
doesn’t work, they move on to other opportunities. Think of early human
hunters and gatherers. They would hunt a deer, but if the deer eluded
them, they would either continue pursuit or move on. They did this for
millennia until the discovery of farming and livestock. They made the
shift from hunting to gathering to farming, again opportunistically. Why
leave your home and expose yourself to the elements for food if you can
grow it right next to you and sleep in your own bed every night? Hunting
still exists today, albeit it’s more of a hobby than a necessity for
many. The point is that given a choice, the hunter (predator) became one
who lived off the land as a farmer. Now we can call this symbiosis or
parasitic, it all depends upon the care the farmer puts back into the
land they cultivate. But humans can be both predator and symbiote. Both
predator and parasite, it all depends on their posture towards what
they’re consuming. Were many White Americans predators in the
18<sup>th</sup> and 19<sup>th</sup> centuries, or parasites? Depends
upon who you ask. If you were Native American, then the answer through
genocide and the treatment of buffalo (and other lifeforms) would be
predator, with a secondary parasitoid trait. If you were African
American, then the answer through slavery would be parasitoid, with a
secondary predator trait (through terrorism). But the history is there,
and you don’t have to go far in the distant past to find it, it just
means you need to cock your head to the side a bit and put on a
biological/evolutionary hat to see it. I’ll ask for a little patience
from you, as this may seem a little odd or unusual.

| Title  | Role       | Quarry                           |
|--------|------------|----------------------------------|
| Hunter | Predator   | Animals, berries, vegetables     |
| Farmer | Symbiote   | Vegetables/Fruit/Livestock       |
| Farmer | Parasitoid | Monoculture Vegetable/Fruit      |
| Miner  | Parasitoid | Strip mining; gold, silver, etc. |

We use these terms “hunter,” “gatherer,” “farmer” and “miner” because
we’ve grown up with them. And we do it because it’s uncomfortable
thinking of ourselves as predators or symbiotes – or worse parasitoids.
But, from an evolutionary perspective, from a biological perspective,
“if the shoe fits, wear it.”

Our sympathetic nervous system is part of the immune system for the
mind-body connection. It helps regulate our system against outside
forces that would dysregulate or harm us. Immune systems, famously
during respiratory illnesses like the “Spanish” flu and COVID-19 would
go into a severe overreaction called a “cytokine storm” which could
deliver symptoms more deadly than the original infection. It happens
when the immune system, in a desperate attempt to battle the illness,
puts in a last-ditch effort to drive out the invader *at all costs*.
What if there were a “cytokine storm” equivalent inside the Autistic
Acute Stress Response to Bullying (fight-flight-freeze-fawn/feign). What
if you had over-exposure to predation, or worse a form of predation that
behaved more like a parasitic infection? What would the sympathetic
nervous system do to remedy the situation? An all-out sustained assault,
perhaps? The equivalent to a cytokine storm?  And what would that look
like exactly?  And if you got rid of the microbe, would the cytokine
storm abate? Or do you treat the cytokine storm first and microbe
second? In COVID-19 wards, it was not unusual to treat the cytokine
storm first and the COVID-19 infection second, to save the patient. Our
body’s cure, in some cases, is worse than the disease.

Remember that these systems are designed to keep your mind and body in
balance, known as equilibrium (homeostasis), with your environment. But
if you *can’t* stay in equilibrium, the mind-body processes have no
other option. It’s not like it knows what else to do, so if at first you
don’t succeed try, try again.  Turn up the volume. Turn up the response.
Try harder.  Go longer.  Until you burn out.  Because evolutionary
biology *presented no other option*. We, thankfully, have more options
than simply ordering off a one-item menu.
