## Evidence Erratum — 2026-05-31

This imported source chapter contains a known evidence-register error.

The passage beginning:

```text
The Journal of Applied Behavioral Analysis (JABA) said about Lovaas’s 1974 conversion therapy research paper, as they were retracting it:
```

must not be carried forward as written.

Current evidence mapping and C challenge indicate that JABA issued an expression of concern / editor's note regarding the Rekers and Lovaas 1974 paper, not a confirmed retraction.

The reader-facing claim should be corrected to:

```text
The journal later flagged the paper with an expression of concern / editor's note, but did not retract it.
```

Any quotation from the JABA expression of concern / editor's note must be verified against the primary source or a quotation-level secondary source before use.

This erratum governs future Options, evidence notes, and promotion records derived from this chapter.

Before Autistic people see results like what happened with people with
Down Syndrome, we need to first understand the origin of the current
so-called "gold standard" of US-based Autism “therapy” (indoctrination).
Building from the work of Ivan Pavlov, Bertrand Russell and John B.
Watson – B.F. Skinner rose to be one of the most influential American
Psychologists of the 20th Century. He developed the concept of *operant*
*conditioning*, rooted in an earlier concept of Behavioral Psychology.
Behavioralists, specifically ABA technicians, believe that the work of
Psychology should focus on observable behavioral and not mental events
(known to the rest of us as *mental health*). This point proves to be
very important, and we’ll get back to this in a moment. Skinner was also
a philosopher, wrote 20 books, one notably on how to create a utopian
society called <u>Walden II</u>, based upon the idea that if you could
control the behavior of a society, you could have a near perfect world.
He was not the only globally influential thinker in the 1940s who had an
ideology rooted in utopian goals based upon totalitarian control. Some
went nowhere. Others shaped the course of history through World War II,
and others later the Soviet Union less than a decade later. The first
global utopian movements during World War II cost 73,000,000 people
their lives. Under Josef Stalin’s utopia, between 20,000,000 and
60,000,000 people paid with their lives. Utopian movements are the
rocket fuel of genocide.

Ivar Lovaas chose a different path. A Psychologist by training, rooted
in the behavioralist tradition of Watson and Skinner, Lovaas was a bit
of a maverick. A migrant from Nazi-occupied Norway, he had no faith in
the "Great Men" of Psychology and 20th Century politics— and he had no
illusions as they did of creating “Walden II” in a top-down way like
Skinner<sup>126</sup>. He believed that if individuals learned to
control the behavior of other individuals, like a chain reaction each
controlling the next person’s behavior, the effect would be a society
free from suffering<sup>126</sup>. From interviews he gave, it was clear
he was deeply affected, as anyone would be, by the horrors of Nazi
rule<sup>126</sup>. After World War II, he came to America and trained
as a psychologist for children<sup>127</sup>. In 1962, he began treating
children suffering from repetitive self-harming behaviors
<sup>126</sup>. The State of California mental institutions sent quite
literally the most extreme cases of Autism to him at the University of
California, Los Angeles for years<sup>126</sup>. <span class="mark">That
they were the most extreme cases will become very important, later in
the story</span>. The events he recounted in a 1974 interview with
Psychology Today would, itself, traumatize anyone<sup>126</sup>. Lovaas,
traumatized or not, reacted to his terrifying patients as traumatized
people sometimes do<sup>129</sup>: with harsh, violent punishment
(so-called "aversives"). These aversives were from the teachings of B.F.
Skinner. But this was his own way, as Lovaas was different, a maverick.
He believed, as Skinner did, that all children would benefit from a
behavioralist approach. Unlike Skinner, whose cool, detached demeanor
meant control from a distance —automated if possible — for Lovaas it was
to be very, very personal. "Once you hit a child…you can’t walk away,"
he once said<sup>126</sup>. He sought to *bind* not only the disabled
patient (and their desperate parents) but the technician to dozens of
hours a week in a years’ long commitment. We now know that this binding

industrialized the other…with the blessing of the American Medical
Establishment, Medical Insurance Industry and last but certainly not
least through the force of law, nationwide.
