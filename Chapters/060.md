## Chapter 6: Positive Mental Attitude (PMA)

As I started researching for this book, two questions kept coming back
to me. One, what is driving that 53-year-old life expectancy to be so
low? And two, what do special forces teams do in survival training?
Turns out these two things are connected in powerful ways.

If you spend a lot of your time in unsafe environments, it turns out you
don’t live so long. What drives a shortened life span, whether you’re a
Navy SEAL or an Autistic person, have a few things in common. First, you
are often in environments seemingly or actually hostile to you. It may
or may not be as dramatic as depictions found in Hollywood special
forces movies, but the risks are just as real. As are the consequences.
Two, risk and their consequences are part of the deal.
<span class="mark">As a neurodivergent person in a neurotypical world,
especially an Autistic person, you may not be in enemy territory— but it
isn’t exactly friendly to you, either</span>. And while most of us try
not to think about it this way, in fact <span class="mark">we were
*gaslighted* (or trained) to believe that *we* are the unfriendly ones
but make no mistake about it — your body knows the truth. You’re often
in unwelcoming environments that can and do hurt you</span>. Third, it’s
possible to do unsafe things safely. Or at least safer. And that’s the
reason Special Forces train…a lot, and *as a team*.

Ok, so what’s the big deal? Lots of people train. Teachers train.
Firefighters train. Regular army, navy, air force and marines train.
They all train. But what makes Special Forces *special* is <u>how</u>
they train. And it starts with the mind. Not the body.

Probably because my Autistic father was in the US Green Berets (Special
Forces), it led me to study several of the world’s elite special forces
and how and more importantly *on what* they train:

- US Navy SEALs

- Special Boat Service (SBS): UK

- Special Air Service (SAS): UK

- Sayeret Matkal: Israel

- National Gendarmerie Intervention Group (GIGN): France

- US Army Delta Force (1st SFOD-D)

- Alpha Group: Russia

- Polish JW GROM

- The Special Service Group (SSG): Pakistan

- Unidad de Operaciones Especiales (UOE): Spain

You know what they said?

- Train as a *unit*, constantly.

- Be physically strong (not bodybuilder strong but fit) to be ready for
  your environments.

- Be ready to adapt, improvise and overcome.

- Know when to withdraw.

- Know your threats by doing a threat assessment.

- Have/find access to clean water: stay hydrated!

- Find/develop shelter from the elements (cold/heat/wind/snow-rain)

- Sleep/rest when you can.

- Have a spiritual discipline or strong sense of purpose and a practice
  to go along with it.

OK, that’s all important. But that’s not the first thing every one of
them said. And I mean *every single one*. You want to know the most
important thing to survive, that every Special Forces team I studied
said was most important:

> Rule \#0: Keep a Positive Mental Attitude (PMA).

No, this book is not devolving into typical feel-good nonsense. I’m not
talking about the Power of Positive Thinking<sup>TM</sup> or “keep your
chin up.” I’m talking about <span class="mark">the single most important
thing you need to survive in a hostile situation: the Neurotypical world
we inhabit</span>. More important than water, shelter or food. It’s how
you think – before, during and after you’re in a survival situation.
This is true for both Special Forces and Autistic people. Perhaps, this
is even more true for Autistic people and the people who care about
Autistic people. <span class="mark">Healthy, long lives depend upon a
positive mental attitude</span>.

Let me give you an example. Say you’re a Navy SEAL, trapped behind enemy
lines, cut off from your team, low on water, low on ammunition and
surrounded on all sides by the enemy. In fact, you’re actually lost.
What do you do first? Well, when interviewed, people who have survived
these situations will almost always say, "that’s where the training
kicks in." OK, but what training? Specifically. Break it down. What’s
priority one, two and three? They’re all the same: keeping a positive
mental attitude, also known as Positive Psychology.<sup>42</sup>
<span class="mark">If you allow yourself to start/continue thinking,
"I’m not going to make it," or "I can’t stand this for one more minute"
you have now *put yourself* in real danger</span>. Why? Because panic
will start to set in, and adrenaline and cortisol flood your system. You
get narrowed vision (figurative and literal), and with it, your choices
dwindle. Creative thinking goes out the window. What about finding
water? Shelter? Rest? Nope. All now are, because you believe it to be,
are now quite impossible. <span class="mark">What’s worse, you may reach
for a solution that actually makes things worse (in the case of our
Autistic loved ones, that’s precisely what often happens with sending
you to ABA)</span>. Your mindset makes injury much more likely and
survival less likely. So, you are now, because your attitude directs it,
unlikely to thrive. But it doesn’t have to be this way. It doesn’t have
to feel this way. And it’s critical to your wellbeing that it doesn’t.

Why am I telling you this? And what does this have to do with Autism?
Let me break down the similarities in situations we as Autistic people
face on a daily basis:

1.  Going to school

2.  Going to work

3.  Going to the supermarket or another crowded shop

4.  Interviewing for a job

5.  Attending or, God forbid, hosting a party

6.  Going to eat in a noisy restaurant

7.  Living with someone who abuses you, verbally or otherwise

8.  Having Flashbacks from previous abuse

9.  Having an Autistic Meltdown in public

10. Going through Autistic Shutdown or Burnout

The list goes on. Tell me, if you are being honest, that those aren’t
situations in which you feel in jeopardy. That some part (or all) of
your life is at risk. That at times you feel like you’re not going to
survive. You, in that moment, have much in common psychologically and
neurologically with a Navy SEAL. <span class="mark">And keeping a
positive mental attitude is the difference between feeling like you’re
in danger, and actually being in danger</span>.

*Controlling stress*

Life in a neurotypical world, unregulated, is a highly stressful world
for Autistic people.

Stimming is your first tool in de-stressing. In re-regulating.

But stimming is not enough. Think of the neurotypical world as sunshine.
It’s radiation. Get the right amount and you make a key nutrient;
vitamin D. Get too much, and you get a sunburn or worse! Limiting
exposure is critical. To their sounds, to their smells, to the way they
act including the way they treat Autistic people.

Remember, the dosage makes the poison. Limit your interactions with
neurotypicals where you can, but most importantly bump up the
interactions with other Actually Autistic people at the same time. By
doing both you limit the exposure to what is harmful, but you also
dilute the effects of it at the same time. Both are important.

*Tapping into your elders*

Having a group of older Autistic voices is important for two
life-sustaining reasons. Older Autists can help younger Autists navigate
the pitfalls and learn best practices that can make life much easier,
and safer. Secondarily, elder Autists benefit by giving back and sharing
their wisdom fueling a sense of purpose and belonging. NTs worldwide are
getting worse and worse at this. And it’s no wonder. Elders treat their
young with suspicion and disdain, and their children grow up returning
the "favor." This is a decades-long trend. We don’t have to be like
them. We don’t have to be like our families of origin, either. We can
make a new family. A better family, one based upon mutual respect and a
commitment to wellbeing and longevity. But it takes commitment and a bit
of courage.

*Medicine/Nutrition*

We talked about this briefly above – but let’s take one more crack at
it. What is medicine when you’re disabled not sick? What do we mean by
nutrition?

Hippocrates said, "let food be thy medicine and medicine be thy food."
This is taken to an extreme in modern society. If you have epilepsy, and
you’re under the care of a neurologist who has prescribed anti-seizure
meds, you take them, not spirulina. If bipolar or schizophrenia have
been diagnosed for you, these are conditions with grave consequences for
"going off your meds." Remember Autism is a disability but some of these
conditions are *diseases*, and they need to be treated by a doctor. But
it’s also eating a balanced diet that keeps you well, prepared in a way
that balances safefood with ones that don’t trigger texture issues. For
you, it might mean supplementing it with vitamin B-complex, vitamin D,
magnesium, and hydration salts. Do your research and don’t overdo the
dosages. As always, check with your doctor before thinking of a
do-it-yourself supplement regimen. My grandmother took 20 different
supplements a day. Bit excessive. Some of these supplements, especially
in high dosages recommended by some, can and do make you sick. “Check
yourself before you wreck yourself.”

What’s nutrition? Well, it’s certainly not what most nutritionists and
food scientists and agribusinesses have been pushing for 50 years.
They’re clearly making us physically sicker and weigh more – which in
turn drives inflammation and both physical and mental illnesses.
Nutrition, simply put, is what your great or great-great grandmother
would recognize as food, eaten together as a community. The
\[traditional\] Mediterranean Diet, for example, is favored by hundreds
of tribes of people who generally live longer and with less disease.
More complex carbs, less meat. I’m not saying you must be a vegan. But
strive for eating and drinking nothing out of a cardboard box, mylar
bag, aluminum can, plastic bottle or shrink-wrapped plate. If someone
else messed with it on the way from the farm to your table, it likely
isn’t nutritious…or as nutritious as it *could* be. It’s Hippocrates’
medicine—food prepared well, by a community local to you that cares
about your health. Because you’re them. They’re you. Together an
interconnected web of well-being. Conversely, factory farms and
processed food lead to factory-scale, manufactured illnesses.

Now, does this mean Ketogenic and other diets are not healthy, even
recommended for some Autistic people, such as people with epilepsy and
others? Of course not. But seek input from a balanced perspective. Seek
input from doctors you trust and *are trusted by the Autistic
community*. A lab coat and a stethoscope aren’t enough to protect you as
an Autistic person. I wish it were, but it’s simply not. There have been
way too many kooky and, in some cases, downright dangerous supplements,
diets and treatments with next to no scientific evidence pushed on
Autistic children and their parents seeking something, anything to
"cure" their child. And they usually make their way to hapless and in
some cases predatory medical practitioners: uninformed doctors,
misguided therapists, and a few unethical scientists who make Autistic
"cures" or "causes" all seem legitimate. We’re not sick.
<span class="mark">Some diets and supplements can, however, make us
sick</span>, deathly so in some cases. Nutrition is a community-driven
and sustained activity that drives health *across* the Autistic
community. And, as always, with exceptions: food allergies and texture
issues are no joking matter, so we must of course take them seriously
and balance them as well.

*Activity/Volunteering*

We talked about this above, but let’s give it another crack. What is
activity? What is volunteering?

Activity is you, in motion. It could be writing, painting, dancing,
stimming, walking, rolling, swimming or doing yoga. To your best
ability. For your wellbeing.

Volunteering is you, in motion, with others. It could be gardening,
walking, hiking, rolling, reading to others, spending time donating your
gifts and wisdom to others. Things you do, for others, that respect your
sensory needs. For the wellbeing of others. And, strangely, for your
own.

You don’t need to be rich to do this. You don’t need to staff a food
bank or soup kitchen to do this. You can be a mentor to a younger
Autistic person…or, funny enough, an older one! We need "Big Brothers,
Big Sisters" for Autistic People. Desperately. Don’t wait for it to
materialize. Look for opportunities online to be that voice. Or seek
that voice as you do. And mentoring is chemistry. If it doesn’t fit,
move on. But keep searching. You may think you’re too young. Likely not.
I’ve been mentored very recently by people in their *very* early
twenties. There is wisdom all around us. It’s being shared. That’s
volunteering. But it also might be a tabletop game, video game clan or
D&D session. Is a game volunteering? Yes, it is. Any activity that
builds an Autistic community, where joy, time and wisdom can all be
shared, is volunteering.

Is going on a walk volunteering? Can be. My grandpa had a garden and
went on daily walks with his neighbor Bellamy. Was it just good for him?
No, no it wasn’t. It was good for both. Got them out of the house, and
I’m not sure who got the better gift out of it — the husbands or the
spouses! But all kidding (not kidding) aside, that garden he had fed a
dozen people. My other grandfather also had a garden. Fed twice that
many. And neither man "volunteered" or "exercised" a day in their very
long lives. But the community felt their gifts of time just the same.
And, on a very deep level, so did they.

*Rest/Recreation*

We talked about this above, but let’s give it another crack. What is
rest? What is recreation?

Rest is you, reconnecting with that part of you that hungers to do
absolutely nothing.

Recreation is you, doing "nothing" (of value to our modern society) with
a group. It’s an activity designed to let you rest with others from the
seemingly endless series of demands placed upon you by a society that
seems divorced from its responsibility to care for you. It’s like kids
at school recess. But kids who are all nice to each other. 😊

There is something about our modern society, all over the globe, that is
deeply toxic. Nowhere do you see it more than in the latest fad of the
Cult of Productivity: Hustle Culture. By the time this book comes out,
it may be called something else. But it goes like this: no matter how
much you’re doing, it’s half of your potential. You can be, you should
be, you must be doing more. All the time. Hustle Culture is sick – *and
it will make you sick*. And no group of people suffers more than the
disabled. Autistic people bear the brunt of this sickness in
particularly insidious and lethal ways.

Rest is important. In Autistic meltdown, it is essential first aid. In
Autistic shutdown and burnout, it may be all that is possible — your
mind and body is lighting the way, stripping skills you need to function
right away from you. A dimmer switch. In Autistic shutdown, well, it’s
mandatory. Your body has taken all alternatives off the table. Took its
toys and left the sandbox. But what if we took the hint, but *much*
sooner? That more rest, more downtime, is required for us so that we
don’t get to the point of meltdown, burnout and shutdown? What if we
recognized that most people’s 100% is our 150% or 200%? Is that ok? Can
we consider it? I think we should. My wife often says our 50% might just
be the right amount of effort, and more than that is unhealthy. But it
will take a shift in thinking and support not just within the Autistic
community, but Neurotypical society as well. But we’re going to have to
lead the way. We’re going to have to fight for it. Like our lives depend
on it.

Rest is not just "lying flat" or going into a sensory room for relief.
That’s rest, and that’s good. But it’s more than that – we need to
*re-create*. Recreation is also playing D&D with Neurodivergent friends.
Or getting together once a week over a meal. Or going for a nature walk,
swim, yoga or roll. Reading, painting, listening to music, writing is
all good. You re-create when you’re doing the right kinds of recreation.
What are you re-creating? You. A better, healthier you. Every time.

One last thought, and I’ve said it above a few times. There is mounting
evidence that therapy dogs, specifically trained in either Epilepsy (if
you have it) or Autism, are enormously helpful as part of a recreation
routine. Not quite rest (dogs need a lot of love!) and not quite
volunteering, dogs are somewhere in between. There is an old (Autistic?)
saying: “the more I know people the more I love my dog.” I think there
is something more to this bit of folk wisdom. I think the more we love
dogs, the more we as Autistic people can connect with and love people.
This has certainly been shown to be true for families with Autistic
children with trained therapy dogs<sup>119,120</sup>.

### : What We Can Learn from Down Syndrome

> "I neither know nor think I know" — Plato, quoting Socrates

Acknowledging our ignorance is the beginning of wisdom, and said another
way, knowing that one does not know is the foundation of many a best
practice. We need to keep a posture of humility in what we know and
don’t know, especially when it comes to approaching the support and
services, we provide the complex array of Autistic people. This type of
multidisciplinary, cautious approach was used over the last several
decades with a condition seemingly unrelated to Autism: Down Syndrome.
My Uncle Tommy was institutionalized with Down Syndrome in the 1950s,
where he later died at the age of 14. Conventional medical wisdom and
available data was people at that time with Down Syndrome died before
the age of 10. Uncle Tommy beat the odds, he must’ve been one tough kid.
But decades later, this is what longevity looks like in people with Down
Syndrome <sup>121,122</sup>:

<img src="media/image1.png" style="width:5in;height:3.11458in" />

That’s a *fifty-year* increase for what was universally, scientifically,
and medically *known* to be a lifespan capped at ten years old! That
shift in thinking didn’t happen by accident, and it clearly didn’t
happen overnight. But it *did* happen in *one generation*. Something, or
several somethings, had to radically change in the way we medically
treat — but most importantly — *view* people with Down Syndrome.

There was a shift from *knowing* what the right treatment was for Down
Syndrome (the 1950s "gold standard" — isolate and institutionalize), to
*we don’t know* and most importantly to *we know we don’t know*. In
1950, the gold standard of care for Down Syndrome wasn’t a gold
standard. It was the *only option presented*.

Some of the factors that increased the lifespan in the Down Syndrome
population:<sup>121,124,125</sup>

- improved medical care, especially cardiac care

- social inclusion

- educational opportunities

- advocacy for people with Down Syndrome

And at the heart of these factors (the "secret sauce") was a shift from:

- Thinking “we know what we know and what to do,” to

- Thinking “we ***don’t*** know what we know and what to do,” to

- Advocating/working as a community and ending isolation, to

- Growing and sharing a body of knowledge through social networks and
  better research

However, some of the challenges that remain are congenital heart
defects, Alzheimer's disease, and racial disparities. We still have work
to do to bring proper support and services to people with Down Syndrome.
But we now *know* we don’t know all the answers on support and services
for people with Down Syndrome, and armed with the right perspective
informed by wisdom, the work remains to bring full health and vitality
to this neglected and still disabled community. Neglected because of a
behavioral/external symptom-based "gold standard" model of medical
treatment. Just like my Uncle Tommy received. My grandmother never
recovered from the shame and trauma of how her and her son were treated
by society. Unforgivable was the posture of the medical establishment
and what ultimately happened to him. It was said that part of her died,
when he died. We see echoes of this in the Autistic people of today.
Autistic people nearly in the situation 1950’s kids with Down Syndrome
were, medically. How did we get here? How can we replicate the
lifesaving and life-affirming results of Down Syndrome support and
services to another Neurotype, Autism?

To benefit from the lessons learned from the Down Syndrome Community, we
must first understand the Autistic equivalent of the Down Syndrome
1950’s “gold standard of treatment.” In this part of the 21<sup>st</sup>
Century, in the United States and parts of Western Europe, the “gold
standard of treatment” is Applied Behavioral Analysis (ABA). As I
mentioned earlier, it is neither a treatment nor a therapy as Autism
isn’t an illness. Also, as mentioned ABA trains Autistic people,
intentionally or not, to feel bad about themselves for who they are;
thus, it is abuse.

Are All ABA Clinics and Providers Abusing Autistic Patients?

Importantly, I am not saying \*your\* ABA clinic or technician is
causing damage as many have. But do yourself and your loved one/child
this favor. Ask to speak with a half-dozen 25- to 35-year-old Autistic
patients who went through ABA with that specific technician for years.
Ask how they feel. Ask if their life is improved or better because of
ABA. Ask if they would recommend ABA to other Autistic kids. If the ABA
clinic or technician tells you they can’t do that because of
confidentiality laws, that’s bending the truth *more than just a bit*. A
willing, happy patient can and will sign a legal medical information
release for the ABA technician *if they were treated well*. And,
importantly, *if asked*. But if they won’t if they aren’t following up
or keeping track of their patients (to see how they’re doing as a caring
therapist might). If the real issue is that patients are unwilling to
sign a release to talk about their experience, one must ask why. A lot
of us now use Yelp before we try out a new restaurant. This is
infinitely more important than avoiding undercooked steak.

So, if Not ABA, what are Parents To Do?

There are proven, alternative methods to ABA for Autistic children that
are humane, ethical, and effective, such as DIR<sup>®</sup>/Floortime,
RDI, play or music therapy. For older children/teens: dialectical
behavior therapy (DBT) and cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT). Kids and
adults of all ages can benefit from mindfulness, self-regulation
techniques and Autistic therapy/support dogs. But interview any
therapist, first. Get a referral from people you trust, or see these
sites:

- 
- 

[<https://www.ndtherapists.com><https://neurodivergentpractioners.org>](http://www.nd)

And, importantly, just because they’re not using ABA or demonstrate
overt Ableism doesn’t mean they’re safe to be alone with your loved one.
This is why interviews and referrals are so important.

You will find one thing in common with all the above approaches: they
approach the Autistic person as a person, with a rich inner lived
experience just like everyone else. Just like people with Down Syndrome.
They seek to help the Autistic person alter their environment to them as
much as them to their environment, in balance (homeostasis), as it
should be. Finally, they drive towards mental and physical health of the
whole person, often using a multidisciplinary approach of multiple
experts all working together with the parents and most importantly the
Autistic person. There are other multidisciplinary approaches like
SCERTS, but make sure an ABA therapist is nowhere near your child as
part of it. It takes a village to raise a child, but as we all know by
now, not everyone *in* a village can be *trusted* with a child.

Finally, these alternatives to ABA may not be as "cheap" — and may not
be covered by insurance. But fast food is cheaper too in the short term.
The long-term costs in suffering to eating at fast food chains are
enormous and, in many cases, result in a sicker, shorter life. For more,
see Morgan Spurlock’s excellent documentary "Super Size Me." We need
such a documentary for the ABA franchise. Now. And we need parental
voices demanding Autistic therapy *choice through the Medical Insurance
system*.

Sometimes no food is better for you than cheaper, bad food. Choose
wisely. Let’s talk about how, and what, next.

### : Into the Light

> "It is better to light a single candle than curse the darkness." —
> Anonymous, various sources

Let’s shift gears away from the root of many evils in the Autistic
population. This book, after all, is called “You Have Another Option.”
It’s not enough to survive ABA, or survive life as an Autistic Person,
as justice demands more. So, as you might recall there was a
decades-long health push in another neurodivergent group, people with
Down Syndrome. An alliance of neurodivergent and
neurodivergent-affirming loved ones and medical professionals and
researchers came together, gathering best practices and data along the
way. To change lives for the better.

The "key stakeholders" in the Down Syndrome community all had to first
adopt the Socratic Method as the guiding principle overriding the
Scientific Method: we don’t know and we *know* we don’t know. That means
we must challenge and be willing to attack the notion of an
“evidence-based gold standard” of any Autistic support or service. There
certainly cannot be an “evidence-based gold standard” of Autistic
*treatment*, because Autism is not a disease. If treated as an illness,
as it is today, we will continue to make Autistic people sick and die
younger than they should. Even one is too many. We need better data to
know the full extent and depth of the damage ABA has caused. We need
holistic longitudinal studies. And documentaries. And media advocates.

Recall that the 1950s "gold standard" treatment for Down Syndrome —
isolate and institutionalize — failed twice. It failed first because it
formed up a treatment without longitudinal studies on outcomes, which
should be the gold standard of *evidence* of any medical treatment.
Second it buried the evidence, by not attempting and then making
longitudinal data-gathering hidden behind patient confidentiality. We
saw this with Big Tobacco. We saw this with Big Oil. They knew but they
buried the evidence and then discredited the critics.

<span class="mark">Spectrums of behavior are part of what makes us
human, no question. But do we have a single behavioral profile that
defines humanity – one single behavioral profile that we should enforce
as a standard? That sounds racist, colonialist, and creepy. Changing a
person’s behavior and claiming cure would be like stopping the behavior
of a wince of pain for someone going through cancer treatment, and then
claiming they were cured of cancer. That wouldn’t make sense, would it?
But even the basic theory is wrong. Autism is a neurotype, not a
disease. Autists are people. Autistic people are not a “shell of a
person”<sup>126,140</sup> that ABA technicians must make human. Also,
childhood is not an appropriately long timeframe, longitudinal studies
are needed that stretch into adulthood. We know the human brain keeps
developing until 25 years of age<sup>155</sup>, that might be a better
target of study length.</span>

<span class="mark">With 1950s Down Syndrome gold standard (isolate and
institutionalize) and 2020s Autism “gold standard” ABA (isolate in a
room with an ABA adult), the effect is the same: no possibility of
falsifiability. The 1950s Down Syndrome “gold standard” treatment
couldn’t be falsified because of Institutionalization. The Autism “gold
standard” treatment can’t be falsified because the only measure allowed
is *proximate behavior — "they act more like ‘normal’ (white,
heterosexual) kids now, don’t they?"* Remember, it’s not science if it’s
not falsifiable. ABA kicked sand in the eyes of the Scientific
Community, and by extension parents (voters) and Legislators. *Until
recently. *</span>

<span class="mark">Unsurprisingly to most of us, people are not like
rats. A little less intuitive, but equally sensible, but people are not
limited to the sum of their behaviors. Every school aged child knows
this. People are bodies, minds, hopes, thoughts, emotions and behaviors.
We are *all* a whole person. Lovaas and the behavioralists “*knew”*
something, that Autistic people (a) weren’t people and (b) all you had
to do was alter behavior to make these so-called non-people a person,
and *it just wasn’t so*.</span>

<span class="mark">So, the beginning of lighting a candle in this
darkness is wisdom, not knowledge. To deliver humane Autistic care and
support services, it’s vital that first we admit that *we don’t know*
and most importantly that we *know we don’t know*. In 1950, the “gold
standard” of care for Down Syndrome wasn’t a gold standard. It was the
*only option presented*. And so, it is with Autism. In 2023, the “gold
standard” of care for Autism isn’t a gold standard. Because of:</span>

- <span class="mark">understandably desperate parents</span>

- <span class="mark">disinformation/bad science</span>

- <span class="mark">legislation from bad science and desperate
  parents</span>

- <span class="mark">single-option insurance reimbursement</span>

- <span class="mark">misinformed medical practitioners</span>

- <span class="mark">majority of citizens living paycheck to
  paycheck</span>

<span class="mark">ABA is not the gold standard, in many cases it’s the
*only option presented*. The key word is <u>presented</u>. So, let’s
recap on what helped dig out the folks with Down Syndrome, and their
families, from a dark and dismal hole. These brave people, in fact, have
lit all neurodivergent people a candle in the darkness. They have and
are leading the way. To recap, some of the factors that increased the
lifespan in the Down Syndrome population:</span>

- <span class="mark">improved medical care</span>

- <span class="mark">social inclusion</span>

- <span class="mark">educational opportunities</span>

The core principle of science, above all other ideas, is falsifiability:
can I prove that a theory is wrong? This sounds odd if you’re not
trained in it, but science moves forward because sunlight is the best
disinfectant. Let people, other scientists not of your discipline or
training, look at your hypothesis, methods, data and see if they can
prove you wrong. Make it longitudinal: let’s see what happens when these
kids grow into fully formed adults using comprehensive mental and
physical health as measures: the whole person. If a thing can’t be
replicated in such a way so it can be disproven, then it can’t be
science. These are trappings of science-like methods and conversion
techniques that are not falsifiable by longitudinal studies. Repeated
stories of abuse. Silenced victims through intimidation and being
discredited.
